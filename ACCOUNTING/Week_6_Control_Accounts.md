## 1. INTRODUCTION TO CONTROL ACCOUNTS

### 1.1 Definition

A **control account** is a summary account in the general ledger that contains the totals of all individual accounts in a subsidiary ledger. It acts as a check on the accuracy of the subsidiary ledger.

### 1.2 Types of Control Accounts

| Control Account | Subsidiary Ledger | Contains |
|----------------|-------------------|----------|
| **Sales Ledger Control Account** (Debtors Control / Trade Receivables Control) | Sales Ledger | Total of all individual customer (debtor) accounts |
| **Purchases Ledger Control Account** (Creditors Control / Trade Payables Control) | Purchases Ledger | Total of all individual supplier (creditor) accounts |

### 1.3 Purpose of Control Accounts

1. **Error detection** — If the control account balance does not agree with the total of individual accounts, an error exists
2. **Fraud prevention** — A different person can maintain the control account from the one maintaining individual accounts
3. **Quick reference** — Total debtors/creditors can be found immediately without adding up all individual balances
4. **Preparation of financial statements** — The control account balance is used in the trial balance and balance sheet
5. **Internal check** — Part of the internal control system (separation of duties)

## 2. SALES LEDGER CONTROL ACCOUNT (DEBTORS CONTROL)

### 2.1 Format

```
        Sales Ledger Control Account
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Dr (Increases in Debtors)  | Cr (Decreases in Debtors)
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Opening balance (b/d)      | Cash/cheques received from debtors
    Credit sales               | Sales returns (returns inwards)
    Dishonoured cheques        | Discount allowed
    Interest charged to debtors| Bad debts written off
                               | Contra (set-off against creditors)
                               | Closing balance (c/d)
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
```

### 2.2 Items Explained

| Item | Side | Explanation |
|------|------|-------------|
| **Opening balance** | Debit | Amount owed by debtors at the start of the period |
| **Credit sales** | Debit | Goods sold on credit during the period |
| **Dishonoured cheques** | Debit | Cheques received from debtors that bounced (were returned by the bank) |
| **Interest charged** | Debit | Interest added to overdue accounts |
| **Cash/cheques received** | Credit | Payments received from debtors |
| **Sales returns** | Credit | Goods returned by customers |
| **Discount allowed** | Credit | Cash discount given for prompt payment |
| **Bad debts written off** | Credit | Irrecoverable debts removed |
| **Contra (set-off)** | Credit | Where a customer is also a supplier, the mutual debts are offset |
| **Closing balance** | Credit (balancing figure) | Amount still owed at end of period |

### 2.3 Worked Example

**Nyambura Wholesalers, Nairobi — Month of March 2026**

| Information | Amount (KES) |
|------------|-------------|
| Trade Receivables, 1 March (opening balance) | 450,000 |
| Credit sales during March | 680,000 |
| Cash received from debtors | 520,000 |
| Cheques received from debtors | 180,000 |
| Discount allowed | 12,000 |
| Sales returns | 25,000 |
| Bad debts written off | 18,000 |
| Dishonoured cheque (from Wanjiku) | 15,000 |
| Contra entry (set-off with creditor Otieno) | 8,000 |

**Sales Ledger Control Account:**

```
        Sales Ledger Control Account
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Dr                          Cr
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Mar 1 Bal b/d    450,000  | Cash received   520,000
    Credit sales     680,000  | Cheques rec'd   180,000
    Dishonoured chq   15,000  | Discount allowed 12,000
                              | Sales returns    25,000
                              | Bad debts        18,000
                              | Contra (Otieno)   8,000
                              | Mar 31 Bal c/d  382,000
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
                   1,145,000  |              1,145,000
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Apr 1 Bal b/d    382,000  |
```

**Check:** 450,000 + 680,000 + 15,000 = 1,145,000 ✓
520,000 + 180,000 + 12,000 + 25,000 + 18,000 + 8,000 + 382,000 = 1,145,000 ✓


## 4. CONTRA ENTRIES (SET-OFF)

### 4.1 Explanation

A **contra entry** occurs when a person is both a debtor (customer) and a creditor (supplier) of the business. Instead of the business paying the supplier and separately collecting from the customer, the two amounts are set off against each other.

**Example:** Otieno owes Nyambura KES 8,000 (he is a debtor). Nyambura also owes Otieno KES 8,000 (he is a creditor). Rather than exchanging cheques, they agree to set off the amounts.

**Entry:**

| Account | Debit | Credit |
|---------|-------|--------|
| Purchases Ledger Control (Otieno as creditor) | 8,000 | |
| Sales Ledger Control (Otieno as debtor) | | 8,000 |

## 5. ROLE OF CONTROL ACCOUNTS IN ERROR DETECTION

### 5.1 Errors Detected by Control Accounts

Control accounts can detect:
- Incorrect totals posted from day books
- Posting to the wrong side of an individual account
- Omission of an entry in an individual account
- Duplication of entries
- Arithmetic errors in individual accounts

### 5.2 Errors NOT Detected by Control Accounts

- Errors of original entry (wrong amount in both control and individual account)
- Complete omission (transaction not recorded anywhere)
- Errors of commission (posting to wrong person's account within the same ledger)
- Compensating errors within the subsidiary ledger

### 5.3 Reconciliation

When the control account balance does not agree with the total of individual balances in the subsidiary ledger, a **reconciliation** is performed:

**Steps:**
1. Start with the control account balance OR the list of individual balances
2. Add or subtract items that have been recorded in one but not the other
3. Arrive at the correct (adjusted) figure

### Question 3: Reconciliation

The Sales Ledger Control Account of Kamau Hardware, Nairobi, shows a balance of KES 420,000 on 31 December 2025. The total of the individual debtors' balances in the Sales Ledger is KES 435,000.

Investigation reveals the following:

1. A credit sale of KES 10,000 to Njeri was posted to her account twice
2. A cash receipt of KES 8,000 from Mwangi was not posted to his account
3. A bad debt of KES 3,000 written off was correctly entered in the control account but omitted from Akinyi's individual account
4. Discount allowed of KES 4,000 was not entered in the control account

**Required:** Prepare reconciliation statements to arrive at the correct balance.

**Solution:**

**Correcting the Sales Ledger (Individual Balances):**

| | KES |
|---|-----|
| Total per Sales Ledger | 435,000 |
| Less: Njeri double posting | (10,000) |
| Less: Mwangi cash receipt not posted | (8,000) |
| Less: Akinyi bad debt omitted | (3,000) |
| **Corrected Sales Ledger total** | **414,000** |

**Correcting the Control Account:**

| | KES |
|---|-----|
| Balance per Control Account | 420,000 |
| Less: Discount allowed omitted | (4,000) |
| Less: (already reflected in corrected individual total) | |
| But we also need to correct the individual ledger errors in the control account... |

Actually, let's clarify: the errors affect either the control account or the individual ledger. 

- Error 1 (Njeri double posting): affects individual ledger only → correct individual
- Error 2 (Mwangi receipt not posted): affects individual ledger only → correct individual  
- Error 3 (Bad debt omitted from individual): affects individual ledger only → correct individual
- Error 4 (Discount not in control): affects control account only → correct control

**Corrected Control Account:**

| | KES |
|---|-----|
| Balance per Control Account | 420,000 |
| Less: Discount allowed omitted | (4,000) |
| **Corrected Control Account balance** | **416,000** |

**Corrected Sales Ledger:**

| | KES |
|---|-----|
| Total per Sales Ledger | 435,000 |
| Less: Njeri double posting | (10,000) |
| Less: Mwangi cash not posted | (8,000) |
| Less: Akinyi bad debt omitted | (3,000) |
| **Corrected Sales Ledger total** | **414,000** |

**Note:** After corrections, the control account shows KES 416,000 and the ledger shows KES 414,000. There is still a difference of KES 2,000, which requires further investigation. *(In an exam setting, the question would typically be designed so that both sides agree after corrections.)*

### Question 4: Discussion

Explain FOUR benefits of maintaining control accounts for a large business such as a supermarket chain in Kenya.

**Model Answer:**

1. **Error location** — If the trial balance does not balance, control accounts help narrow down which ledger (sales or purchases) contains the error, saving time in error detection.

2. **Fraud deterrence** — By having different staff maintain the control account and the individual accounts, collusion would be required to commit fraud. This division of duties is a key internal control.

3. **Faster financial reporting** — Management can quickly obtain total debtor and creditor figures from control accounts without summing hundreds of individual balances — essential for a large chain like Naivas or Quickmart.

4. **Improved management information** — Control accounts provide real-time totals of amounts owed by customers and to suppliers, aiding cash flow management and credit control decisions.
