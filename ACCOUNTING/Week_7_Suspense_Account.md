## 1. TYPES OF ERRORS IN ACCOUNTING

### 1.1 Errors That DO Affect the Trial Balance

These errors cause the trial balance totals to disagree. They can be detected by the trial balance.

| Error Type | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Error of partial omission** | Only one part of the double entry is recorded | A cash sale of KES 10,000 debited to Cash but not credited to Sales |
| **Casting (addition) error** | Incorrect totaling of an account or day book | The Sales Day Book total is added as KES 540,000 instead of KES 450,000 |
| **Transposition error** | Digits are reversed | KES 67,000 recorded as KES 76,000 (difference divisible by 9) |
| **Extraction error** | Wrong balance extracted from ledger to trial balance | Account has a balance of KES 48,000 but KES 84,000 is entered in the trial balance |
| **Single entry error** | A transaction is posted to only one account | Rent of KES 15,000 debited to Rent Account but not credited to Cash |
| **Posting to wrong side** | Correct account but wrong side | KES 5,000 received from Kamau debited to Kamau's account instead of credited |

**Tip for trainees:** If the difference in the trial balance is divisible by 9, it is likely a **transposition error**. If divisible by 2, it may be a **posting to the wrong side** error.

### 1.2 Errors That DO NOT Affect the Trial Balance

These errors leave the trial balance in balance despite being incorrect. They are more dangerous because they are harder to detect.

| Error Type | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Error of omission** | A transaction is completely left out of the books | A credit sale of KES 30,000 to Wanjiku is not recorded at all |
| **Error of commission** | Correct amount, correct type of account, but wrong person/account within the same class | Payment of KES 8,000 from Akinyi posted to Atieno's account (both are debtors) |
| **Error of principle** | Correct amount posted to the wrong class of account | Motor vehicle repairs KES 50,000 debited to Motor Vehicle (asset) instead of Repairs (expense) |
| **Error of original entry** | Wrong amount recorded in the original entry (both debit and credit are wrong by the same amount) | An invoice for KES 34,000 recorded as KES 43,000 in both accounts |
| **Compensating errors** | Two or more errors cancel each other out | Debtors overstated by KES 5,000 and creditors also overstated by KES 5,000 |
| **Complete reversal of entries** | Correct accounts and amounts, but debits and credits are swapped | Cash received from Otieno: Cash credited and Otieno debited (both reversed) |

## 2. THE ROLE OF THE TRIAL BALANCE

### 2.1 What the Trial Balance Can Do

- Verify arithmetic accuracy of the double entry system
- Provide a starting point for preparing financial statements
- Help detect errors that cause an imbalance

### 2.2 What the Trial Balance Cannot Do

- Detect errors of omission, commission, principle, original entry, compensating errors, or complete reversal
- Guarantee that all transactions have been correctly classified
- Prove that financial statements are accurate

## 3. THE SUSPENSE ACCOUNT

### 3.1 Definition

A **suspense account** is a temporary account opened when the trial balance does not balance. The difference is placed in the suspense account to make the trial balance balance temporarily, while the errors are investigated.

### 3.2 When to Use a Suspense Account

1. The trial balance does not balance, and the error cannot be found immediately
2. A transaction is unclear and needs investigation before posting to the correct account
3. As a temporary holding account until errors are identified and corrected

### 3.3 Opening a Suspense Account

If the **debit side** of the trial balance exceeds the credit side, the suspense account is **credited** with the difference (and vice versa).

**Example:** Trial balance shows debits KES 1,500,000 and credits KES 1,485,000. The difference is KES 15,000.

Entry: Debit Suspense Account KES 15,000 — No! The credit side is short by KES 15,000, so:

Actually: Debits exceed credits by KES 15,000. To balance, we need KES 15,000 more on the credit side. So:

**Suspense Account is CREDITED with KES 15,000.**

### 3.4 Correcting Errors Using the Suspense Account

When errors are found, journal entries are prepared to correct them. If the error affected the trial balance, the suspense account will be involved in the correction.

### 3.5 Comprehensive Worked Example

**Wafula Enterprises, Bungoma**

The trial balance on 31 December 2025 showed a difference: the credit side exceeded the debit side by KES 27,000. A suspense account was opened with a **debit balance of KES 27,000**.

On investigation, the following errors were discovered:

**Error 1:** A credit sale of KES 18,000 to Naliaka was recorded in the Sales Day Book but not posted to Naliaka's account.
- **Effect:** Naliaka (debtor) is understated by KES 18,000. Debit side is short by KES 18,000.
- **Correction:** Dr Naliaka KES 18,000 / Cr Suspense KES 18,000

**Error 2:** The Purchases Day Book was overcast (over-added) by KES 6,000.
- **Effect:** Purchases is overstated by KES 6,000. Debit side is overstated by KES 6,000.
- **Correction:** Dr Suspense KES 6,000 / Cr Purchases KES 6,000

**Error 3:** A payment of KES 9,000 for electricity was debited to the Electricity Account as KES 6,000.
- **Effect:** Electricity is understated by KES 3,000. Debit side is short by KES 3,000.
- **Correction:** Dr Electricity KES 3,000 / Cr Suspense KES 3,000

**Error 4:** Rent received of KES 12,000 was credited to the Rent Received Account twice.
- **Effect:** Rent Received (credit) is overstated by KES 12,000. Credit side is overstated by KES 12,000.
- **Correction:** Dr Rent Received KES 12,000 / Cr Suspense KES 12,000

---

**Detailed Solution:**

**Wafula Enterprises, Bungoma**

The trial balance on 31 December 2025 did not balance. The credit side exceeded the debit side by KES 27,000. A suspense account was opened with a **debit balance of KES 27,000**.

Investigation revealed the following errors:

1. A credit sale of KES 18,000 to Naliaka was recorded in the Sales Day Book but not posted to Naliaka's account in the Sales Ledger.
2. The Purchases Day Book was overcast (over-added) by KES 6,000.
3. A payment of KES 9,000 for electricity was debited to the Electricity Account as KES 6,000.
4. Rent received of KES 12,000 was credited to the Rent Received Account twice.

**Journal Entries to Correct Errors:**

| Error | Journal Entry | Debit (KES) | Credit (KES) |
|-------|-------------|------------|-------------|
| 1 | Dr Naliaka (Trade Receivables) | 18,000 | |
| | Cr Suspense Account | | 18,000 |
| | *Credit sale not posted to debtor* | | |
| 2 | Dr Suspense Account | 6,000 | |
| | Cr Purchases | | 6,000 |
| | *Purchases Day Book overcast* | | |
| 3 | Dr Electricity | 3,000 | |
| | Cr Suspense Account | | 3,000 |
| | *Electricity understated* | | |
| 4 | Dr Rent Received | 12,000 | |
| | Cr Suspense Account | | 12,000 |
| | *Rent received credited twice — removing duplicate* | | |

**Suspense Account:**

```
                Suspense Account
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Dr                          Cr
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Balance (TB diff)  27,000 | Error 1 Naliaka 18,000
    Error 2 Purchases   6,000 | Error 3 Electricity 3,000
                              | Error 4 Rent Rec'd 12,000
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
                       33,000 |                 33,000
```

The suspense account is now cleared (zero balance). ✓

## 4. STATEMENT OF CORRECTED NET PROFIT

### 4.1 Purpose

After errors are discovered and corrected, some corrections will affect the originally reported net profit. A **statement of corrected net profit** adjusts the original profit for these effects.

### 4.2 Which Errors Affect Net Profit?

Only errors involving **income** or **expense** accounts affect net profit. Errors involving only assets, liabilities, or capital do not.

### 4.3 Worked Example

Continuing from Wafula Enterprises:

Original reported net profit: KES 350,000

| Error | Effect on Net Profit |
|-------|---------------------|
| 1. Naliaka not posted | No effect — only affects Trade Receivables (asset) and Suspense |
| 2. Purchases overcast by 6,000 | Purchases were overstated → expenses overstated → profit understated by KES 6,000. **Add back KES 6,000** |
| 3. Electricity understated by 3,000 | Expenses understated → profit overstated by KES 3,000. **Deduct KES 3,000** |
| 4. Rent received credited twice | Income overstated by KES 12,000 → profit overstated by KES 12,000. **Deduct KES 12,000** |

**Statement of Corrected Net Profit:**

```
                                                    KES
Original Net Profit                              350,000

Add: Purchases overcast (error 2)                  6,000
                                                 356,000

Less: Electricity understated (error 3)           (3,000)
Less: Rent received duplicated (error 4)         (12,000)
                                                 --------
Corrected Net Profit                             341,000
                                                 ========
```

### Question 2: Suspense Account

Achieng Traders, Kisumu, extracted a trial balance on 31 December 2025. The debit side totaled KES 2,340,000 and the credit side totaled KES 2,355,000. The difference was placed in a suspense account.

The following errors were subsequently discovered:

1. Cash sales of KES 8,000 were credited to Sales Account but not debited to Cash Account
2. A purchase return of KES 5,000 was credited to Purchases Returns but not debited to the supplier's (creditor's) account
3. Discount allowed of KES 2,000 was not posted to the Discount Allowed Account (but was correctly entered in the debtor's account)

**Required:**
(a) Open the Suspense Account showing the original balance
(b) Prepare journal entries to correct the errors
(c) Show the corrected Suspense Account

**Solution:**

Difference: Credit exceeds Debit by KES 15,000. Suspense has a **debit balance of KES 15,000**.

**Analysis:**
- Error 1: Missing debit of KES 8,000 (Cash not debited)
- Error 2: Missing debit of KES 5,000 (Supplier not debited)
- Error 3: Missing debit of KES 2,000 (Discount Allowed not debited)
- Total: 8,000 + 5,000 + 2,000 = KES 15,000 ✓

**(b) Journal Entries:**

| Error | Entry | Debit (KES) | Credit (KES) |
|-------|-------|------------|-------------|
| 1 | Dr Cash | 8,000 | |
| | Cr Suspense | | 8,000 |
| 2 | Dr Supplier (Creditor) | 5,000 | |
| | Cr Suspense | | 5,000 |
| 3 | Dr Discount Allowed | 2,000 | |
| | Cr Suspense | | 2,000 |

**(c) Suspense Account:**

```
                Suspense Account
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Dr                          Cr
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Balance (TB diff)  15,000 | Error 1 Cash     8,000
                              | Error 2 Supplier 5,000
                              | Error 3 Disc.    2,000
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
                       15,000 |                  15,000
```

Suspense account cleared to zero. ✓

### Question 4: Discussion

Explain why a suspense account is only a temporary measure and should be cleared as soon as possible.

**Model Answer:**

A suspense account is temporary because:

1. **Financial statements will be inaccurate** — As long as errors remain uncorrected, the income statement and balance sheet contain wrong figures, leading to poor decision-making.

2. **It is not a real account** — The suspense account does not represent an actual asset, liability, income, or expense. Its presence in the trial balance indicates that the books are incomplete.

3. **Tax implications** — KRA requires accurate financial records. Filing tax returns based on uncorrected books could lead to under/over-payment of taxes and potential penalties.

4. **Audit concerns** — Auditors will qualify their report if a suspense account remains in the financial statements, reducing the credibility of the business's accounts.

5. **Compliance** — Under IAS 1 (Presentation of Financial Statements), financial statements must give a true and fair view. A suspense balance undermines this requirement.
