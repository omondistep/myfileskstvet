## 1. SOURCE DOCUMENTS

### 1.1 Definition

**Source documents** are the original records that provide evidence that a financial transaction has occurred. They are the starting point of the accounting process.

### 1.2 Common Source Documents

| Source Document | Purpose | Example |
|----------------|---------|---------|
| **Invoice** | Sent by seller to buyer detailing goods/services sold and amount due | Bidco sends an invoice to Naivas Supermarket for cooking oil supplied |
| **Receipt** | Acknowledges payment received | A Nakumatt receipt for cash purchase of office supplies |
| **Credit Note** | Issued by seller to reduce the amount owed by buyer (for returns or overcharges) | A Mombasa supplier issues a credit note for damaged goods returned |
| **Debit Note** | Issued by buyer to notify seller of a debit raised (for returns or undercharges) | A Nairobi retailer sends a debit note for defective goods being returned |
| **Cheque** | Written order to a bank to pay a specified sum | Equity Bank cheque for rent payment |
| **Delivery Note** | Confirms goods have been delivered | A courier service delivers goods to a Kisumu shop with a delivery note |
| **Purchase Order** | Buyer's formal request to a supplier for goods/services | A school in Eldoret sends a purchase order for textbooks |
| **Goods Received Note (GRN)** | Confirms goods received match what was ordered | A hospital stores department confirms receipt of medical supplies |
| **M-Pesa Statement** | Record of mobile money transactions | M-Pesa till transaction records for a small business in Machakos |
| **Bank Statement** | Record of transactions from the bank's perspective | KCB Bank statement showing deposits and withdrawals |
| **Petty Cash Voucher** | Authorizes small cash payments | KES 500 petty cash voucher for buying tea and sugar for the office |
| **Payslip** | Shows employee salary details and deductions | A payslip showing gross pay, PAYE, NHIF, NSSF, and net pay |

### 1.3 Flow of Source Documents

```
Transaction occurs
       |
       v
Source document created (invoice, receipt, etc.)
       |
       v
Recorded in Books of Original Entry (journals/day books)
       |
       v
Posted to Ledger Accounts
       |
       v
Trial Balance extracted
       |
       v
Financial Statements prepared
```

## 2. BOOKS OF ORIGINAL ENTRY

### 2.1 Definition

**Books of original entry** (also called journals or day books) are the books in which transactions are first recorded from source documents before being posted to the ledger.

### 2.2 Types of Books of Original Entry

| Book | Transactions Recorded | Source Document |
|------|----------------------|-----------------|
| **Sales Journal (Sales Day Book)** | Credit sales only | Sales invoices |
| **Purchases Journal (Purchases Day Book)** | Credit purchases only | Purchase invoices |
| **Sales Returns Journal** | Goods returned by credit customers | Credit notes issued |
| **Purchases Returns Journal** | Goods returned to credit suppliers | Debit notes / Credit notes received |
| **Cash Book** | All cash and bank transactions (receipts and payments) | Receipts, cheques, bank statements, M-Pesa records |
| **Petty Cash Book** | Small cash expenses | Petty cash vouchers |
| **General Journal (The Journal Proper)** | Transactions not recorded in any other book (e.g., opening entries, corrections, depreciation, bad debts written off) | Journal vouchers, memos |

### 2.3 Illustration: Sales Day Book

Wambui Traders, Nairobi — Sales Day Book for January 2026

| Date | Customer | Invoice No. | Amount (KES) |
|------|----------|-------------|-------------|
| Jan 3 | Ochieng Stores | 001 | 45,000 |
| Jan 10 | Akinyi Retailers | 002 | 30,000 |
| Jan 18 | Mwangi Wholesalers | 003 | 60,000 |
| Jan 25 | Njoroge Enterprises | 004 | 25,000 |
| | | **Total** | **160,000** |

**Posting:** The total (KES 160,000) is debited to Trade Receivables (Debtors) and credited to Sales Account. Individual amounts are posted to each customer's account in the Sales Ledger.

### 2.4 Illustration: Purchases Day Book

Wambui Traders — Purchases Day Book for January 2026

| Date | Supplier | Invoice No. | Amount (KES) |
|------|----------|-------------|-------------|

**Posting:** Total debited to Purchases Account, credited to Trade Payables (Creditors). Individual amounts posted to supplier accounts in the Purchases Ledger.

## 3. LEDGER ACCOUNTS

### 3.1 Definition

A **ledger** is the principal book of accounts where transactions are classified and summarized into individual accounts. Each account collects all transactions of the same type.

### 3.2 Types of Ledgers

| Ledger | Contents |
|--------|----------|
| **General Ledger (Nominal Ledger)** | All income, expense, asset, and liability accounts |
| **Sales Ledger (Debtors Ledger)** | Individual accounts for each credit customer |
| **Purchases Ledger (Creditors Ledger)** | Individual accounts for each credit supplier |

### 3.3 The Double Entry System

Every transaction is recorded in **two** accounts — one account is debited and another is credited.

**The Golden Rules:**

| Account Type | Debit | Credit |
|-------------|-------|--------|
| **Assets** | Increase | Decrease |
| **Liabilities** | Decrease | Increase |
| **Capital (Equity)** | Decrease | Increase |
| **Income/Revenue** | Decrease | Increase |
| **Expenses** | Increase | Decrease |

**Memory Aid:** **DEAD CLIC**
- **D**ebits: **E**xpenses, **A**ssets, **D**rawings increase
- **C**redits: **L**iabilities, **I**ncome, **C**apital increase

### 3.4 Format of a Ledger Account (T-Account)

```
                    Account Name
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Debit (Dr)           |    Credit (Cr)
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Date | Details | KES  | Date | Details | KES
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
```

## 4. THE TRIAL BALANCE

### 4.1 Definition

A **trial balance** is a list of all ledger account balances at a particular date, arranged in debit and credit columns. It is used to check the arithmetic accuracy of the double entry records.

### 4.2 Purpose of a Trial Balance

1. To check that total debits equal total credits (arithmetic accuracy)
2. To provide a summary of balances for preparing financial statements
3. To help detect certain types of errors

### 4.3 Limitations of the Trial Balance

A trial balance that balances does NOT guarantee that the books are error-free. The following errors will NOT be detected:

| Error Type | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Error of omission** | A transaction completely left out | A cash sale of KES 5,000 not recorded at all |
| **Error of commission** | Correct amount posted to wrong account of the same class | Payment from Wanjiku posted to Wanjiru's account |
| **Error of principle** | Transaction posted to wrong class of account | Motor vehicle repair (expense) recorded as motor vehicle (asset) |
| **Compensating errors** | Two or more errors that cancel each other out | Debit side overstated by KES 1,000 and credit side also overstated by KES 1,000 |
| **Error of original entry** | Wrong amount recorded in both debit and credit | Invoice for KES 56,000 recorded as KES 65,000 in both accounts |
| **Complete reversal of entries** | Correct accounts used but debits and credits are swapped | Cash received from Odhiambo: Cash credited, Odhiambo debited (reversed) |

## 5. COMPUTERIZED ACCOUNTING SYSTEMS

### 5.1 Definition

A **computerized accounting system** uses software to record, store, and process financial transactions electronically.

### 5.2 Common Accounting Software Used in Kenya

| Software | Typical Users |
|----------|--------------|
| **QuickBooks** | SMEs, retail shops, service businesses |
| **Sage (Pastel)** | Medium to large businesses |
| **SAP** | Large corporations (e.g., Safaricom, EABL) |
| **Tally** | Small businesses, popular in retail |
| **Xero** | Cloud-based, growing in popularity among Kenyan startups |
| **M-Pesa Business (Lipa Na M-Pesa)** | Micro and small enterprises — integrates with basic records |
| **KRA iTax System** | All taxpayers for tax filing |

### 5.3 Advantages of Computerized Accounting

1. **Speed** — Transactions are processed much faster than manual systems
2. **Accuracy** — Reduces arithmetic errors; the system auto-balances entries
3. **Storage** — Large volumes of data stored electronically (saves physical space)
4. **Reporting** — Financial reports generated instantly (trial balance, P&L, balance sheet)
5. **Backup and security** — Data can be backed up and protected with passwords
6. **Integration** — Can integrate with KRA's iTax system for seamless tax filing
7. **Audit trail** — Every entry is timestamped and traceable

### 5.4 Disadvantages of Computerized Accounting

1. **Cost** — Software licenses and hardware can be expensive for small businesses
2. **Training** — Staff need training to use the system effectively
3. **Power dependency** — Requires electricity (a challenge in some Kenyan rural areas)
4. **System failure** — Risk of data loss due to crashes, viruses, or hardware failure
5. **Fraud risk** — Unauthorized access can lead to manipulation of records
6. **Over-reliance** — Users may not understand underlying accounting principles

| Error | Affects TB? | Explanation |

### Question 4: Discussion

Explain FOUR advantages and TWO disadvantages of using a computerized accounting system for a medium-sized business in Nairobi.

**Model Answer:**

**Advantages:**
1. **Speed of processing** — Transactions are recorded and reports generated instantly, saving time compared to manual bookkeeping
2. **Accuracy** — The software automatically balances double entries, reducing arithmetic errors
3. **Integration with KRA iTax** — Many systems allow direct filing of tax returns, simplifying compliance
4. **Instant reporting** — Management can generate real-time trial balances, profit & loss statements, and balance sheets at any time

**Disadvantages:**
1. **Initial cost** — Purchasing software (e.g., QuickBooks or Sage) and hardware, plus staff training, can be costly for a medium-sized business
2. **Vulnerability to power outages** — Nairobi experiences occasional power interruptions; without a backup generator or UPS, work can be lost
