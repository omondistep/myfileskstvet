## 1. BAD DEBTS

### 1.1 Definition

A **bad debt** is an amount owed by a credit customer (debtor/trade receivable) that is considered irrecoverable — the debtor is unable or unwilling to pay.

**Common causes in Kenya:**
- A debtor's business has collapsed (e.g., a shop in Gikomba market destroyed by fire)
- The debtor has been declared bankrupt
- The debtor has absconded and cannot be traced
- The debt is too old and legally unenforceable (statute-barred under the Limitation of Actions Act — typically 6 years in Kenya)
- The cost of pursuing recovery exceeds the debt itself

### 1.2 Accounting Treatment

When a debt is confirmed as irrecoverable, it is **written off**:

| Account | Debit/Credit | Effect |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| Bad Debts Account (Expense) | Debit | Increases expenses, reducing profit |
| Trade Receivables (Debtor's Account) | Credit | Removes the debtor from the books |

### 1.3 Worked Example: Writing Off Bad Debts

**Wambui Traders, Nairobi:**

Wambui is owed KES 35,000 by Otieno Stores. Otieno's business has permanently closed and the debt is irrecoverable.

**Journal Entry:**

| Date | Account | Debit (KES) | Credit (KES) |
|------|---------|------------|-------------|
| | Bad Debts | 35,000 | |
| | Otieno Stores (Trade Receivables) | | 35,000 |
| | *Being bad debt written off* | | |

**Ledger Accounts:**

```
                  Bad Debts Account
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Dr                          Cr
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Otieno Stores     35,000  | P&L (year-end) 35,000

               Otieno Stores Account
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Dr                          Cr
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Balance b/d       35,000  | Bad Debts      35,000
```

**Effect on Financial Statements:**
- Income Statement: Bad debts KES 35,000 appears as an expense, reducing net profit
- Balance Sheet: Trade receivables reduced by KES 35,000

## 2. BAD DEBTS RECOVERED

### 2.1 Definition

A **bad debt recovered** occurs when a debtor who was previously written off as bad unexpectedly makes a payment (full or partial).

### 2.2 Accounting Treatment (Two Steps)

**Step 1:** Reinstate the debtor's account

| Account | Debit/Credit |
|---------|-------------|
| Trade Receivables (Debtor) | Debit |
| Bad Debts Recovered (Income) | Credit |

**Step 2:** Record the cash received

| Account | Debit/Credit |
|---------|-------------|
| Cash/Bank | Debit |
| Trade Receivables (Debtor) | Credit |

### 2.3 Worked Example

Continuing from above: Six months later, Otieno pays KES 20,000 as partial settlement.

**Journal Entries:**

Step 1 — Reinstate:

| Account | Debit (KES) | Credit (KES) |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Otieno Stores | 20,000 | |
| Bad Debts Recovered | | 20,000 |

Step 2 — Receive cash:

| Account | Debit (KES) | Credit (KES) |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Cash/Bank | 20,000 | |
| Otieno Stores | | 20,000 |

**Ledger Accounts:**

```
               Otieno Stores Account
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Bad Debts Recovered 20,000 | Cash/Bank      20,000

           Bad Debts Recovered Account
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Dr                          Cr
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    P&L (year-end)    20,000  | Otieno Stores  20,000
```

**Effect:** Bad debts recovered is treated as **income** in the Statement of Profit or Loss.

## 3. PROVISION FOR DOUBTFUL DEBTS

### 3.1 Definition

A **provision for doubtful debts** (also called allowance for doubtful debts) is an estimate of the amount of trade receivables that may not be collected. It applies the **prudence concept** — anticipating possible losses.

Unlike bad debts (which are specific and confirmed), provision for doubtful debts is an estimate based on experience.

### 3.2 Methods of Estimating

1. **Percentage of total debtors** — e.g., 5% of total trade receivables
2. **Ageing of debtors** — Different percentages applied to different age categories

### 3.3 Accounting Treatment

#### Creating a New Provision

| Account | Debit/Credit |
|---------|-------------|
| Provision for Doubtful Debts (Expense — P&L) | Debit |
| Provision for Doubtful Debts (Balance Sheet — contra asset) | Credit |

#### Increasing an Existing Provision

The **increase** is charged as an expense in the P&L.

#### Decreasing an Existing Provision

The **decrease** is treated as income (or reduction of expense) in the P&L.

### 3.4 Comprehensive Worked Example

**Chebet Enterprises, Kericho**

| Year | Trade Receivables (after bad debts written off) | Provision Rate |
|------|-----------------------------------------------|---------------|
| Year 1 (2024) | KES 400,000 | 5% |
| Year 2 (2025) | KES 500,000 | 5% |
| Year 3 (2026) | KES 350,000 | 5% |

**Year 1 (2024):**
- Required provision: 5% × 400,000 = KES 20,000
- Existing provision: KES 0
- P&L charge (new provision): **KES 20,000** (expense)

**Year 2 (2025):**
- Required provision: 5% × 500,000 = KES 25,000
- Existing provision: KES 20,000
- P&L charge (increase): **KES 5,000** (expense)

**Year 3 (2026):**
- Required provision: 5% × 350,000 = KES 17,500
- Existing provision: KES 25,000
- P&L credit (decrease): **KES 7,500** (income/reduction in expense)

**Provision for Doubtful Debts Account:**

```
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Dr                          Cr
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    YEAR 1:
    Bal c/d           20,000  | P&L            20,000
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    YEAR 2:            
                              | Bal b/d        20,000
    Bal c/d           25,000  | P&L             5,000
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
                      25,000  |                25,000
    YEAR 3:
    P&L                7,500  | Bal b/d        25,000
    Bal c/d           17,500  |
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
                      25,000  |                25,000
```

**Balance Sheet Presentation (Year 3):**

```
Trade Receivables                      350,000
Less: Provision for Doubtful Debts     (17,500)
                                       -------
                                       332,500
```

### 3.5 Ageing Method Example

**Njoroge Hardware, Nairobi — Debtors Ageing Analysis, 31 December 2025:**

| Age Category | Amount (KES) | Estimated % Doubtful | Provision (KES) |
|-------------|-------------|---------------------|----------------|
| 0–30 days | 200,000 | 2% | 4,000 |
| 31–60 days | 100,000 | 5% | 5,000 |
| 61–90 days | 50,000 | 10% | 5,000 |
| Over 90 days | 30,000 | 25% | 7,500 |
| **Total** | **380,000** | | **21,500** |

Required provision = KES 21,500

## 4. PROVISION FOR DISCOUNT ALLOWED

### 4.1 Definition

A **provision for discount allowed** is an estimate of cash discounts that will be given to debtors who pay promptly. It is calculated on the **net debtors** (trade receivables minus provision for doubtful debts), because doubtful debtors are unlikely to pay and therefore unlikely to earn a discount.

### 4.2 Accounting Treatment

Same principle as provision for doubtful debts:
- Creating/increasing the provision = expense in P&L
- Decreasing the provision = income in P&L

### 4.3 Worked Example

**Akinyi Wholesalers, Kisumu — 31 December 2025:**

- Trade Receivables: KES 500,000
- Provision for Doubtful Debts: 5% of receivables
- Provision for Discount Allowed: 2% of net receivables (after doubtful debts provision)

**Calculation:**

| Step | Calculation | Amount (KES) |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| Trade Receivables | | 500,000 |
| Less: Provision for Doubtful Debts (5%) | 5% × 500,000 | (25,000) |
| Net Receivables | | 475,000 |
| Provision for Discount Allowed (2%) | 2% × 475,000 | 9,500 |

**Balance Sheet Presentation:**

```
Trade Receivables                           500,000
Less: Provision for Doubtful Debts          (25,000)
                                            475,000
Less: Provision for Discount Allowed         (9,500)
                                            465,500
```

- Juma: KES 40,000
- Hassan: KES 25,000

- Juma's debt of KES 40,000 is written off as bad (business closed)
- Hassan pays KES 25,000 in full

**Required:** Prepare the Bad Debts Account, Bad Debts Recovered Account, and Juma's Account.

### Question 2: Provision for Doubtful Debts

Kiprop Stores, Eldoret, provides the following information:

| Year End | Trade Receivables (KES) | Bad Debts Written Off (KES) |
|----------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| 31 Dec 2024 | 600,000 | 30,000 |
| 31 Dec 2025 | 480,000 | 20,000 |
| 31 Dec 2026 | 720,000 | 15,000 |

The provision for doubtful debts is maintained at 5% of trade receivables (after bad debts are written off). The receivable figures shown are BEFORE writing off bad debts.

**Required:** For each year, calculate (a) the provision required and (b) the amount charged/credited to the P&L.

**Solution:**

| Year | Receivables after Bad Debts | Provision Required (5%) | Existing Provision | P&L Effect |

| 2026 | 720,000 - 15,000 = 705,000 | 35,250 | 23,000 | **12,250 (expense — increase)** |

**Income Statement Effect:**

| Year | Bad Debts Written Off | Provision Change | Total P&L Charge |
|------|---------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| 2024 | 30,000 | +28,500 | 58,500 (expense) |
| 2025 | 20,000 | -5,500 | 14,500 (net expense) |
| 2026 | 15,000 | +12,250 | 27,250 (expense) |

### Question 3: Provision for Discount Allowed

Using Kiprop Stores' Year 2026 data:
- Trade Receivables (after bad debts): KES 705,000
- Provision for Doubtful Debts: KES 35,250
- Provision for Discount Allowed: 3% of net receivables

**Calculate** the provision for discount allowed and show the balance sheet presentation.

**Solution:**

Net Receivables = 705,000 - 35,250 = KES 669,750
Provision for Discount Allowed = 3% × 669,750 = **KES 20,093** (rounded to KES 20,093)

**Balance Sheet Presentation:**

```
Trade Receivables                           705,000
Less: Provision for Doubtful Debts          (35,250)
                                            669,750
Less: Provision for Discount Allowed        (20,093)
                                            649,657
```

### Question 4: Discussion

Explain the difference between bad debts and provision for doubtful debts. Why is the provision for doubtful debts important?

**Model Answer:**

| Feature | Bad Debts | Provision for Doubtful Debts |
|---------|-----------|---------------------------|
| **Nature** | Specific, confirmed irrecoverable debts | General estimate of debts that MAY be irrecoverable |
| **Certainty** | Certain — the debtor will not pay | Uncertain — based on past experience and judgement |
| **Treatment** | Written off directly against the debtor's account | Created as a contra-asset reducing trade receivables |
| **Concept** | Matching concept — matching the expense to the period of the sale | Prudence concept — anticipating potential losses |

The provision is important because:
1. It ensures that trade receivables on the balance sheet reflect their **net realizable value** (not an overstated figure)
2. It applies the **prudence concept** by anticipating possible losses
3. It matches the estimated cost of bad debts against the revenue of the period in which sales were made
4. It gives a **more accurate** picture of the business's financial position to users such as banks (e.g., KCB assessing Kiprop's creditworthiness)
