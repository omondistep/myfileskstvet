## 1. INTRODUCTION TO BANK RECONCILIATION

### 1.1 Definition

A **bank reconciliation** is the process of comparing the bank balance as shown in the business's cash book with the bank balance as shown on the bank statement, identifying and explaining any differences.

### 1.2 Why Reconcile?

At any given date, the balance in the business's **Cash Book (bank column)** will usually differ from the balance on the **Bank Statement**. This does not necessarily mean there is an error — the difference is usually caused by timing and information gaps.

### 1.3 Purpose of Bank Reconciliation

1. **Detect errors** — In either the cash book or the bank statement
2. **Update the cash book** — With transactions the business was not aware of (e.g., bank charges, interest, standing orders)
3. **Prevent fraud** — Identifies unauthorized transactions
4. **Ensure accuracy** — Confirms the bank balance shown in the financial statements is correct
5. **Comply with good accounting practice** — Required by auditors and expected by KRA

## 2. THE CASH BOOK vs THE BANK STATEMENT

### 2.1 Understanding the Difference in Perspective

| Item | Cash Book (Business Records) | Bank Statement (Bank Records) |
|------|---------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Deposit/Receipt** | Debit (increases asset) | Credit (the bank owes MORE to the business — liability from bank's view) |
| **Payment/Withdrawal** | Credit (decreases asset) | Debit (the bank owes LESS to the business) |
| **Positive balance** | Debit balance = money in bank | Credit balance = money in bank |
| **Negative balance** | Credit balance = overdrawn | Debit balance = overdrawn |

**Key point:** The bank statement is a **mirror image** of the cash book from the bank's perspective. A deposit appears as a debit in the cash book but as a credit on the bank statement.

### 2.2 Format of the Cash Book (Bank Column)

```
                    Cash Book (Bank Column)
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Dr (Receipts)               | Cr (Payments)
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Date | Details      | KES   | Date | Details      | KES
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
```

## 3. CAUSES OF DIFFERENCES

### 3.1 Items in the Cash Book but NOT on the Bank Statement

These are **timing differences** — the transaction has been recorded by the business but the bank has not yet processed it.

| Item | Explanation | Effect |
|------|-------------|--------|
| **Unpresented cheques** | Cheques written by the business and recorded in the cash book, but not yet presented to/cleared by the bank | Cash book balance < Bank statement balance |
| **Uncredited deposits (lodgements)** | Cash or cheques deposited by the business but not yet credited by the bank (e.g., deposited late on the last day) | Cash book balance > Bank statement balance |

### 3.2 Items on the Bank Statement but NOT in the Cash Book

These need to be entered in the cash book to update it.

| Item | Explanation | Typical Example in Kenya |
|------|-------------|------------------------|
| **Bank charges** | Fees charged by the bank for account maintenance, transactions, etc. | KCB/Equity Bank monthly ledger fees, ATM charges |
| **Bank interest charged** | Interest on an overdraft | Interest on overdraft facility |
| **Bank interest earned** | Interest credited on savings/deposit account | Interest on fixed deposit at Co-operative Bank |
| **Standing orders** | Automatic regular payments set up by the business | Monthly insurance premium, loan repayment |
| **Direct debits** | Payments authorized by the business, initiated by the payee | KPLC (Kenya Power) electricity bill, Zuku internet |
| **Direct credits (RTGS/EFT)** | Payments received directly into the bank account | Customer payment via bank transfer, M-Pesa to bank |
| **Dishonoured cheques** | Cheques deposited by the business that were returned unpaid ("bounced") | A cheque from a debtor returned "refer to drawer" |
| **Dividends received** | Dividend payments credited directly to the account | Safaricom dividend credited to account |
| **Errors by the bank** | Bank has debited or credited the wrong account | Bank deducted another customer's cheque from your account |

## 4. STEPS IN PREPARING A BANK RECONCILIATION

### Step 1: Compare the Cash Book and Bank Statement

Tick off items that appear in both. Unticked items are the differences.

### Step 2: Update the Cash Book

Enter items from the bank statement that are not in the cash book (bank charges, interest, standing orders, direct debits, direct credits, dishonoured cheques, etc.).

Calculate the **adjusted (updated) cash book balance**.

### Step 3: Prepare the Bank Reconciliation Statement

Start with the **bank statement balance** and adjust for timing differences (unpresented cheques and uncredited deposits) to arrive at the **adjusted cash book balance**.

## 5. COMPREHENSIVE WORKED EXAMPLE

**Njeri Hardware, Nairobi — 31 January 2026**

**Cash Book (Bank Column) before adjustments:**

```
                    Cash Book (Bank Column)
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Dr (Receipts)               | Cr (Payments)
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Jan 1  Bal b/d     185,000 | Jan 5  Chq 201    45,000
    Jan 8  Ochieng      30,000 | Jan 10 Chq 202    28,000
    Jan 15 Cash sales   50,000 | Jan 18 Chq 203    60,000
    Jan 22 Akinyi       40,000 | Jan 25 Chq 204    35,000
    Jan 28 Mwangi       25,000 | Jan 30 Chq 205    22,000
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
                       330,000 |                   190,000
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Balance c/d = 330,000 - 190,000 = KES 140,000 (Dr)
```

**Bank Statement as at 31 January 2026:**

| Date | Details | Debit (KES) | Credit (KES) | Balance (KES) |
|------|---------|------------|-------------|--------------|
| Jan 1 | Balance b/f | | | 185,000 Cr |
| Jan 5 | Chq 201 | 45,000 | | 140,000 Cr |
| Jan 8 | Deposit | | 30,000 | 170,000 Cr |
| Jan 10 | Chq 202 | 28,000 | | 142,000 Cr |
| Jan 12 | Standing order — Insurance | 8,000 | | 134,000 Cr |
| Jan 15 | Deposit | | 50,000 | 184,000 Cr |
| Jan 20 | Direct credit — Kamau | | 15,000 | 199,000 Cr |
| Jan 22 | Deposit | | 40,000 | 239,000 Cr |
| Jan 25 | Chq 204 | 35,000 | | 204,000 Cr |
| Jan 27 | Bank charges | 3,500 | | 200,500 Cr |
| Jan 28 | Akinyi chq dishonoured | 40,000 | | 160,500 Cr |
| Jan 30 | Interest on deposit | | 2,000 | 162,500 Cr |

**Bank statement balance: KES 162,500 (Cr = money in bank)**

### Comparison:

**Items in Cash Book but NOT on Bank Statement (timing differences):**
- Chq 203 (KES 60,000) — **unpresented cheque**
- Chq 205 (KES 22,000) — **unpresented cheque**
- Mwangi deposit KES 25,000 (Jan 28) — **uncredited deposit**

**Items on Bank Statement but NOT in Cash Book (update cash book):**
- Standing order — Insurance: KES 8,000 (payment)
- Direct credit — Kamau: KES 15,000 (receipt)
- Bank charges: KES 3,500 (payment)
- Akinyi cheque dishonoured: KES 40,000 (reversal of receipt)
- Interest on deposit: KES 2,000 (receipt)

### Step 2: Updated Cash Book

```
              Cash Book (Bank Column) — Updated
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Dr (Receipts)               | Cr (Payments)
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Jan 31 Bal b/d     140,000 | Standing order    8,000
    Direct credit       15,000 | Bank charges      3,500
    (Kamau)                    | Dishonoured chq  40,000
    Interest earned      2,000 | (Akinyi)
                              |
                              | Jan 31 Bal c/d  105,500
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
                       157,000 |                 157,000
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Feb 1 Bal b/d      105,500 |
```

**Adjusted Cash Book balance: KES 105,500**

### Step 3: Bank Reconciliation Statement

```
    Njeri Hardware
    Bank Reconciliation Statement as at 31 January 2026
    ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                                                    KES
    Balance as per Bank Statement                162,500

    Add: Uncredited deposits
         Mwangi (28 Jan)                          25,000
                                                 -------
                                                 187,500

    Less: Unpresented cheques
         Chq 203                     60,000
         Chq 205                     22,000
                                                 (82,000)
                                                 -------
    Balance as per Updated Cash Book             105,500
                                                 =======
```

**Verification:** Bank statement balance (162,500) + Uncredited deposits (25,000) - Unpresented cheques (82,000) = 105,500 ✓

## 6. BANK RECONCILIATION WITH OVERDRAFT

If the business has a bank overdraft, the cash book will show a **credit balance** and the bank statement will show a **debit balance**.

**Example:** If the bank statement shows a debit balance of KES 50,000 (overdraft):

```
    Balance as per Bank Statement              (50,000)  [Overdraft]

    Add: Uncredited deposits                    20,000
                                               -------
                                               (30,000)

    Less: Unpresented cheques                  (15,000)
                                               -------
    Balance as per Updated Cash Book           (45,000)  [Overdraft]
                                               =======
```

### Question 3: Discussion

(a) Explain why a business should prepare a bank reconciliation statement regularly (e.g., monthly).

**Model Answer:**

1. **Error detection** — Monthly reconciliation catches errors in both the business's cash book and the bank's records early, before they compound.

2. **Fraud prevention** — Regular reconciliation can detect unauthorized withdrawals, forged cheques, or M-Pesa transactions that the business did not initiate.

3. **Cash flow management** — Knowing the true bank balance helps the business make informed decisions about payments, purchases, and investments. A Nairobi retailer needs to know exactly how much is available before placing an order with suppliers.

4. **Financial statement accuracy** — The bank balance on the Statement of Financial Position must be the updated (adjusted) balance. Without reconciliation, the balance sheet would be inaccurate.

5. **Audit requirement** — External auditors expect to see monthly bank reconciliations as evidence of proper internal controls. This is particularly important for businesses seeking loans from Kenyan banks.

(b) Explain the difference between a standing order and a direct debit.

**Model Answer:**

| Feature | Standing Order | Direct Debit |
|---------|---------------|-------------|
| **Initiated by** | The account holder (payer) | The payee (recipient) |
| **Amount** | Fixed amount each time | Can vary each time |
| **Control** | Payer sets up and can cancel directly | Payee initiates collection; payer authorizes |
| **Example in Kenya** | Monthly rent payment of KES 30,000 to landlord | KPLC electricity bill (varies monthly based on usage) |
| **Change** | Payer must instruct bank to change amount | Amount changes automatically based on the bill |
