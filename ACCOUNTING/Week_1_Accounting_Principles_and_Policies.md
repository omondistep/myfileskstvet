## 1. NATURE AND PURPOSE OF ACCOUNTING

### 1.1 Definition of Accounting

**Accounting** is the process of identifying, measuring, recording, classifying, summarizing, interpreting, and communicating financial information to users for decision-making purposes.

The American Accounting Association (AAA) defines accounting as:
> "The process of identifying, measuring, and communicating economic information to permit informed judgements and decisions by the users of information."

### 1.2 Purpose of Accounting

Accounting serves the following purposes:

1. **Recording transactions** — Maintaining a systematic record of all financial transactions (e.g., sales at a kiosk in Nairobi, purchases from suppliers in Mombasa)
2. **Stewardship** — Reporting to owners on how resources have been managed
3. **Decision-making** — Providing information to help managers, investors, and lenders make informed choices
4. **Legal compliance** — Meeting requirements of the Kenya Revenue Authority (KRA) for tax purposes, the Companies Act 2015, and other regulations
5. **Performance measurement** — Determining whether a business is making a profit or loss
6. **Planning and control** — Budgeting and comparing actual results against targets

### 1.3 Branches of Accounting

| Branch | Description | Example in Kenya |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| Financial Accounting | Recording and reporting financial transactions to external users | Safaricom PLC's annual financial statements |
| Management Accounting | Providing internal reports for decision-making | Cost analysis for a manufacturing firm in Thika |
| Cost Accounting | Determining the cost of products/services | Calculating cost per unit at a tea factory in Kericho |
| Auditing | Independent examination of financial statements | KPMG auditing Kenya Airways' accounts |
| Tax Accounting | Computing and managing tax obligations | Preparing VAT returns for KRA |
| Public Sector Accounting | Accounting for government entities | County government financial reporting |

## 3. QUALITATIVE CHARACTERISTICS OF ACCOUNTING INFORMATION

According to the IASB Conceptual Framework (which Kenya follows through adoption of IFRS):

### 3.1 Fundamental Qualitative Characteristics

1. **Relevance** — Information must be capable of influencing users' decisions
   - Has *predictive value* (helps forecast future outcomes)
   - Has *confirmatory value* (confirms or corrects prior expectations)
   - *Example:* A Nairobi shop owner needs current month's sales figures to decide whether to restock, not data from five years ago

2. **Faithful Representation** — Information must represent what it claims to represent
   - Must be **complete** — includes all necessary information
   - Must be **neutral** — free from bias
   - Must be **free from error** — accurate within reasonable limits
   - *Example:* If a Mombasa exporter reports revenue of KES 5,000,000, that figure should reflect actual sales, not inflated numbers

### 3.2 Enhancing Qualitative Characteristics

1. **Comparability** — Users should be able to compare financial information across periods and between entities
   - *Example:* Comparing Safaricom's 2024 and 2025 financial results

2. **Verifiability** — Different knowledgeable observers could reach the same conclusion
   - *Example:* An auditor can verify that a KES 200,000 payment was actually made by checking the bank statement

3. **Timeliness** — Information must be available when needed
   - *Example:* A KRA tax return filed after the deadline loses its usefulness for compliance

4. **Understandability** — Information should be clear to users with reasonable business knowledge
   - *Example:* Financial statements should use standard formats familiar to Kenyan accountants

## 4. ACCOUNTING CONCEPTS, PRINCIPLES, AND CONVENTIONS

### 4.1 Fundamental Accounting Concepts

#### (a) Going Concern Concept
The business is assumed to continue operating for the foreseeable future. Assets are valued at cost, not forced-sale value.

*Example:* Wanjiku's Hardware Store in Nyeri is valued based on its continued operation. If the business were closing, assets would be valued at what they could fetch in a quick sale (usually much less).

#### (b) Accruals (Matching) Concept
Revenue and expenses are recognized when they are earned or incurred, not when cash is received or paid.

*Example:* Kamau delivers goods worth KES 50,000 to a customer in December 2025 on credit. The revenue is recognized in December 2025 even though payment is received in January 2026.

#### (c) Consistency Concept
Once an accounting method is adopted, it should be applied consistently from one period to the next.

*Example:* If Otieno's Transport Company uses the straight-line method of depreciation for its matatus, it should continue using this method each year unless there is a valid reason to change.

#### (d) Prudence (Conservatism) Concept
Revenues and assets should not be overstated; losses and liabilities should not be understated. Anticipate losses but do not anticipate gains.

*Example:* A Nakuru-based trader is owed KES 100,000 by a customer who is experiencing financial difficulties. Under prudence, the trader should make a provision for the possible bad debt rather than assuming full payment.

### 4.2 Other Key Accounting Concepts

#### (e) Business Entity Concept
The business is treated as a separate entity from its owner(s).

*Example:* Akinyi runs a salon in Kisumu. Her personal M-Pesa transactions must be kept separate from the salon's M-Pesa till number transactions.

#### (f) Money Measurement Concept
Only transactions that can be expressed in monetary terms are recorded.

*Example:* If a hotel in Malindi has excellent staff morale, this cannot be recorded in the books because it cannot be measured in Kenya Shillings. However, the staff salaries paid (KES 500,000) can be recorded.

#### (g) Historical Cost Concept
Assets are recorded at their original purchase price.

*Example:* Muthoni bought land in Kiambu for KES 2,000,000 in 2015. Even though the market value is now KES 8,000,000, the land is recorded in the books at KES 2,000,000 (unless the entity applies revaluation).

#### (h) Dual Aspect (Duality) Concept
Every transaction has two effects — this is the basis of double entry bookkeeping.

*Example:* If a business buys furniture for KES 30,000 cash:
- Furniture (asset) increases by KES 30,000
- Cash (asset) decreases by KES 30,000

#### (i) Materiality Concept
Only items significant enough to influence users' decisions need strict accounting treatment. Immaterial items can be treated in the most convenient way.

*Example:* A pencil sharpener costing KES 50 bought by a large Nairobi firm is expensed immediately rather than capitalized and depreciated, even though technically it is an asset.

#### (j) Periodicity Concept
The life of a business is divided into regular accounting periods (usually 12 months) for reporting purposes.

*Example:* Most Kenyan companies report annually. The Kenyan government's financial year runs from 1 July to 30 June.

## 5. ACCOUNTING STANDARDS

### 5.1 What Are Accounting Standards?

Accounting standards are authoritative rules and guidelines that govern how financial transactions are recorded and reported. They ensure uniformity, comparability, and transparency.

### 5.2 Accounting Standards in Kenya

Kenya has adopted **International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS)** as issued by the International Accounting Standards Board (IASB).

The **Institute of Certified Public Accountants of Kenya (ICPAK)** is the regulatory body that oversees accounting standards in the country.

| Category | Applicable Standards | Example Entities |
|----------|---------------------|------------------|
| Listed companies and public interest entities | Full IFRS | Safaricom PLC, Equity Group, EABL |
| Small and Medium Enterprises | IFRS for SMEs | A medium-sized retail shop in Eldoret |
| Micro entities | May use simplified reporting | A small kiosk or mama mboga business |

### 5.3 Key International Accounting Standards (IAS) and IFRS

| Standard | Title | Relevance |
|----------|-------|-----------|
| IAS 1 | Presentation of Financial Statements | How financial statements should be structured |
| IAS 2 | Inventories | Valuation of stock (e.g., FIFO, weighted average) |
| IAS 16 | Property, Plant and Equipment | Recording and depreciation of fixed assets |
| IAS 37 | Provisions, Contingent Liabilities and Contingent Assets | Provisions for doubtful debts |
| IFRS 15 | Revenue from Contracts with Customers | When to recognize revenue |
| IFRS 9 | Financial Instruments | Classification of receivables and payables |

### 5.4 Role of Regulatory Bodies in Kenya

- **ICPAK** — Sets and enforces accounting and auditing standards
- **Capital Markets Authority (CMA)** — Regulates financial reporting of listed companies at the Nairobi Securities Exchange (NSE)
- **Kenya Revenue Authority (KRA)** — Requires proper accounting records for tax purposes
- **Registrar of Companies** — Requires filing of annual returns and financial statements under the Companies Act, 2015

**(b)** Explain the difference between the accruals concept and the prudence concept. Give a Kenyan example for each.

**Solution:**

- **Accruals concept:** Revenue and expenses are recognized when earned or incurred, not when cash changes hands. *Example:* Njeri's Boutique in Westlands delivers clothes worth KES 80,000 in March but receives payment in April. The sale is recorded in March.

- **Prudence concept:** Anticipated losses are provided for, but anticipated gains are not recognized until realized. *Example:* A Nairobi wholesaler is owed KES 200,000 by a struggling retailer. Prudence requires the wholesaler to create a provision for bad debts rather than assume full payment.

### Question 4: Discussion

Explain why it is important for Kenya to adopt International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS) rather than develop its own unique standards.

**Model Answer:**

Kenya's adoption of IFRS is important because:

1. **Comparability** — Kenyan companies' financial statements can be compared with those of companies in other countries, which is essential for attracting foreign investment (e.g., foreign investors at the NSE can compare Kenyan firms with South African or Nigerian firms).

2. **Access to international capital markets** — Companies like Safaricom or Equity Bank that seek foreign investors or list on international exchanges need internationally recognized financial statements.

3. **Cost efficiency** — Developing unique Kenyan standards would be expensive and time-consuming. Adopting IFRS leverages the expertise of the IASB.

4. **Credibility and transparency** — IFRS is globally recognized, which enhances the credibility of Kenyan financial reporting.

5. **Regional harmonization** — Within the East African Community (EAC), common standards facilitate cross-border trade and investment among Kenya, Uganda, Tanzania, Rwanda, and other member states.
