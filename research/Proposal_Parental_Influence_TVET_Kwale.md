## 2. BACKGROUND JUSTIFICATION

Kwale County is among Kenya's most educationally marginalised counties, with poverty rates significantly above the national average. The county has approximately 7 public TVET institutions (TVETA, 2023), yet enrolment figures remain persistently low. Nationally, only 275,000 youth were enrolled in approximately 1,900 TVET institutions in 2017, compared to over 520,000 in fewer than 40 universities (Brookings Institution, 2018). The TVETA Annual Returns Report (2023) further established that students in rural and arid regions are three times less likely to enrol in TVET programmes compared to their urban counterparts.

Research consistently identifies parental influence as one of the strongest predictors of a young person's educational and career pathway decisions. In Taita Taveta County — a neighbouring Coast region county — parents were identified as the most influential factor in course choice, with 58.5% of trainees agreeing that parents shaped their enrolment decisions (Mwangi et al., 2023). Despite this evidence, no study has specifically examined parental influence on TVET enrolment in Kwale County, leaving a significant research gap.

To investigate the influence of parental factors on trainee enrolment in public TVETs in Kwale County, Kenya.

### Indicator 1: Parental Attitude Towards TVET

**Definition:** The beliefs, perceptions, and values that parents hold regarding TVET as an educational and career pathway, and how these shape their children's willingness to enrol.

**Literature Support:**

**Source 1:**
Kennedy, M. G., & Chizoba, E. C. (2025). Influence of parental perception on students' willingness to pursue vocational careers: A synthesis of literature. *Advances in Social Sciences Research Journal, 12*(12), 19–29. https://doi.org/10.14738/assrj.1212.19653

*Summary:* This synthesis of literature from African and Asian contexts found that parents' beliefs, expectations, and cultural values significantly shape students' career decisions. In many societies, vocational education is perceived as inferior to academic professions, contributing to stigma, low enrolment, and reduced student confidence in TVET. The study concluded that when parents are informed about the economic benefits of vocational careers, they become strong advocates for their children's participation.

**Source 2:**
Mwangi, J., Mwangi, N., & Kariuki, P. (2023). Influence of parents' expectations on course choice in vocational training centres in Taita Taveta County, Kenya. *Saudi Journal of Humanities and Social Sciences, 8*(8), 267–275. https://www.saudijournals.com/articles/9451/

*Summary:* This Kenyan study analysed data from 2,386 trainees in Taita Taveta County VTCs. Parents were identified as the most influential factor in course choice, with 58.5% of trainees agreeing that parental expectations shaped their enrolment decisions (χ²=139.95, p<0.0001). Parents' expectations significantly predicted course choice (F=6.381, p<0.05), confirming the centrality of parental attitude in TVET enrolment decisions.

### Indicator 2: Parental Level of Education

**Definition:** The highest level of formal education attained by a trainee's parent or guardian, and how this shapes their orientation towards TVET as a viable pathway.

**Literature Support:**

**Source 1:**
Alqahtani, T. H. (2020). Vision 2030 and reducing the stigma of vocational and technical training among Saudi Arabian students. *International Journal for Research in Vocational Education and Training, 7*(1), 100–119. https://doi.org/10.13152/IJRVET.7.1.5

*Summary:* This quantitative study of 1,007 TVET students found a statistically significant relationship between perceptions of TVET and parental educational level. Students whose parents had lower levels of formal education were more likely to hold negative perceptions of TVET, while higher parental education was associated with more informed and positive attitudes towards vocational pathways.

**Source 2:**
Shi, J., & Zhu, Y. (2019). Technical and vocational education in China: Enrolment and socioeconomic status. *Journal of Vocational Education and Training, 71*(4), 543–560. https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ1233896

*Summary:* Using a nationally representative dataset from China, this study found that participants in upper-secondary TVET tended to come from relatively disadvantaged family backgrounds, particularly in terms of parental education levels. Parents with lower educational attainment were less likely to guide their children towards TVET, often due to limited awareness of its benefits and career outcomes.

### Indicator 3: Parental Occupation

**Definition:** The type of work or employment engaged in by a trainee's parent or guardian, and how exposure to skilled trades or professional occupations influences the trainee's enrolment decision.

**Literature Support:**

*Summary:* This Malaysian study using logistic regression found that father's job (FatherJob) and mother's job (MotherJob) were statistically significant variables in predicting a student's decision to enrol in TVET (p<0.05). *Summary:* This German study found that having parents with higher occupational status encouraged apprentices to pursue more demanding career paths within VET. The study supports the notion that parental occupation serves as a reference point for young people's educational and career decisions, with children of skilled workers more likely to enrol and persist in vocational training.

## 7. BACKGROUND OF THE STUDY

### 7.1 Global Perspective

Parental influence on children's educational and career decisions is a well-documented phenomenon across the world. Globally, parents serve as primary socialising agents who shape their children's aspirations, attitudes, and ultimately their choice of educational pathways, including Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET). UNESCO (2023) recognises TVET as a critical vehicle for skills development, youth employment, and inclusive economic growth, yet notes that enrolment in TVET programmes continues to lag behind academic pathways in most countries, partly due to persistent negative social perceptions reinforced within family units. A joint report by UNESCO, the World Bank, and the International Labour Organization (ILO) (2023) established that global youth unemployment stood at 16 percent in 2022, and called for urgent reforms to make TVET more attractive — including addressing the social stigma that parents and families attach to vocational training.

Research from Europe and Asia demonstrates that parental attitudes, education levels, and occupational backgrounds are among the strongest predictors of whether a young person chooses TVET over academic education. In Germany, Mühlemann and Wolter (2024) found that parental occupational status significantly influenced apprentices' career trajectories within vocational education, with children of skilled workers more likely to enrol and persist in VET programmes. Similarly, a study in China by Shi and Zhu (2019) using nationally representative data found that TVET participants tended to come from families with lower parental education levels, and that parental guidance — or the lack thereof — was a key determinant of enrolment patterns. In Malaysia, Yahaya et al. (2022) used logistic regression to demonstrate that both father's and mother's occupations were statistically significant predictors of a student's decision to enrol in TVET (p<0.05). These findings collectively establish that parental influence is not a peripheral factor but a central determinant of TVET enrolment globally.

Furthermore, Kennedy and Chizoba (2025), in a synthesis of literature from African and Asian contexts, found that parental perceptions of TVET as inferior to academic education contribute directly to low enrolment and reduced student confidence in vocational pathways. The study argued that reshaping parental perceptions through structured counselling and awareness interventions is essential to improving TVET participation rates worldwide.

### 7.2 Regional Perspective (Africa)

In sub-Saharan Africa, the relationship between parental influence and TVET enrolment is shaped by a complex interplay of socio-cultural norms, economic constraints, and historical attitudes towards vocational education. Across the continent, TVET has long been associated with academic failure, poverty, and low social status — perceptions that are transmitted and reinforced by parents within households (Akosah-Twumasi et al., 2021). A study by Akosah-Twumasi et al. (2021) on sub-Saharan African youth found that parents exerted strong influence over career choices through financial support, role modelling, and guidance, but that this influence was often directed away from vocational pathways in favour of professional academic careers perceived as more prestigious.

In Zimbabwe, Chikwanda et al. (2020) found that most parents held negative attitudes towards TVET, viewing it as a last resort for academically weak students. The study recommended media campaigns and awareness programmes to shift parental perceptions and encourage enrolment in TVET institutions. This finding is consistent with evidence from Rwanda, where Uwizeye et al. (2021) established that household socioeconomic status — closely tied to parental income and occupation — showed a very strong correlation with TVET enrolment among females (p<0.05), with low-income families facing the greatest barriers to access.

The African Union's Continental Education Strategy for Africa (CESA 2016–2025) explicitly identifies the need to address social and cultural barriers to TVET participation, including negative parental attitudes, as a priority for member states (African Union, 2016). Despite this policy recognition, empirical studies specifically examining parental influence on TVET enrolment at the county or district level remain limited across the continent, pointing to a persistent research gap that this study seeks to address in the Kenyan context.

### 7.3 Local Perspective (Kenya — Public TVETs)

In Kenya, TVET has been identified as a strategic pillar for achieving the goals of Vision 2030 and the Big Four Agenda, particularly in the areas of manufacturing, affordable housing, and food security. The government has invested significantly in expanding the TVET sector, with the number of registered institutions growing to approximately 2,286 accredited institutions by 2023 (TVETA, 2023). Despite this expansion, enrolment in public TVETs remains disproportionately low. The Brookings Institution (2018) reported that only 275,000 youth were enrolled in approximately 1,900 TVET institutions in 2017, compared to over 520,000 in fewer than 40 universities — a stark imbalance that reflects deep-seated societal preferences for academic over vocational education. The TVETA Annual Returns Report (2023) further confirmed that students in rural and marginalised regions are three times less likely to enrol in TVET programmes compared to their urban counterparts, with counties in the Coast region among the most underserved.

Research within Kenya consistently points to parental influence as a key factor shaping TVET enrolment decisions. Mwangi et al. (2023), in a study of 2,386 trainees across Vocational Training Centres in Taita Taveta County, found that parents were the most influential factor in course choice, with 58.5% of trainees citing parental expectations as the primary driver of their enrolment decisions (χ²=139.95, p<0.0001). The study established that parents' expectations significantly predicted course choice (b=0.599, p<0.05), underscoring the critical role of parental influence in the Kenyan TVET context. In Kwale County specifically, where poverty rates exceed the national average and educational participation remains among the lowest in the country, the influence of parental factors on TVET enrolment is likely to be particularly pronounced, yet remains unstudied.

### 7.4 Legal Background

The study is anchored in a robust legal and policy framework that underscores the government's commitment to expanding access to education and training, including TVET. At the constitutional level, Article 43(1)(f) of the Constitution of Kenya (2010) guarantees every person the right to education, while Article 53(1)(b) provides that every child has the right to free and compulsory basic education. Article 55(a) further obligates the State to take measures, including affirmative action programmes, to ensure that youth access relevant education and training. These provisions establish a constitutional imperative for addressing barriers — including parental influence — that prevent young people from accessing TVET.

At the legislative level, the Technical and Vocational Education and Training Act No. 29 of 2013 (TVET Act, 2013) provides the primary legal framework governing TVET in Kenya. The Act established the Technical and Vocational Education and Training Authority (TVETA) as the regulatory body responsible for licensing, registering, and accrediting TVET institutions, as well as setting standards for quality assurance in training programmes. The Act mandates the expansion of TVET access across all counties, making low enrolment in counties such as Kwale a matter of legal concern and policy priority.

The Kenya National TVET Policy (2014) further articulates the government's commitment to promoting TVET as an equal and valued pathway alongside academic education, and calls for community sensitisation programmes to address negative perceptions of vocational training. At the international level, Kenya is a signatory to the UNESCO Recommendation on TVET (2015), which calls on member states to promote TVET as a pathway to decent work, social equity, and sustainable development, and to address social and cultural barriers — including family attitudes — that limit participation (UNESCO, 2015). These legal and policy instruments collectively provide the normative foundation for investigating and addressing parental influence as a determinant of TVET enrolment in Kwale County.

## 8. STATEMENT OF THE PROBLEM

Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET) is globally recognised as a critical pathway for equipping youth with practical skills, enhancing employability, and driving economic development. In Kenya, the government has invested substantially in expanding the TVET sector, with over 2,286 accredited institutions established by 2023 (TVETA, 2023). Ideally, public TVET institutions should attract adequate enrolment of youth, particularly in marginalised counties, enabling them to acquire marketable skills, reduce unemployment, and contribute to national development goals under Vision 2030. Parents, as primary decision-makers in their children's educational pathways, are expected to play a supportive role in guiding youth towards TVET as a viable and valued career option.

Despite this ideal, enrolment in public TVET institutions in Kenya remains persistently low, particularly in rural and marginalised counties. The Brookings Institution (2018) reported that only 275,000 youth were enrolled in approximately 1,900 TVET institutions nationally in 2017, compared to over 520,000 in fewer than 40 universities. The TVETA Annual Returns Report (2023) further confirmed that students in rural regions are three times less likely to enrol in TVET compared to their urban counterparts. Kwale County, one of Kenya's most educationally and economically marginalised counties, exemplifies this crisis, with approximately seven public TVET institutions recording persistently low trainee enrolment figures despite government efforts to expand access.

Several scholars have examined factors influencing TVET enrolment in various contexts. Mwangi et al. (2023) established that parental expectations were the most significant predictor of course choice among 2,386 trainees in Taita Taveta County, Kenya. Uwizeye et al. (2021) demonstrated that household socioeconomic status strongly correlated with TVET enrolment among females in Rwanda. Chikwanda et al. (2020) found that negative parental attitudes towards TVET in Zimbabwe contributed to low enrolment, while Kennedy and Chizoba (2025) confirmed through a synthesis of African and Asian literature that parental perceptions of TVET as inferior to academic education remain a primary driver of low participation globally.

However, these studies were conducted in contexts geographically and socio-culturally distinct from Kwale County. The Taita Taveta study, while Kenyan and Coast-adjacent, focused on a different county with different demographic and cultural dynamics. The Rwandan and Zimbabwean studies are not transferable to the Kenyan Coast context without empirical validation. No study has specifically investigated the influence of parental factors — including parental attitude, education level, occupation, and income — on trainee enrolment in public TVETs in Kwale County. This contextual gap means that TVET managers, county education officers, and policymakers in Kwale lack locally grounded evidence to design targeted interventions. This study therefore seeks to fill that gap by generating county-specific empirical data on parental influence and its effect on trainee enrolment in public TVETs in Kwale County, Kenya.

## 9. SCOPE OF THE STUDY

This study is delimited by its objectives and geographical location.

In terms of objectives, the study is limited to investigating the influence of parental factors on trainee enrolment in public TVETs in Kwale County. Specifically, it examines four indicators of parental influence: parental attitude towards TVET, parental level of education, parental occupation, and parental income level. The study does not extend to other potential determinants of TVET enrolment such as institutional factors, peer influence, government policy implementation, or individual trainee characteristics. It is further limited to enrolment as the outcome variable and does not examine trainee retention, completion rates, or academic performance.

In terms of geographical location, the study is confined to public TVET institutions within Kwale County, Kenya. Kwale County comprises four sub-counties — Msambweni, Lungalunga, Matuga, and Kinango — and the study targets the approximately seven public TVET institutions operating within these sub-counties. Private TVET institutions and public TVETs in other counties within the Coast region or elsewhere in Kenya fall outside the scope of this study. The findings are therefore specific to the Kwale County context and may not be directly generalised to other counties without further research.

### 2.2 Theoretical Framework

#### 2.2.1 Social Cognitive Career Theory (SCCT)

This study is grounded in the Social Cognitive Career Theory (SCCT), developed by Lent, Brown, and Hackett (1994). SCCT is derived from Bandura's (1986) general social cognitive theory and provides a comprehensive framework for understanding how individuals develop career interests, make educational and career choices, and achieve success in their chosen pathways. The theory identifies three core variables — self-efficacy beliefs, outcome expectations, and personal goals — as the primary mechanisms through which people navigate career development. Critically, SCCT recognises that these variables do not operate in isolation; they are shaped by contextual factors including family background, socioeconomic conditions, and environmental supports and barriers (Lent et al., 1994).

In the context of this study, SCCT is particularly relevant because it explicitly positions parental influence as a contextual factor that shapes a young person's self-efficacy beliefs and outcome expectations regarding TVET. When parents hold negative attitudes towards vocational education, communicate low expectations, or are unable to provide financial support for training, they function as environmental barriers that reduce a trainee's confidence in and motivation to pursue TVET. Conversely, parents who model vocational occupations, hold positive attitudes, and provide financial and emotional support function as contextual supports that enhance TVET enrolment. SCCT thus provides the theoretical lens through which the four indicators of parental influence examined in this study can be understood as interconnected determinants of trainee enrolment behaviour.

#### 2.2.2 Bourdieu's Theory of Cultural and Social Capital

Complementing SCCT, this study also draws on Pierre Bourdieu's theory of cultural and social capital (Bourdieu, 1986). Bourdieu argued that educational outcomes are not determined solely by individual ability or effort, but are profoundly shaped by the capital — economic, cultural, and social — that families possess and transmit to their children. Cultural capital, in particular, refers to the knowledge, skills, attitudes, and dispositions that parents pass on to children through socialisation, which in turn shapes their educational aspirations and choices (Bourdieu, 1986). A parent with higher educational attainment transmits greater cultural capital, equipping their child with the awareness and confidence to navigate educational systems, including TVET.

Bourdieu's framework is directly applicable to the Kwale County context, where low parental education levels, subsistence-based occupations, and limited household income constrain the cultural and economic capital available to young people. These capital deficits translate into limited awareness of TVET opportunities, negative perceptions of vocational pathways, and financial inability to meet training costs — all of which suppress enrolment. The theory thus provides a structural explanation for why parental factors are so consequential in determining TVET participation in marginalised communities.

### 2.3 Conceptual Framework

The conceptual framework of this study posits that trainee enrolment in public TVETs (dependent variable) is influenced by parental influence (independent variable), operationalised through four indicators: parental attitude towards TVET, parental level of education, parental occupation, and parental income level. These indicators are drawn from the literature and are mediated by the trainee's own career decision-making process, as explained by SCCT. External factors such as government policy, institutional quality, and peer influence are acknowledged but fall outside the scope of this study.

### 2.4 Parental Attitude Towards TVET and Trainee Enrolment

Parental attitude towards TVET is one of the most consistently documented determinants of youth enrolment in vocational education across global and African contexts. Kennedy and Chizoba (2025) conducted a synthesis of literature from African and Asian contexts and found that parents' beliefs, expectations, and cultural values significantly shape students' career decisions. The study established that in many societies, vocational education is perceived as inferior to academic professions, contributing to stigma, low enrolment, and reduced student confidence in TVET. Misconceptions about income potential and employment opportunities further reinforce negative parental attitudes, creating a cycle of low participation that is difficult to break without targeted intervention.

In the Kenyan context, Mwangi et al. (2023) analysed data from 2,386 trainees in Taita Taveta County and found that parents were the most influential factor in course choice, with 58.5% of trainees agreeing that parental expectations shaped their enrolment decisions (χ²=139.95, p<0.0001). Parents' expectations significantly predicted course choice (b=0.599, p<0.05), confirming that parental attitude is not merely a background variable but an active predictor of enrolment behaviour. This finding is consistent with evidence from Zimbabwe, where Chikwanda et al. (2020) found that most parents viewed TVET as a last resort for academically weak students, and recommended awareness campaigns to shift these perceptions.

At the global level, Alqahtani (2020) found a statistically significant relationship between parental educational level and perceptions of TVET among 1,007 students in Saudi Arabia, with students from families holding negative attitudes towards vocational training being significantly less likely to enrol. The study concluded that parental attitude functions as a social norm that either legitimises or delegitimises TVET as a pathway, with profound consequences for enrolment rates. These findings collectively suggest that addressing parental attitudes is a prerequisite for improving TVET participation in Kwale County.

---

### 2.5 Parental Level of Education and Trainee Enrolment

The level of formal education attained by parents has been widely studied as a determinant of children's educational choices, including TVET enrolment. Shi and Zhu (2019), using a nationally representative dataset from China, found that TVET participants tended to come from families with lower parental education levels, and that parental guidance — or the lack thereof — was a key determinant of enrolment patterns. Parents with lower educational attainment were less likely to guide their children towards TVET, often due to limited awareness of its benefits and career outcomes. This finding highlights a paradox: the families most likely to benefit from TVET are often the least equipped to advocate for it.

Alqahtani (2020) corroborated this finding in the Saudi Arabian context, establishing that parental educational level was significantly associated with students' perceptions of TVET (p<0.05). Students whose parents had attained higher levels of education were more likely to hold informed and positive views of vocational training, while those from less educated households were more susceptible to social stigma surrounding TVET. In the Norwegian context, Holen et al. (2021) found that students from families with low socioeconomic resources — closely correlated with low parental education — were at greater risk of not completing upper secondary VET, underscoring the compounding effect of parental education on both enrolment and persistence.

In Kenya, the relationship between parental education and TVET enrolment is particularly salient in marginalised counties such as Kwale, where adult literacy rates remain below the national average. Parents with limited formal education are less likely to be aware of TVET opportunities, less able to navigate enrolment processes, and less likely to perceive vocational training as a credible pathway to employment. This creates a structural barrier to TVET participation that is rooted in intergenerational educational disadvantage, consistent with Bourdieu's (1986) concept of cultural capital transmission.

---

### 2.6 Parental Occupation and Trainee Enrolment

Parental occupation has been identified in multiple studies as a significant predictor of a young person's decision to enrol in TVET. Yahaya et al. (2022), using logistic regression analysis in Malaysia, found that both father's occupation (FatherJob) and mother's occupation (MotherJob) were statistically significant predictors of a student's decision to enrol in TVET (p<0.05). Students whose parents were employed in technical or vocational occupations were significantly more likely to choose TVET pathways, suggesting that occupational role modelling by parents is a key mechanism through which enrolment decisions are shaped.

This finding is supported by Mühlemann and Wolter (2024), who studied reference group effects on VET participation in Germany and found that parental occupational status significantly influenced apprentices' career trajectories. Students with parents in higher-status occupations were more likely to pursue demanding vocational pathways, while those from lower-status occupational backgrounds were more likely to drop out or choose less demanding programmes. The study concluded that parental occupation functions as a reference point that shapes young people's aspirations and self-efficacy in relation to vocational training.

In the sub-Saharan African context, Akosah-Twumasi et al. (2021) found that parents in professional occupations were more likely to steer their children away from vocational pathways towards academic careers perceived as more prestigious. This occupational prestige bias is particularly pronounced in communities where skilled trades are associated with low social status, as is the case in many parts of coastal Kenya. In Kwale County, where the dominant occupations are subsistence farming, fishing, and informal trade, the absence of parental role models in skilled technical occupations may further suppress TVET enrolment by limiting young people's exposure to and aspirations for vocational careers.

---

### 2.7 Parental Income Level and Trainee Enrolment

Parental income level is among the most extensively documented barriers to TVET enrolment in low- and middle-income countries. Uwizeye et al. (2021), in a study of female TVET access in Rwanda, found that household socioeconomic status showed a very strong correlation with TVET enrolment (p<0.05). Families with lower income levels were significantly less likely to enrol their children in TVET due to the direct and indirect costs of training, including tuition fees, tools and materials, uniforms, and transport. The study concluded that economic barriers are among the most significant determinants of TVET access in sub-Saharan Africa, disproportionately affecting girls and youth from rural households.

The Brookings Institution (2018) similarly identified the high cost of technical training as a primary barrier to TVET enrolment in Kenya, noting that despite government subsidies, many low-income households remain unable to meet the residual costs of vocational training. This is particularly acute in counties such as Kwale, where poverty rates significantly exceed the national average. The TVETA Annual Returns Report (2023) confirmed that students in rural and marginalised regions are three times less likely to enrol in TVET compared to their urban counterparts, a disparity that is closely linked to household income differentials.

At the global level, a systematic review by Kluve et al. (2017) of TVET interventions in low- and middle-income countries found that financial barriers — including inability to pay fees and opportunity costs of foregone income — were consistently cited as the primary reasons for non-enrolment in vocational programmes. The review recommended targeted financial support mechanisms, including bursaries and conditional cash transfers, as essential tools for improving TVET access among low-income households. In the Kwale County context, where a significant proportion of households depend on subsistence livelihoods, parental income level is likely to be a particularly powerful determinant of whether a young person can access and remain enrolled in public TVET institutions.

---

### 2.8 Summary of Literature Review

The reviewed literature consistently establishes that parental influence — operationalised through attitude towards TVET, level of education, occupation, and income level — is a significant determinant of trainee enrolment in vocational education across global, African, and Kenyan contexts. The Social Cognitive Career Theory (Lent et al., 1994) and Bourdieu's theory of cultural and social capital (Bourdieu, 1986) provide complementary theoretical lenses for understanding how these parental factors shape young people's career decision-making and enrolment behaviour. While studies from Kenya (Mwangi et al., 2023), Rwanda (Uwizeye et al., 2021), Zimbabwe (Chikwanda et al., 2020), and globally (Kennedy & Chizoba, 2025; Yahaya et al., 2022) confirm the significance of parental influence on TVET enrolment, none has examined this relationship specifically within the context of public TVETs in Kwale County. This study therefore addresses a clear contextual gap in the literature.

## CHAPTER THREE: RESEARCH METHODOLOGY

### 3.1 Introduction

This chapter describes the research design, target population, sample and sampling technique, research instruments, data collection procedures, and data analysis methods used in this study. The methodological choices made here are informed by the nature of the research problem, the objectives of the study, and the approaches adopted by scholars in related studies reviewed in Chapter Two. The chapter is guided by the principle that methodology must be appropriate to the research questions being investigated (Kothari, 2004).

---

### 3.2 Research Design

This study adopted a **descriptive survey research design**. A descriptive survey design is a systematic method of collecting data from a defined population in order to describe, explain, and interpret the characteristics, opinions, attitudes, and behaviours of that population at a given point in time (Kothari, 2004). Creswell (2014) defines descriptive research as a design that seeks to describe the current state of a phenomenon as it exists, without manipulating variables. This design is appropriate for this study because the researcher sought to describe and explain the influence of parental factors — attitude, education level, occupation, and income level — on trainee enrolment in public TVETs in Kwale County, without introducing any experimental intervention.

The descriptive survey design has been widely used in similar TVET studies. Mwangi et al. (2023) employed a descriptive survey design in their study of parental expectations and course choice in Taita Taveta County VTCs, citing its suitability for capturing the perceptions and experiences of a large number of respondents efficiently. Similarly, Uwizeye et al. (2021) used a survey approach in their study of socio-economic barriers to TVET enrolment in Rwanda, noting that surveys allow for the collection of standardised data that can be analysed quantitatively to identify patterns and relationships. The design is therefore well-suited to the objectives of this study, which require the collection of structured data from trainees and TVET managers across multiple institutions in Kwale County.

---

### 3.3 Target Population

The target population refers to the entire group of individuals or elements that the researcher wishes to study and to whom the findings of the study will be generalised (Kothari, 2004). For this study, the target population comprised all **trainees** and **principals/managers** of public TVET institutions in Kwale County, Kenya.

According to the TVETA accredited institutions register (2023), there are approximately **seven (7) public TVET institutions** in Kwale County, namely:

| 1 | Diani Vocational Training Center | Msambweni |
| 2 | Kinango Technical and Vocational College | Kinango |
| 3 | Kinango Vocational Training Center | Kinango |

In addition, the seven principals/managers of these institutions constitute a secondary target population, giving a combined target population of approximately **357 respondents**.

### 3.4 Sample Size

The sample size for this study was determined using the **Krejcie and Morgan (1970)** table for sample size determination. Krejcie and Morgan (1970) developed a widely used formula and corresponding table that allows researchers to determine the minimum sample size required to adequately represent a given population at a 95% confidence level with a margin of error of ±5%. This approach has been widely adopted in Kenyan educational research (Mwangi et al., 2023; Kothari, 2004).

Using the Krejcie and Morgan (1970) table, a target population of **350 trainees** yields a recommended sample size of **183 trainees**. Given the small number of principals/managers (7), a **census approach** was adopted for this sub-group, meaning all seven principals/managers were included in the study. The total sample for the study was therefore **190 respondents** (183 trainees + 7 principals/managers).

|---|---|---|---|

| Principals/Managers | 7 | 7 (census) | Total population |

### 3.5 Sampling Technique

#### 3.5.1 Stratified Random Sampling (Trainees)

For the trainee respondents, the study employed **stratified random sampling**. This technique involves dividing the target population into distinct subgroups (strata) based on a shared characteristic, and then randomly selecting respondents from each stratum in proportion to its size within the total population (Kothari, 2004). In this study, the strata were the seven public TVET institutions in Kwale County. Proportionate allocation was used to determine the number of trainees to be sampled from each institution, ensuring that each institution was represented in the sample in proportion to its enrolment size.

Stratified random sampling was chosen over simple random sampling because it ensures representation from all seven institutions, preventing the possibility that smaller institutions are excluded from the sample. This is consistent with the approach used by Mwangi et al. (2023), who employed stratified sampling across multiple VTCs in Taita Taveta County to ensure institutional representation. Within each stratum, respondents were selected using **simple random sampling** — specifically, the lottery method — to ensure every trainee had an equal chance of being selected.

#### 3.5.2 Census (Principals/Managers)

For the principals and managers, a **census approach** was adopted, meaning all seven were included in the study. This is justified by the small size of this sub-group; Kothari (2004) recommends that when a sub-population is small (typically fewer than 30), a census is preferable to sampling to avoid loss of important information. The principals/managers provided institutional-level data on enrolment trends, parental engagement, and institutional challenges, complementing the trainee-level data.

---

### 3.6 Research Instruments

#### 3.6.1 Questionnaire (Trainees)

The primary data collection instrument for trainee respondents was a **structured questionnaire**. A questionnaire is a set of written questions designed to collect standardised data from a large number of respondents efficiently and consistently (Creswell, 2014). The questionnaire for trainees was divided into sections corresponding to the study's specific objectives:

- **Section A:** Demographic information (age, gender, sub-county)
- **Section B:** Parental attitude towards TVET (Likert scale items, 1–5)
- **Section C:** Parental level of education (categorical items)
- **Section D:** Parental occupation (categorical items)
- **Section E:** Parental income level (ordinal items)
- **Section F:** Trainee enrolment decision (items on how parental factors influenced the decision to enrol)

A **5-point Likert scale** (1 = Strongly Disagree to 5 = Strongly Agree) was used for attitudinal items, consistent with the approach used by Mwangi et al. (2023) and Alqahtani (2020) in similar studies. The questionnaire was self-administered with the assistance of the researcher to ensure clarity and completeness.

#### 3.6.2 Interview Guide (Principals/Managers)

For the seven principals/managers, a **semi-structured interview guide** was used. Semi-structured interviews allow the researcher to collect in-depth qualitative data while maintaining a consistent structure across respondents (Creswell, 2014). The interview guide covered enrolment trends in the institution, the perceived role of parental factors in enrolment decisions, and institutional strategies for engaging parents. This qualitative data served to triangulate and contextualise the quantitative findings from the trainee questionnaires, consistent with the mixed-methods approach recommended by Kothari (2004) for descriptive survey studies.

#### 3.6.3 Validity and Reliability

**Content validity** of the instruments was established through expert review by the study supervisor and two colleagues with experience in TVET research, who assessed whether the items adequately covered the constructs being measured. **Reliability** of the questionnaire was tested using a **pilot study** conducted with 19 trainees (10% of the sample) from a public TVET institution in a neighbouring county not included in the main study. The Cronbach's Alpha coefficient was computed to assess internal consistency, with a threshold of α ≥ 0.7 considered acceptable (Kothari, 2004).

---

### 3.7 Data Collection Procedure

The researcher obtained a research permit from the National Commission for Science, Technology and Innovation (NACOSTI) and an introductory letter from the institution. Permission was then sought from the County Director of Education and the principals of the seven public TVET institutions. Questionnaires were administered to trainees during scheduled class sessions to maximise response rates. Interviews with principals/managers were conducted at mutually agreed times. All respondents were assured of confidentiality and anonymity, and participation was voluntary.

---

### 3.8 Data Analysis

#### 3.8.1 Quantitative Data Analysis

Quantitative data from the trainee questionnaires was analysed using both **descriptive** and **inferential statistics**, with the aid of the Statistical Package for Social Sciences (SPSS) version 26.

**Descriptive statistics** — including frequencies, percentages, means, and standard deviations — were used to summarise and describe the characteristics of the sample and the distribution of responses across the four IV indicators. Descriptive analysis is appropriate for answering research questions that ask "what is the extent of..." or "how does... distribute across the sample" (Kothari, 2004). This directly addresses Research Questions 1 through 4 at the descriptive level.

**Inferential statistics** — specifically **Pearson's correlation coefficient** and **simple linear regression** — were used to test the relationships between each parental influence indicator (IV) and trainee enrolment (DV). Pearson's correlation was used to determine the direction and strength of the relationship between each indicator and enrolment, while regression analysis was used to determine the predictive power of each indicator. This approach is consistent with Mwangi et al. (2023), who used regression analysis (b=0.599, p<0.05) to establish the predictive relationship between parental expectations and course choice in Taita Taveta County VTCs. Inferential analysis allows the findings to be generalised beyond the sample to the broader population of trainees in public TVETs in Kwale County.

Results were presented using **frequency tables**, **bar charts**, and **regression output tables** for clarity and ease of interpretation.

#### 3.8.2 Qualitative Data Analysis

Qualitative data from the principal/manager interviews was analysed using **thematic analysis**. Thematic analysis involves identifying, analysing, and reporting patterns (themes) within qualitative data (Creswell, 2014). Responses were transcribed, coded, and organised into themes aligned with the study's specific objectives. The qualitative findings were used to triangulate and enrich the quantitative results, providing institutional context for the patterns identified in the trainee data.

### 3.9 Ethical Considerations

The study adhered to the following ethical principles:

- **Informed consent:** All respondents were informed of the purpose of the study and their right to withdraw at any time without consequence.
- **Confidentiality:** No personally identifying information was collected or disclosed. Questionnaires were anonymous.
- **Voluntary participation:** Participation was entirely voluntary.
- **Research permit:** A research permit was obtained from NACOSTI prior to data collection.
- **Institutional permission:** Written permission was obtained from the County Director of Education and each institution's principal before data collection commenced.
