# CBET Curriculum Development — Unit B7102
**Qualification:** Professional Technical Trainer Level 6  
**Unit Code:** B7102 | **Duration:** 30 Hours  
**Unit Description:** This unit covers competencies required to develop a competency-based curriculum. It involves applying curriculum development concepts, developing CBET curriculum, and reviewing curriculum. Applies in the TVET sector.

---

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this unit, the learner should be able to:

1. Apply curriculum development concepts
2. Develop competency-based education and training curriculum
3. Review CBET curriculum

## Topic 1: Introduction — Terms, Concepts, Rationale, Purpose & Dimensions

### 1.1 Key Terms and Concepts

**Education**
- Derived from the Latin *educatio* (from *educare*)
- Oluoch (1982): *"A process of acquiring and developing desired knowledge, skills and attitudes/values"*

**Curriculum**
- From Latin *currere* — meaning a race course or track
- Includes all that is in the timetable AND co-curricular activities outside the timetable but within the school environment
- Key definitions:
  - **Kerr (1968):** Learning which is planned and guided by the school, whether in groups or individually, inside or outside the school
  - **Bishop (1995):** Sum total of all experiences a pupil undergoes within or without the school to enable students adopt a positive attitude to learning
  - **Shiundu & Omulando (1992):** Class and out-of-class activities carried out under the aegis of a school in response to societal demands
  - **Oluoch (1982):** All that which is planned to enable students to develop the desired knowledge, skills and attitudes

**Curriculum Development**
- Ndwiga & Maithya (2006): *"The planning of learning opportunities intended to bring about certain changes in the learners"*
- The culmination of curriculum development is a school curriculum

---

### 1.2 Rationale for Studying Curriculum Development

Aims of teaching curriculum development:
- Help teacher trainees understand procedures and strategies of curriculum development
- Help them correctly interpret curriculum programs
- Enables teachers to be invited by KNEC to set, moderate, invigilate and mark exams
- Enables participation in KIE subject and course panels (writing books, developing course outlines, preparing teaching aids and evaluation materials)
- Enables translation of national goals of education into instructional objectives
- Curriculum development is a professional responsibility of all teachers

---

### 1.3 Purposes of the School Curriculum

- Translates national goals into concrete, observable behavioral changes
- Regulates teachers' roles and activities
- Specifies objectives that schools should attain
- Specifies activities that take place in an institution
- Ensures uniformity in coverage of activities
- Regulates evaluation procedures of an education system

---

### 1.4 Dimensions of the School Curriculum

Dimensions are aspects or facets of a curriculum. There are **four dimensions**:

| Dimension | Description | Examples |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Formal** | Courses of study (syllabi) offered in school; structured and graded subject matter | History, Geography, Kiswahili |
| **Non-formal** | Organized, planned out-of-class learning activities | Games, sports, drama, clubs |
| **Informal** | Created environment where students learn unconsciously through day-to-day experiences | School rules, staff interactions, play |
| **Emerging** | Unplanned learning opportunities that occur in school | Parents' day, speech day, prize giving, graduation |

## Topic 2: Curriculum Elements

Elements are the **components or parts** of the school curriculum. Ralph Tyler (1949) identified four fundamental elements by asking four questions:

1. What educational purposes should the school seek to attain? → **Objectives**
2. What educational experiences can be provided to attain these purposes? → **Learning Experiences / Content**
3. How can these experiences be organized effectively? → **Organization of Learning Experiences**
4. How can we determine whether objectives have been achieved? → **Evaluation**

---

### 2.1 Curriculum Objectives

Also called *educational objectives* — descriptions of the ends being sought in educating learners.

**Three categories of objectives:**

**a) Aims of Education**
- General, broad statements of intent providing direction to educational action
- Written in amorphous terms: *learn, know, understand, appreciate* — not directly measurable
- Guide the education system in planning content

**b) Goals of Education**
- Broad statements of educational intention specified for each level of schooling (pre-primary to higher education)
- Time-bound; stated as expected end-results of schooling at each level
- May be in amorphous or more specific behavioral terms

**c) Instructional Objectives**
- Describe an intended **behavioral change** a learner is expected to exhibit after a learning experience
- Expressed in behavioral terms using **action verbs**
- Describe an outcome of action (knowledge, skills, techniques or attitudes acquired)

**Functions of Curriculum Objectives:**
1. Define the direction of educational development
2. Determine the number of levels for the program
3. Inform students of standards and expectations
4. Serve as an implicit contract between instructor and students
5. Drive curriculum planning
6. Guide selection of content, learning experiences and strategies
7. Serve as a guide in evaluating instructional progress
8. Reflect the needs and aspirations of society

**Sources of Educational Objectives:**

1. **Needs of Society** — national philosophy, ideology, value systems; changing economic, technological, political, religious life
2. **Students' Needs and Interests** — learners' ability (physical and mental), special needs, individuality vs. societal needs; learners suggest what to teach and what materials to use
3. **Academic Specialists** — teachers who interact with learners; assist in formulating objectives, selecting learning activities, applying child psychology, conducting evaluation
4. **Community Sources** — community advisory groups; input from parents, vocational groups, field trips
5. **Psychodynamic Factors** — biological inheritance, IQ, readiness, maturation, individual differences in ability to learn

**Screens of Curriculum Objectives** (used to test if objectives are relevant, adequate, valid, within scope, and appropriate):

- **Philosophical Screens:** Values and beliefs of the nation; expressed in the governing political party's manifesto (e.g., peace, love, unity; integrity, transparency, accountability)
- **Psychological Screens:** Capabilities of learners, maturational level, rate of learning, how students learn, individual aspects
- **Sociological Screens:** Cultural values, societal needs, learners' background (family and religious influences); knowledge and skills youth need to cope with tasks in society

---

### 2.2 Learning Experiences

- Tyler (1949): *"The interaction between the learner and the external conditions in the environment to which we react"*
- Learning takes place through the experiences the learner has — through reactions with the environment

**Examples of learning experiences/opportunities:**
- Content or subject matter
- The teacher
- Resource persons
- Fellow learners
- Resource materials
- Teaching methods
- Learning activities

**Criteria for Selection of Content and Learning Experiences:**

| Criterion | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| Conformity with objectives | Experiences must give students opportunity to practice behavior implied by objectives |
| Validity | Must be consistent with current developments in the subject area |
| Significance | Must be meaningful and contribute to the learning process |
| Learner ability | Must be adjusted to abilities and individual differences |
| Interests | Must be inviting/stimulating to maintain motivation |
| Satisfaction | Learners must derive satisfaction from the behavior implied by objectives |
| Relevance | Must relate to life outside school and solve real problems |
| Consistency with social reality | Must not contradict actual situations at home or in society |
| Economy | Select experiences likely to bring about multiple learning outcomes |
| Reinforcement | Select experiences that reinforce previous experiences |

---

### 2.3 Organization of Learning Experiences

Once selected, learning experiences must be organized. Curriculum is usually organized by subjects, at three levels:
- **The lesson** — planned for several days to cover a given topic
- **The topic** — covers several lessons over several days
- **The unit** — covers several topics related to a theme

**Principles of Organization:**

- **Scope:** Breadth and depth of content; what is covered and how much emphasis it receives
- **Sequence:** Vertical development from one level to another; building new knowledge on prior knowledge; simple to complex / familiar to unfamiliar
- **Continuity:** Repeated exposure to important content at different levels to enable high retention
- **Integration:** Horizontal relationships between different curriculum areas; linking content from one field to another for a unified view
- **Balance:** Each educational objective is adequately achieved; develops all major areas of human competence

---

### 2.4 Curriculum Evaluation

**Definition:**
- The process of establishing the extent to which objectives of a programme are being or have been achieved
- Shiundu & Omulando (1992): *"A process of collection and provision of data for the sake of facilitating decision making at various stages of curriculum development"*

**Areas evaluated:**
- Objectives
- Scope of content
- Quality of personnel
- Relative importance of school subjects
- Equipment and instructional materials

**Purposes of Evaluation:**

| Level | Purpose |
|-------|---------|
| Individual students | Discover what students have learnt; identify areas needing help |
| Classroom | Determine effectiveness of teaching; assess relevance of content, methods, activities |
| Curriculum materials | Assess relevance, usability, appropriateness, affordability |
| School-wide | Assess overall effectiveness; reveal areas needing attention |
| Curriculum developers | Facilitate decision making; guide revision and improvement |
| Community | Assess community attitudes and inputs to the curriculum |

**Types of Evaluation:**

1. **Pre-assessment / Reflective Evaluation** — conducted at inception; determines if students have prerequisite knowledge and skills
2. **Formative Evaluation** — takes place *during* implementation; guides and promotes development by providing data for improvement; should occur at ALL stages
3. **Summative Evaluation** — carried out at the *end* of a programme; facilitates major decisions (continue, expand, modify, or stop)
4. **Impact Evaluation** — aspect of summative evaluation; establishes impact on beneficiaries and the community

**Criteria for Curriculum Evaluation:**

| Criterion | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Consistency with objectives | Measures attainment of objectives across cognitive, affective, psychomotor domains |
| Comprehensiveness | Covers all objectives, administrative aspects, instructional methods, individual learners, teachers |
| Validity | Instruments measure what they are intended to measure |
| Reliability | Instruments give same results when administered at different times |
| Continuity | Evaluation is continuous; integral part of curriculum development |
| Relevance | Value of programme in relation to stakeholder needs and national priorities |
| Efficiency | Programme uses resources in the most economical manner |
| Effectiveness | Activity achieves satisfactory results in relation to stated objectives |
| Impact | Results of intervention — intended/unintended, positive/negative |
| Sustainability | Activities and impact likely to continue after external support is withdrawn |

**Methods of Evaluation:**
- Questionnaires
- Interviews
- Tests
- Observation
- Documentary analysis
- Focused group discussions

**Role of Teachers in Curriculum Evaluation:**
- Provide data on progress of students and materials
- Best placed to judge quality of materials, depth of topics, and sequencing
- Observe, document, construct tests and examinations, report findings systematically
- Report experiences in subject panels during curriculum development

## Topic 3: Curriculum Development Models

A curriculum development model provides a systematic framework for planning, designing, and implementing a curriculum. Key models include:

---

### 3.1 Ralph Tyler's Model (1949)

Tyler's model is a **linear/rational model** based on four fundamental questions (the Tyler Rationale):

1. What educational purposes should the school seek to attain? **(Objectives)**
2. What educational experiences can be provided to attain these purposes? **(Content/Experiences)**
3. How can these educational experiences be organized effectively? **(Organization)**
4. How can we determine whether the purposes are being attained? **(Evaluation)**

**Key features:**
- Objectives-driven — everything flows from clearly stated objectives
- Logical, step-by-step sequence
- Objectives are derived from: needs of society, needs of learners, and subject specialists
- Objectives are filtered through philosophical and psychological screens
- Widely used as a foundation for most curriculum development frameworks

**Strengths:**
- Simple and logical
- Provides clear direction for curriculum planning
- Widely applicable across education levels

**Limitations:**
- Too linear — does not account for the dynamic, interactive nature of curriculum
- Overemphasis on behavioral objectives
- Does not adequately address the role of the teacher or learner in the process

---

### 3.2 Wheeler's Model (1967)

Wheeler's model is a **cyclical model** that builds on Tyler but addresses the limitation of linearity. It has five stages arranged in a cycle:

1. **Aims, goals and objectives** — the intended outcomes
2. **Selection of learning experiences** — activities to achieve the objectives
3. **Selection of content** — subject matter through which experiences are provided
4. **Organization and integration of learning experiences and content** — sequencing and structuring
5. **Evaluation** — assessing whether objectives have been achieved; feeds back into stage 1

**Key features:**
- Cyclical — evaluation feeds back into the objectives stage, allowing continuous improvement
- Separates selection of experiences (stage 2) from selection of content (stage 3), unlike Tyler
- Recognizes curriculum development as an ongoing, iterative process

**Strengths:**
- More dynamic than Tyler's linear model
- Allows for revision and improvement through feedback
- Recognizes the interrelationship between curriculum components

**Limitations:**
- Still largely objectives-driven
- Can be complex to implement in practice

---

### 3.3 KICD Model (Kenya Institute of Curriculum Development)

The KICD model is the **official curriculum development framework used in Kenya** for the formal education system. It is a systematic, phased process:

**Key stages:**
1. **Needs Assessment** — identifying gaps and needs in the education system and society
2. **Curriculum Design** — developing the curriculum framework, syllabuses, and learning materials
3. **Piloting** — testing the curriculum in selected schools before full rollout
4. **Implementation** — rolling out the curriculum nationally with teacher training and resource provision
5. **Monitoring and Evaluation** — continuous assessment of curriculum effectiveness; leads to review and revision

**Key features:**
- Stakeholder involvement at all stages (teachers, parents, employers, government)
- Evidence-based — grounded in research and needs assessment
- Aligned to national goals of education and government policy
- Currently driving the **Competency Based Curriculum (CBC)** in Kenya

---

### 3.4 CBET Curriculum Development Model (TVET CDACC)

Used in the TVET sector in Kenya. The **TVET CDACC** (Curriculum Development, Assessment and Certification Council) was established under the TVET Act No. 29 of 2013, in line with Sessional Paper No. 2 of 2015.

**CDACC mandates:**
- Coordinating development of competency-based curriculum
- Identifying and appointing Sector Skills Advisory Committees (SSACs)
- Overseeing evaluation of curriculum and support materials
- Approving proposals for curriculum development
- Overseeing printing, publishing and dissemination of curricula

**CBET Curriculum Development Process (Three Phases):**

1. **Analysis of tasks and formulation of competences** — occupational profile development
2. **Development of curriculum** based on occupational competences
3. **Facilitation (teaching)** of the acquisition (learning) of these competences

**DACUM (Developing A CurriculUM):**
- A systematic process that analyzes a job by capturing major occupational duties and tasks
- Performed by a focus group of high-performing, incumbent workers through a facilitated workshop

## Topic 4: Curriculum Designs

**Definition (Taba, 1962):**
> *"A statement which identifies the elements of the curriculum, states what their relationships are to each other and indicates the principles of organization and the requirements of that organization for the administrative conditions under which it is to operate."*

---

### 4.1 Subject-Centred Design

Organizes curriculum around **academic disciplines or subjects** (e.g., Mathematics, English, Science).

**Strengths:**
- Easy to organize and administer
- Clear scope and sequence within each subject
- Familiar to teachers and students
- Efficient for transmitting established knowledge

**Weaknesses:**
- Fragmented — subjects taught in isolation with little integration
- May not relate to real-life problems or learner needs
- Teacher-centred; passive learning
- May not develop higher-order thinking or practical skills

**Relevance in CBET:** Limited — CBET requires integration of knowledge, skills and attitudes, which subject-centred design does not naturally support.

---

### 4.2 Broad-Based (Interdisciplinary) Design

Combines related subjects into **broader fields** (e.g., Social Studies combines History, Geography, Civics).

**Strengths:**
- Reduces fragmentation; promotes integration
- More relevant to real-life situations
- Allows for broader coverage of related content
- Encourages critical thinking across disciplines

**Weaknesses:**
- May lack depth in individual subject areas
- Requires teachers with broad knowledge
- Difficult to assess comprehensively

**Relevance in CBET:** Moderate — supports integration of related competencies.

---

### 4.3 Learner-Centred Design

Organizes curriculum around the **needs, interests, and experiences of the learner**.

**Strengths:**
- Highly motivating — learners engage with content relevant to them
- Develops self-directed learning
- Caters for individual differences
- Promotes active participation

**Weaknesses:**
- Difficult to plan and standardize
- May neglect important societal or disciplinary knowledge
- Requires highly skilled, flexible teachers
- Assessment can be challenging

**Relevance in CBET:** High — CBET is fundamentally learner-centred; the learner takes responsibility for their own learning.

---

### 4.4 Activity-Based (Experience) Design

Organizes curriculum around **activities and experiences** rather than subjects. Grew out of the child-centred movement.

**Rationale:** People learn only what they experience; learning rooted in active purpose translates into behavior change.

**Characteristics:**
- Teacher discovers students' interests first; pre-planning is undesirable
- Cooperative (teacher-pupil) planning is essential
- Learners use problem-solving methods and set their own tasks
- Discourages rigid scheduling
- In orthodox method, adult interference is eliminated

**Advantages:**
- High motivation — activities based on student needs and interests
- Students are self-driven
- Provides for individual differences
- Students acquire problem-solving skills needed in society

**Disadvantages:**
- Cannot adequately prepare learners for all aspects of life
- Lacks horizontal structure and continuity/sequence
- Learner needs and interests may not be valid or lasting
- Time-consuming

**Relevance in CBET:** High — CBET emphasizes active, experiential learning linked to real workplace tasks.

---

### 4.5 Core Curriculum Design

Organizes curriculum around a **common, compulsory core** of learning experiences essential for all students, supplemented by electives.

**Characteristics:**
- Promotes integration of learning by unifying subject matter
- Relates learning to life problems and student interests
- Serves the needs of society
- Gives students opportunity to make choices through electives
- Uses problem-solving approach with social and personal problems as content

**Relevance to education:**
- Addresses individual problems of the learner
- Provides for acquisition of basic knowledge
- Provides for a variety of activities
- Combines subject matter with realities and challenges of the day
- Socially rather than cognitively oriented
- Uses broader units, more flexible instructional procedures
- Connects with learner motivation through use of students' experiences

**Relevance in CBET:** Moderate — the concept of core units of competency in CBET mirrors the core curriculum idea.

---

## Topic 5: Occupational Standards

### 5.1 Introduction to CBET Key Concepts

**Skill:** Ability to perform a given task to the standard of performance expected in the workplace.

**Knowledge (Underpinning Knowledge):** The level of knowledge required to understand *why* a skill must be performed in a specific way, how it relates to other skills, and to different and more complex situations.

**Competence:**
- Ability to apply knowledge and skills to achieve intended results
- Associated with the clear ability to successfully carry out occupational activities
- Described in terms of skills, knowledge, and attitudes, as well as the typical context and level

**Competency:** Defined in terms of:
- What a person is required to **do** (performance)
- Under what **conditions** it is to be done
- How **well** it is to be done (standards)

**Four dimensions of competency:**

| Dimension | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Task skills | Skills needed to do the task/job to the required standard |
| Task management skills | Ability to effectively manage a number of inter-related tasks making up the job outcome |
| Contingency management skills | Ability to cope with irregularities and breakdowns in routine within a job or workplace |
| Job/role environment skills | Ability to deal effectively with expectations of the work environment; working with others and under instruction |

**A person is competent when** they are able to consistently apply their knowledge and skills to the standard of performance required in the workplace.

---

### 5.2 Competency-Based Education and Training (CBET)

- An organizational structure for learning/teaching that requires description **in advance** of the knowledge, skills, and attitudes a student must possess upon exit from a program
- Competency-based curricula clearly identify objectives, organize instruction based on performance standards, and evaluate student performance based on **mastery of competencies**
- Links tasks and responsibilities professionals encounter in actual work situations to the required learning outcomes for that specific occupation
- What a professional has to **do** in their work and the competences required are the **leading elements** in curriculum development

**CBET Principles:**
- Actual workplace is leading — what to DO, why and how, is the guiding principle
- Learning outcomes relate to real tasks
- Knowledge, skills and attitudes are integrated
- Students are given major responsibility in their own learning
- Independent learning

**Characteristics of CBT:**
- Training is based on competency standards
- Learning is modular in structure
- Training delivery is individualised and self-paced
- Training is based on work that must be performed
- Training resources are directly related to competency standards
- Assessment is based on collection of evidence of performance to industry standards
- Allows for Recognition of Prior Learning (RPL)
- Based on both on-the-job and off-the-job components
- Allows for multiple entry and exit points; gives credits for units/modules completed

**Advantages of CBT:**
- Focuses on the success of the learner
- Only deals with essential content
- Learner is in control of own learning
- Continuous interaction and feedback
- Learning is carried out in a sequential approach

**Disadvantages of CBT:**
- Requires significant learning resources
- Learning environment should simulate the workplace
- Critics argue it does not suit higher-order learning

**Roles in CBT:**
- **Learner:** Self-directed — takes initiative in what, how, and when they will learn
- **Teacher:** Facilitator — prepares the learning environment; coaches, prompts and helps learners reach their learning goal

**Paradigm Shift — Knowledge-Based vs. Competence-Based:**

| Knowledge-Based Learning | Competence-Based Learning |
|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| Outcomes derived from academic disciplines/theory | Outcomes related to workplace tasks |
| Knowledge, skills, attitudes separate | Knowledge, skills, attitudes integrated |
| Emphasis on theory, application thereafter | Emphasis on doing; theory supports practice |
| Teacher-centred: transferring content | Learner-centred: teacher facilitates |
| Didactics based on instruction/transmission | Didactics based on active learning |

---

### 5.3 Steps of Developing an Occupational Standard

#### Step 1: Training Needs Assessment (TNA)

A TNA is conducted to identify gaps between current and required competencies in an occupation. It establishes:
- What skills and knowledge workers currently have
- What skills and knowledge are required by industry
- The gap that training must address

#### Step 2: Occupation Analysis

**DACUM (Developing A CurriculUM)** is the primary method used:
- A systematic process that analyzes a job by capturing major occupational **duties** and **tasks**
- Conducted by a focus group of high-performing, incumbent workers through a facilitated workshop

**Duty vs. Task:**

| Term | Definition | Role in OS/Curriculum |
|------|------------|----------------------|
| **Duty** | General area of competence that successful workers demonstrate or perform on an ongoing basis | Unit of Competency in the OS and curriculum |
| **Task** | A work activity with a definite beginning and ending; observable; consists of two or more steps; leads to a product, service or decision | Element in the OS and learning outcome in the curriculum |

**How to Write a Duty or Task:**

Formula 1: **Verb + Modifier + Noun**
- e.g., *Manage Program Budget*
- e.g., *Implement Training Program*
- e.g., *Identify Garden Tools*

Formula 2: **Verb + Object + Condition (Context)**
- e.g., *Identify tools for preparing a seedbed in a farm*

**Example:**
- **Duty:** Maintain Personal Automobile
- **Tasks:** Wash car exterior | Vacuum floor mats | Wax car exterior | Check fluid levels | Check tire air pressure

#### Step 3: Occupational Analysis Chart

Used to display duties and their respective tasks in a structured format.

**Sample Format — Occupational Analysis Chart:**

| DUTIES | TASKS |
|--------|-------|
| A | A1, A2, A3, A4, A5, A6 |
| B | B1, B2, B3, B4, B5, B6 |
| C | C1, C2, C3, C4, C5, C6 |
| D | D1, D2, D3, D4, D5, D6 |

**Enablers section includes:**
- Required knowledge (e.g., Principles of management)
- Required skills (e.g., Problem-solving skills)
- Worker behaviour/personal attributes (e.g., Honesty)
- Required resources — tools, equipment, materials and supplies

**Workshop Panel Members:** Expert 1 (Chair), Expert 2 (Secretary), Expert 3, Expert 4, Expert 5

#### Step 4: Task Analysis

A detailed breakdown of each task into the specific steps, knowledge, skills, attitudes, and resources required to perform it competently.

#### Step 5: Packaging Occupational Standards

The duties, tasks, enablers and performance criteria are packaged into a formal **Occupational Standard (OS)** document that specifies:
- Units of competency (duties)
- Elements (tasks)
- Performance criteria
- Required knowledge and skills
- Evidence requirements

---

## Topic 6: CBET Curriculum

### 6.1 Components of a Competency-Based Curriculum

A CBET curriculum is developed directly from the Occupational Standard. Key components include:

- **Qualification title and level**
- **Units of competency** (derived from duties in the OS)
- **Learning outcomes** (derived from tasks/elements in the OS)
- **Performance criteria** — standards against which competence is assessed
- **Required knowledge and skills** — underpinning knowledge and practical skills
- **Assessment methods and evidence requirements**
- **Duration and credit hours**
- **Learning and teaching resources**

---

### 6.2 Guidelines for Packaging a Competency-Based Curriculum

- Curriculum must be directly derived from the Occupational Standard
- Each unit of competency corresponds to a duty in the OS
- Learning outcomes correspond to tasks/elements
- Content must integrate knowledge, skills and attitudes
- Modular structure — each unit can be delivered and assessed independently
- Must allow for multiple entry and exit points
- Must specify both on-the-job and off-the-job training components
- Must include RPL provisions

---

### 6.3 Curriculum Validation

Validation ensures the curriculum accurately reflects industry needs and is fit for purpose:
- Reviewed by industry experts, employers, and practitioners
- Checked against the Occupational Standard for accuracy and completeness
- Piloted in selected institutions before full rollout
- Feedback from pilot used to refine the curriculum

---

### 6.4 Curriculum Evaluation and Revision

Ongoing process to ensure the curriculum remains relevant and effective:
- **Formative evaluation** — during implementation; identifies areas for improvement
- **Summative evaluation** — at end of a cycle; determines overall effectiveness
- Feedback from trainers, learners, and industry informs revision
- Curriculum is updated to reflect changes in technology, industry practices, and national policy

---

### 6.5 Curriculum Maintenance

- Regular review cycles (typically every 3–5 years or when significant industry changes occur)
- Sector Skills Advisory Committees (SSACs) play a key role in flagging when updates are needed
- TVET CDACC oversees the maintenance and dissemination of updated curricula
- Ensures currency and relevance of qualifications in the labour market

---

### 6.6 Implementation of CBET

- Change to CBET does not happen overnight — takes time and commitment from all stakeholders
- TTI (Technical Training Institution) management plays a key role in initiating and supporting the process
- Staff and students need time to adapt to the CBET approach
- Industry involvement is crucial
- A detailed **Implementation Plan** is required

## Topic 7: Curriculum Change and Innovation

### 7.1 Definitions

**Curriculum Change:** Any alteration, modification, or transformation in the content, structure, methods, or goals of a curriculum.

**Curriculum Innovation:** One major type of change in which something **new is added** to an existing curriculum. It involves initiating something new to facilitate the achievement of set goals and objectives. Any innovation should be systematically implemented and can only be justified on the basis of achieving anticipated outcomes.

---

### 7.2 Types of Curriculum Change and Innovation

1. **Substitution** — replacing one element with another (e.g., replacing an outdated topic with a current one)
2. **Alteration** — making minor changes to existing curriculum without replacing it entirely
3. **Addition** — adding new content, subjects, or approaches to the existing curriculum
4. **Restructuring** — reorganizing the entire curriculum structure (e.g., shifting from subject-centred to competency-based)
5. **Value orientation change** — shifting the underlying philosophy or values driving the curriculum (e.g., from knowledge-based to competency-based)

---

### 7.3 Factors Influencing Curriculum Change and Innovation

**i. Social and Demographic Factors**
- The nature of the population has a direct relation to curriculum planning, development and change
- Changes in society are reflected in the school curriculum
- Social problems such as unemployment affect people's way of life — the curriculum is charged with alleviating such problems
- Public institutions, organizations and private agencies may be sources of change or may support/resist change
- Changes in these institutions generally affect the curriculum, directly or indirectly

**ii. Economic Factors**
- The existing economic order and new technology require students to be introduced to new sets of skills
- The state of the economy influences the curriculum
- Economic problems such as poverty and unemployment have forced educational systems to change curricula
- A curriculum not in line with official economic policies may not succeed
- A new curriculum requires more trained personnel, facilities, materials, textbooks, experiments and research — implying an increase in the national education budget

**iii. Political Factors**
- Governments control and finance education — they have significant control over curriculum development
- A good relationship must exist between curriculum developers and the government in power
- The government can either promote or block curriculum projects that do not favour its policies and ideology
- Frequent government changes can delay or terminate curriculum projects
- Curriculum projects should not contradict national policies

**iv. Research and Growth in Knowledge (Epistemology)**
- Research findings greatly affect what and how we teach
- Knowledge is an important source of curriculum content
- Curriculum specialists must update themselves with new knowledge and establish its validity and reliability
- Theories on the nature of the child and how learning takes place must be considered in curriculum development

**v. Technological Factors**
- Technology forces the school curriculum to change as schools try to cope with new developments
- Key questions for curriculum planners:
  - What kind of technology can the school use?
  - How much should schools change in response to technology?
  - What are the implications of these changes on the functions of the school?
- Technological changes may contradict the knowledge and values society wants its youth to have
- May lead to changes in values and norms of society, putting pressure on the curriculum

---

### 7.4 Curriculum Change and Innovation Process

A systematic approach to implementing curriculum change:

1. **Identification of need** — through research, evaluation, stakeholder feedback, or policy direction
2. **Planning** — developing a change proposal with clear objectives, timelines, and resources
3. **Piloting** — testing the change on a small scale before full implementation
4. **Implementation** — rolling out the change with adequate support (training, resources, communication)
5. **Monitoring and Evaluation** — assessing the impact of the change and making adjustments
6. **Institutionalization** — embedding the change into standard practice

---

### 7.5 Criteria for Judging (Accepting or Rejecting) an Innovation

Five basic criteria upon which an innovation can be judged:

**i. Relative Advantage**
- The degree to which an innovation is perceived as being better or more useful determines its acceptability and rate of adoption
- An innovation is considered relevant if it compares favourably with other practices
- Adopters will accept an innovation if it: improves learning, is economically manageable, has low initial cost, and is less risky than existing standards

**ii. Compatibility**
- The degree to which an innovation is seen to agree with or support the potential adopter's needs and values
- How the new idea or practice fits into or supports the existing educational programme
- Compatibility ensures greater security and less risk to the user, and makes the innovation more meaningful

**iii. Triability**
- The extent to which an innovation can be **piloted** without taking too much time, energy or funds
- Adequate data from a small-scale trial enables a decision on whether the innovation is necessary or not

**iv. Observability**
- Stakeholders (potential adopters and sponsors) want to see **tangible results** from their efforts
- They need to know the immediate consequences of the innovation
- Effective accountability requires availability of concrete information

**v. Complexity**
- An innovation must be **simple enough** to be understood and utilized by the user
- Complexity may lead to rejection by potential adopters
- If teachers find an innovation too difficult to understand and implement, they are likely to reject it
