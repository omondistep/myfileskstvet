**Field:** Economics/Business Education | **Sector:** TVET

## 1. RATIONALE FOR STUDYING CURRICULUM DEVELOPMENT

As a trainer-in-training in Economics/Business, understanding curriculum development is essential for the following reasons (Oluoch, 1982; Shiundu & Omulando, 1992):

- Enables correct interpretation and implementation of curriculum programmes
- Equips trainers to participate in KNEC examination panels (setting, moderating, invigilating, marking)
- Enables participation in KICD/KIE subject and course panels — writing books, developing course outlines, preparing teaching aids and evaluation materials
- Enables translation of national goals of education into instructional objectives relevant to Economics/Business
- Curriculum development is a **professional responsibility** of all teachers and trainers
- Helps trainers understand the procedures and strategies of curriculum development in the TVET context

---

## 2. PURPOSE OF THE CURRICULUM

The school/training curriculum serves the following purposes (Ndwiga & Maithya, 2006):

- Translates national goals of education into **concrete, observable behavioural changes**
- Regulates the roles and activities of teachers/trainers
- Specifies objectives that institutions should attain
- Specifies activities that take place in an institution
- Ensures **uniformity** in coverage of activities across institutions
- Regulates evaluation procedures of an education/training system

---

## 3. CHARACTERISTICS/QUALITIES OF A GOOD CURRICULUM

A good curriculum should possess the following qualities (Kelly, 1989; Oluoch, 1982; Shiundu & Omulando, 1992):

1. **Clearly stated objectives** — goals and outcomes are explicit, measurable, and aligned to learner and societal needs
2. **Relevance** — content is meaningful and applicable to real-life situations; for Economics/Business trainers, this means alignment with industry and workplace demands
3. **Balance** — covers all domains (cognitive, affective, psychomotor) and all major areas of human competence
4. **Continuity** — important concepts are revisited and reinforced across levels to deepen understanding
5. **Sequence** — content is arranged from simple to complex, familiar to unfamiliar, building on prior knowledge
6. **Integration** — links content across subject areas to give learners a unified view of knowledge
7. **Flexibility** — adaptable to diverse learner needs, contexts, and emerging societal demands
8. **Comprehensiveness** — covers all required knowledge, skills, and attitudes without unnecessary gaps
9. **Validity** — content reflects current, accurate, and up-to-date knowledge in the field
10. **Learner-centredness** — designed with the learner's needs, interests, and abilities at the centre
11. **Evaluability** — includes clear criteria and mechanisms for assessing learner progress and curriculum effectiveness
12. **Consistency with national philosophy** — aligned to the national goals, values, and policies of the country

## 4. OPERATIONAL DIMENSIONS OF THE CURRICULUM

Dimensions are the **aspects or facets** of a curriculum. There are four dimensions (Kerr, 1968; Shiundu & Omulando, 1992):

| Dimension | Description | Examples |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Formal** | Structured, graded courses of study (syllabi) offered in an institution; planned and timetabled | Economics, Business Studies, Accounting |
| **Non-formal** | Organized, planned out-of-class learning activities; structured but outside the timetable | Business clubs, entrepreneurship fairs, field trips to firms |
| **Informal** | Unstructured, unconscious learning through the day-to-day environment of the institution | Staff conduct, institutional culture, peer interactions |
| **Emerging** | Unplanned learning opportunities that arise spontaneously | Guest speakers, industry visits, prize-giving days |

> **Note for Economics/Business trainers:** All four dimensions contribute to the holistic development of a business professional. Non-formal activities like entrepreneurship clubs and informal learning from industry mentors are especially valuable in TVET.

## 5. SOURCES/GENESIS OF CURRICULUM OBJECTIVES

Before developing a curriculum, a **needs assessment** is conducted to identify what is required. The following are the main sources of curriculum objectives (Tyler, 1949; Oluoch, 1982):

### 5.1 Needs of Society
- National philosophy, ideology, and value systems
- Changing economic, technological, political, and religious life
- Social problems (unemployment, poverty, corruption) that education must address
- For Economics/Business: the need for skilled entrepreneurs, financial literacy, and business management competencies

### 5.2 Learners' Needs and Interests
- Learners' physical and mental abilities
- Individual differences and special needs
- Learners suggest what to teach and what materials enhance learning
- Gap between the learner's current state and the desired societal norm

### 5.3 Academic Specialists / Subject Experts
- Teachers and trainers who interact with learners daily
- Assist in formulating objectives, selecting learning activities, applying child/adult psychology
- Syllabuses produced by KNEC and KICD are the work of subject specialists

### 5.4 Community Sources
- Community advisory groups, parents, vocational groups
- Field trips and industry partnerships as learning resources
- In TVET: employers and industry bodies are key community sources

### 5.5 Psychodynamic Factors
- Biological inheritance, IQ, readiness, maturation
- Individual differences in learning ability
- Age and cognitive development level of learners

## 6. CURRICULUM OBJECTIVES: AIMS, GOALS, AND INSTRUCTIONAL OBJECTIVES

Curriculum objectives are **descriptions of the ends being sought** in educating learners (Tyler, 1949). They are expressed at three levels:

### 6.1 Aims of Education
- **Broad, general statements** of educational intent
- Provide overall direction to the education system
- Written in **amorphous terms**: *learn, know, understand, appreciate* — not directly measurable
- Example: *"To develop a morally upright, patriotic citizen who contributes to national development"*

### 6.2 Goals of Education
- **Broad statements** specified for each **level of schooling** (pre-primary to higher education/TVET)
- Time-bound; stated as expected end-results at each level
- May be in amorphous or more specific behavioural terms
- Example: *"By the end of the TVET programme, the learner should be able to apply business management principles in a workplace setting"*

### 6.3 Instructional Objectives
- Describe an **intended behavioural change** a learner is expected to exhibit after a learning experience
- Expressed in **behavioural terms** using **action verbs** (e.g., *identify, calculate, demonstrate, analyse*)
- Describe an outcome of action — knowledge, skills, or attitudes acquired
- Example: *"By the end of the lesson, the learner should be able to calculate the break-even point of a business given cost and revenue data"*

### Functions of Curriculum Objectives (Oluoch, 1982)
1. Define the direction of educational development
2. Determine the number of levels for the programme
3. Inform learners of standards and expectations
4. Serve as an implicit contract between trainer and learners
5. Drive curriculum planning
6. Guide selection of content, learning experiences, and strategies
7. Serve as a guide in evaluating instructional progress
8. Reflect the needs and aspirations of society

### Different Types of Objectives by Domain (Bloom et al., 1956)
| Domain | Focus | Example (Economics/Business) |
|--------|-------|------------------------------|
| **Cognitive** | Knowledge and intellectual skills | *Analyse the impact of inflation on business costs* |
| **Affective** | Attitudes, values, feelings | *Appreciate the importance of ethical business conduct* |
| **Psychomotor** | Physical/practical skills | *Prepare a business financial statement* |

## 7. SCREENING/EVALUATION OF CURRICULUM OBJECTIVES

Objectives are **screened** to determine if they are relevant, adequate, valid, within scope, and appropriate (Tyler, 1949; Shiundu & Omulando, 1992). Three types of screens are used:

### 7.1 Philosophical Screen
- Values and beliefs held by the nation
- Expressed in the governing political party's manifesto and national philosophy
- Kenya's current values: integrity, transparency, accountability, justice, democracy, hard work
- Objectives must align with these national values

### 7.2 Psychological Screen
- Capabilities and maturational level of learners
- Rate of learning and individual differences
- How learners learn (learning theories)
- Ensures objectives are achievable given learners' cognitive and developmental stage

### 7.3 Sociological Screen
- Cultural values, societal needs, learners' family and religious background
- Knowledge and skills youth need to cope with tasks in society
- Problems prevalent in society that education should address (e.g., unemployment, poverty)
- For Economics/Business: ensures objectives address real business and economic challenges in Kenya

## 8. LEARNING EXPERIENCES

### 8.1 Definition
Tyler (1949) defines a learning experience as *"the interaction between the learner and the external conditions in the environment to which we react."* Learning takes place through the experiences the learner has — through reactions with the environment.

### 8.2 Types of Learning Experiences
Learning experiences/opportunities include:
- **Content/subject matter** — the body of knowledge to be learned
- **The teacher/trainer** — the facilitator of learning
- **Resource persons** — industry experts, guest speakers (especially relevant in Economics/Business)
- **Fellow learners** — peer learning and group work
- **Resource materials** — textbooks, case studies, financial reports, business plans
- **Teaching/training methods** — lectures, demonstrations, simulations, role plays
- **Learning activities** — projects, assignments, workplace attachments, entrepreneurship exercises

### 8.3 Criteria for Selection of Content and Learning Experiences (Tyler, 1949; Oluoch, 1982)

| Criterion | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Conformity with objectives** | Experiences must give learners opportunity to practise the behaviour implied by the objectives |
| **Validity** | Must be consistent with current developments in the subject area |
| **Significance** | Must be meaningful and contribute to the learning process |
| **Learner ability** | Must be adjusted to abilities and individual differences |
| **Interests** | Must be stimulating to maintain motivation |
| **Satisfaction** | Learners must derive satisfaction from the behaviour implied by objectives |
| **Relevance** | Must relate to life outside the institution and solve real problems |
| **Consistency with social reality** | Must not contradict actual situations at home or in society |
| **Economy** | Select experiences likely to bring about multiple learning outcomes |
| **Reinforcement** | Select experiences that reinforce previous experiences |

---

## 9. PRINCIPLES OF CONTENT ORGANISATION

Once learning experiences are selected, they must be organised. The curriculum is usually organised by subjects at three levels: the **lesson**, the **topic**, and the **unit** (Shiundu & Omulando, 1992).

### Principles of Organisation (Tyler, 1949)

| Principle | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Scope** | Breadth and depth of content — what is covered and how much emphasis it receives |
| **Sequence** | Vertical development from one level to another; simple to complex, familiar to unfamiliar |
| **Continuity** | Repeated exposure to important content at different levels to enable high retention |
| **Integration** | Horizontal relationships between different curriculum areas; linking content from one field to another |
| **Balance** | Each educational objective is adequately achieved; develops all major areas of human competence |

---

## 10. PURPOSE OF CURRICULUM ORGANISATION

Curriculum organisation serves the following purposes (Kelly, 1989):
- Ensures logical progression of learning from simple to complex
- Prevents unnecessary repetition while ensuring adequate reinforcement
- Helps learners see connections between different areas of knowledge
- Facilitates effective planning and delivery by trainers
- Ensures all required competencies are covered within the available time
- Supports assessment by providing a clear structure against which progress can be measured

---

## 11. TYPES OF CURRICULUM ORGANISATION

### 11.1 Formative Organisation (Formative Evaluation in Curriculum)
- Occurs **during** the curriculum development and implementation process
- Provides ongoing feedback to improve the curriculum as it is being developed or delivered
- **Benefits:**
  - Identifies weaknesses early before they become entrenched
  - Allows for timely corrections and adjustments
  - Saves resources by preventing large-scale failures
  - Keeps the curriculum responsive to learner and trainer feedback

### 11.2 Summative Organisation (Summative Evaluation in Curriculum)
- Occurs **at the end** of a programme or curriculum cycle
- Provides a comprehensive judgement on the overall effectiveness of the curriculum
- **Benefits:**
  - Facilitates major decisions: continue, expand, modify, or discontinue the programme
  - Provides accountability data for stakeholders and funders
  - Informs the development of the next curriculum cycle
  - Establishes the overall impact on learners and the community

> **Note:** In TVET/CBET, both formative and summative evaluation are integral to the curriculum cycle. Formative evaluation happens at every stage of development and delivery; summative evaluation happens at the end of each qualification cycle.

---

## 12. CURRICULUM EVALUATION

### 12.1 Definition
Evaluation is *"the process of establishing the extent to which the objectives of a programme are being or have been achieved"* (Shiundu & Omulando, 1992). It involves systematic collection of data to facilitate decision-making at various stages of curriculum development.

### 12.2 Types of Evaluation

| Type | When | Purpose |
|------|------|---------|
| **Pre-assessment/Reflective** | At inception | Determines if learners have prerequisite knowledge and skills |
| **Formative** | During implementation | Guides and improves the programme; provides data for ongoing improvement |
| **Summative** | At end of programme | Facilitates major decisions on continuation, expansion, modification, or termination |
| **Impact** | After programme ends | Establishes impact on beneficiaries and the wider community |

### 12.3 Criteria for Curriculum Evaluation (Shiundu & Omulando, 1992)

| Criterion | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Consistency with objectives** | Measures attainment across cognitive, affective, and psychomotor domains |
| **Comprehensiveness** | Covers all objectives, administrative aspects, methods, individual learners, and trainers |
| **Validity** | Instruments measure what they are intended to measure |
| **Reliability** | Instruments give consistent results across different administrations |
| **Continuity** | Evaluation is an ongoing, integral part of curriculum development |
| **Relevance** | Value of the programme in relation to stakeholder needs and national priorities |
| **Efficiency** | Programme uses resources in the most economical manner |
| **Effectiveness** | Activity achieves satisfactory results in relation to stated objectives |
| **Impact** | Results of the intervention — intended/unintended, positive/negative |
| **Sustainability** | Activities and impact likely to continue after external support is withdrawn |

### 12.4 Methods of Evaluation
- Questionnaires
- Interviews
- Tests and examinations
- Observation
- Documentary analysis
- Focused group discussions

## 13. CURRICULUM DEVELOPMENT MODELS

A curriculum development model provides a **systematic framework** for planning, designing, implementing, and evaluating a curriculum (Kelly, 1989).

---

### 13.1 Ralph Tyler's Model (1949) — The Rational/Linear Model

Tyler's model, presented in *Basic Principles of Curriculum and Instruction* (1949), is built on four fundamental questions:

1. What educational purposes should the school seek to attain? → **Objectives**
2. What educational experiences can be provided to attain these purposes? → **Content/Learning Experiences**
3. How can these experiences be organised effectively? → **Organisation**
4. How can we determine whether purposes are being attained? → **Evaluation**

**Key Features:**
- Objectives-driven — everything flows from clearly stated objectives
- Objectives derived from three sources: needs of society, needs of learners, and subject specialists
- Objectives filtered through **philosophical** and **psychological screens**
- Linear, step-by-step sequence

**Strengths:**
- Simple, logical, and easy to follow
- Provides clear direction for curriculum planning
- Widely applicable across education levels and disciplines
- Foundational — most other models build on it

**Weaknesses:**
- Too linear — does not account for the dynamic, interactive nature of curriculum
- Overemphasis on behavioural objectives; neglects process and values
- Does not adequately address the role of the teacher or learner in the process
- Assumes objectives can be clearly defined in advance — not always possible

---

### 13.2 Hilda Taba's Model (1962) — The Grassroots/Inductive Model

Taba (1962) proposed a **seven-step, grassroots model** that reversed the top-down approach. She argued that curriculum should be designed by teachers, starting from the classroom level upward.

**Seven Steps:**
1. **Diagnosis of needs** — teacher identifies learner needs
2. **Formulation of objectives** — specific objectives are set to address identified needs
3. **Selection of content** — subject matter chosen to match objectives
4. **Organisation of content** — content arranged in a logical sequence
5. **Selection of learning experiences** — activities chosen to engage learners with the content
6. **Organisation of learning activities** — activities structured for effective delivery
7. **Evaluation and means of evaluation** — determining what to evaluate and how

**Key Features:**
- Teacher-initiated and teacher-driven (grassroots approach)
- Inductive — moves from specific classroom observations to general curriculum principles
- Emphasises learner needs as the starting point
- Separates content selection (step 3) from experience selection (step 5), unlike Tyler

**Strengths:**
- Gives teachers greater ownership and involvement in curriculum development
- More responsive to actual learner needs
- Recognises the teacher as a curriculum developer, not just an implementer
- Produces curricula that are more contextually relevant

**Weaknesses:**
- Time-consuming and resource-intensive
- Difficult to implement at a national scale — works better at classroom/school level
- May lack consistency across institutions if each teacher develops their own curriculum
- Still largely objectives-driven despite its grassroots nature

---

### 13.3 Kerr's Model (1968) — The Interdependent/Cyclical Model

John Kerr (1968), in *Changing the Curriculum*, proposed a model with **four interrelated components** that influence each other in a cyclical, non-linear way:

1. **Objectives** — distinguished into cognitive, affective, and psychomotor domains
2. **Knowledge** — must be organised, integrated, sequenced, and reinforced; selected for transfer
3. **School learning experiences** — the activities and interactions through which learning occurs
4. **Evaluation** — assesses achievement of objectives and feeds back into all other components

**Key Features:**
- Cyclical and interdependent — all four components interact with and influence each other
- Evaluation feeds back into objectives, knowledge, and learning experiences simultaneously
- Recognises that curriculum components are not isolated but mutually dependent
- Allows flexibility — changes in one component trigger review of others

**Strengths:**
- More realistic than Tyler's linear model — reflects the actual complexity of curriculum work
- Cyclical nature allows for continuous improvement
- Recognises the interdependence of curriculum components
- Evaluation is embedded throughout, not just at the end

**Weaknesses:**
- More complex to implement than Tyler's model
- The interdependence of components can make it difficult to know where to start
- Less prescriptive — may be challenging for inexperienced curriculum developers
- Still largely product/objectives-oriented

---

### 13.4 CBC/KICD Model — Kenya's Competency-Based Curriculum Model

The **Kenya Institute of Curriculum Development (KICD)**, established under the KICD Act No. 4 of 2013, is the official body responsible for curriculum development in Kenya's formal education system. The current model drives the **Competency Based Curriculum (CBC)** introduced in 2017.

**Key Stages of the KICD Curriculum Development Process:**
1. **Needs Assessment** — identifying gaps and needs in the education system and society
2. **Curriculum Design** — developing the curriculum framework, syllabuses, and learning materials
3. **Piloting** — testing the curriculum in selected schools before full rollout
4. **Implementation** — national rollout with teacher training and resource provision
5. **Monitoring and Evaluation** — continuous assessment leading to review and revision

**Key Features:**
- Stakeholder involvement at all stages (teachers, parents, employers, government)
- Evidence-based — grounded in research and needs assessment
- Aligned to national goals of education and government policy
- Competency-based — focuses on what learners can *do*, not just what they *know*
- Currently driving the shift from 8-4-4 to the CBC system in Kenya

**Strengths:**
- Comprehensive and systematic
- Inclusive — involves multiple stakeholders
- Aligned to national development goals (Vision 2030, Big Four Agenda)
- Responsive to labour market needs

**Weaknesses:**
- Implementation has faced challenges: inadequate resources, teacher preparedness, infrastructure gaps
- Piloting phase was criticised for being rushed
- Transition from 8-4-4 has caused confusion among parents and teachers

---

### 13.5 CBET/CDACC Model — Kenya TVET Curriculum Development Model

The **TVET CDACC** (Curriculum Development, Assessment and Certification Council) was established under the TVET Act No. 29 of 2013, in line with Sessional Paper No. 2 of 2015. It governs curriculum development in Kenya's TVET sector.

**Three Phases of CBET Curriculum Development (TVET CDACC):**
1. **Analysis of tasks and formulation of competences** — occupational profile development via DACUM
2. **Development of curriculum** based on occupational competences (from the Occupational Standard)
3. **Facilitation (teaching)** of the acquisition (learning) of these competences

**Key Features:**
- Workplace-led — actual job tasks drive curriculum content
- Integrates knowledge, skills, and attitudes
- Modular structure — each unit of competency can be delivered and assessed independently
- Allows for Recognition of Prior Learning (RPL)
- Multiple entry and exit points

**Strengths:**
- Directly linked to industry needs — highly relevant for Economics/Business trainers
- Ensures graduates are immediately productive in the workplace
- Flexible and responsive to changing industry demands
- Supports lifelong learning through RPL and credit accumulation

**Weaknesses:**
- Requires significant industry involvement — not always easy to secure
- Resource-intensive (workplace simulations, equipment, industry partnerships)
- Critics argue it may not adequately develop higher-order thinking and transferable skills
- Implementation requires a major paradigm shift from knowledge-based to competence-based approaches

## 14. COMPARISON OF CURRICULUM DEVELOPMENT MODELS

### 14.1 Commonalities

| Feature | Tyler | Taba | Kerr | CBC/KICD | CBET/CDACC |
|---------|-------|------|------|----------|------------|
| Objectives/outcomes | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| Content/knowledge | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| Learning experiences | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| Evaluation | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| Needs assessment | Implied | ✓ | Implied | ✓ | ✓ |
| Stakeholder input | Implied | ✓ | Implied | ✓ | ✓ |

All models share the fundamental elements identified by Tyler (1949): objectives, content, learning experiences, and evaluation. All recognise that curriculum development is a purposeful, planned process aimed at bringing about desired changes in learners.

### 14.2 Key Differences

| Dimension | Tyler (1949) | Taba (1962) | Kerr (1968) | CBC/KICD | CBET/CDACC |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|-------------|----------|------------|
| **Structure** | Linear | Sequential (7 steps) | Cyclical/interdependent | Phased/cyclical | Phased (3 phases) |
| **Starting point** | Objectives | Learner needs | Any component | Needs assessment | Occupational analysis |
| **Who drives it** | Curriculum experts | Teachers (grassroots) | Curriculum experts | KICD + stakeholders | Industry + CDACC |
| **Focus** | Objectives/product | Learner needs/process | Interdependence | Competencies | Workplace competencies |
| **Context** | General education | General education | General education | Formal schooling (Kenya) | TVET (Kenya) |
| **Flexibility** | Low | Moderate | High | Moderate | Moderate |

### 14.3 Strengths and Weaknesses Summary

| Model | Key Strength | Key Weakness |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| **Tyler** | Simple, logical, widely applicable | Too linear; overemphasis on behavioural objectives |
| **Taba** | Teacher-driven; responsive to learner needs | Difficult to scale nationally; time-consuming |
| **Kerr** | Realistic; cyclical; components are interdependent | Complex; less prescriptive |
| **CBC/KICD** | Comprehensive; stakeholder-inclusive; nationally aligned | Implementation challenges; resource demands |
| **CBET/CDACC** | Industry-linked; competency-focused; flexible | Resource-intensive; may neglect higher-order skills |

## 15. AGENCIES INVOLVED IN CURRICULUM DEVELOPMENT IN KENYA

Curriculum development in Kenya involves multiple agencies, each with a distinct mandate (Shiundu & Omulando, 1992; KICD Act No. 4, 2013; TVET Act No. 29, 2013):

### 15.1 Kenya Institute of Curriculum Development (KICD)
- Established under **KICD Act No. 4 of 2013** (formerly Kenya Institute of Education — KIE)
- The **primary national body** for curriculum development in formal education (pre-primary to tertiary, excluding universities)
- **Functions:**
  - Advises the government on all matters relating to curriculum development
  - Develops, evaluates, vets, and approves curricula and curriculum support materials
  - Conducts research to inform curriculum development
  - Develops and disseminates curriculum support materials (textbooks, teacher guides)
  - Organises professional development for educators
  - Promotes equitable access to quality educational materials
- Uses a **panel system** comprising practising teachers, teacher educators, university lecturers, quality assurance officers, and curriculum support officers (KICD, 2016)

### 15.2 TVET CDACC (Curriculum Development, Assessment and Certification Council)
- Established under **TVET Act No. 29 of 2013**
- Responsible for CBET curriculum development, assessment, and certification in the TVET sector
- **Functions:**
  - Coordinates development of competency-based curricula for TVET
  - Identifies and appoints Sector Skills Advisory Committees (SSACs)
  - Oversees evaluation of curriculum and support materials
  - Approves proposals for curriculum development
  - Oversees printing, publishing, and dissemination of curricula

### 15.3 Kenya National Examinations Council (KNEC)
- Responsible for the **development, administration, and certification** of national examinations
- Works closely with KICD to ensure examinations align with curriculum objectives
- Involves teachers in setting, moderating, invigilating, and marking examinations
- Provides examination data that informs curriculum review

### 15.4 Teachers Service Commission (TSC)
- Registers, deploys, and manages teachers across Kenya
- Ensures **uniform teaching standards** across institutions
- Provides curriculum support officers who participate in curriculum development panels
- Involved in teacher professional development to support curriculum implementation

### 15.5 Quality Assurance and Standards Officers (QASO)
- Inspect schools and training institutions to ensure curriculum is implemented as intended
- Provide feedback to KICD and CDACC on curriculum implementation challenges
- Ensure standards are maintained across institutions

### 15.6 Ministry of Education / State Department for TVET
- Sets national education policy and provides the legal and policy framework for curriculum development
- Approves major curriculum changes and provides funding
- Aligns curriculum to national development plans (Vision 2030, Big Four Agenda)

### 15.7 Industry and Employers (in TVET)
- Participate in Sector Skills Advisory Committees (SSACs)
- Provide input on occupational standards and competency requirements
- Offer workplace attachment opportunities for learners
- Validate curricula to ensure relevance to the labour market

### 15.8 Parents and Community
- Provide input through school boards and community advisory groups
- Offer community resources for learning (field trips, resource persons)
- Participate in curriculum review through public participation processes

## 16. OTHER CURRICULUM DEVELOPMENT MODELS (BRIEF SUMMARIES)

### 16.1 Backward Design Model (Wiggins & McTighe, 2005)

Developed by Grant Wiggins and Jay McTighe in *Understanding by Design* (1998, revised 2005), backward design reverses the traditional approach to curriculum planning.

**Three Stages:**
1. **Identify desired results** — What should learners know, understand, and be able to do? (Learning outcomes/objectives)
2. **Determine acceptable evidence** — How will you know learners have achieved the outcomes? (Assessment design)
3. **Plan learning experiences and instruction** — What activities, content, and methods will lead learners to the outcomes?

**Key Idea:** Start with the *end* (outcomes and assessment) and work *backward* to design instruction. This ensures alignment between objectives, assessment, and learning activities — known as **constructive alignment**.

**Strengths:**
- Ensures tight alignment between outcomes, assessment, and instruction
- Focuses on deep understanding, not just content coverage
- Highly applicable in TVET/CBET where competency outcomes are pre-defined
- Encourages purposeful, intentional curriculum design

**Weaknesses:**
- Can be rigid if outcomes are too narrowly defined
- Requires significant upfront planning
- May undervalue the process of learning in favour of measurable outcomes

**Relevance to Economics/Business Training:** Particularly useful when designing units where specific competencies (e.g., *prepare a cash flow statement*, *conduct a market analysis*) must be demonstrated — the trainer designs assessment first, then plans instruction to lead learners there.

---

### 16.2 Stenhouse's Process Model (1975)

Lawrence Stenhouse, in *An Introduction to Curriculum Research and Development* (1975), proposed a **process-oriented** alternative to the objectives-driven models of Tyler and Taba.

**Key Idea:** Curriculum should be defined not by predetermined behavioural objectives but by **principles of procedure** — the values, methods, and processes that guide teaching and learning. The curriculum is a *vehicle for teaching and learning*, not a fixed product.

**Key Features:**
- Focuses on **teacher and learner activities** and the conditions in which learning takes place
- Views knowledge as **evolving and open to inquiry**, not fixed
- Teachers are **reflective practitioners** and curriculum researchers
- Emphasises **intrinsic value of learning** — training, instruction, initiation, and induction
- Does not specify predetermined outcomes; instead specifies content, principles of procedure, and standards of evaluation

**Strengths:**
- Promotes critical thinking, inquiry, and deeper understanding
- Gives teachers professional autonomy and encourages reflective practice
- More flexible and responsive to learner interests and emerging knowledge
- Aligns with constructivist learning theories

**Weaknesses:**
- Difficult to implement in systems that require standardised outcomes and accountability
- Evaluation is problematic without clear, pre-specified objectives
- Places very high demands on teacher expertise and professional judgement
- May not suit TVET contexts where specific workplace competencies must be demonstrated

---

### 16.3 ADDIE Model (1975)

Developed by Florida State University's Center for Educational Technology in **1975** for U.S. Army training programmes, ADDIE is a widely used **instructional design framework** in training and development contexts (Lumenlearning, 2019).

**Five Phases:**

| Phase | Key Activities |
|-------|---------------|
| **Analysis** | Identify the performance gap; define learner characteristics, learning environment, goals, and constraints |
| **Design** | Define specific learning objectives, instructional strategies, content structure, and assessment methods |
| **Development** | Create and assemble course content, materials, and resources; stakeholder review and validation |
| **Implementation** | Deliver the training programme; prepare learners and trainers; ensure resources are available |
| **Evaluation** | Assess effectiveness of the programme (both formative — during, and summative — after) |

**Key Features:**
- Systematic and structured — each phase informs the next
- Originally linear but now often applied iteratively
- Widely used in corporate training, military training, and TVET
- Evaluation is embedded throughout (formative) and at the end (summative)

**Strengths:**
- Comprehensive and systematic — covers all aspects of instructional design
- Flexible — can be applied to any subject or training context
- Widely recognised and used in professional training environments
- Aligns well with CBET principles — starts with analysis of needs and competencies

**Weaknesses:**
- Can be time-consuming if applied rigidly
- Original linear version does not allow for iteration between phases
- May be overly prescriptive for complex, dynamic learning environments
- Heavy documentation requirements

**Relevance to Economics/Business Training:** ADDIE is highly applicable in designing training programmes for Economics/Business — the Analysis phase maps directly to Training Needs Assessment (TNA), and the Design phase aligns with CBET curriculum design from Occupational Standards.

## 17. TASK ANALYSIS AND DACUM CHARTS

### 17.1 Task Analysis

Task analysis is a **detailed breakdown of each task** into the specific steps, knowledge, skills, attitudes, and resources required to perform it competently (TVET CDACC, 2016).

**Purpose of Task Analysis:**
- Identifies exactly what a worker must *know*, *do*, and *value* to perform a task to the required standard
- Provides the detailed content for curriculum development
- Informs the development of performance criteria and assessment instruments
- Ensures training is directly linked to workplace requirements

**Steps in Task Analysis:**
1. Identify the task (from the DACUM/Occupational Analysis Chart)
2. Break the task into sequential **steps** (what the worker does, in order)
3. For each step, identify:
   - **Required knowledge** (what the worker must know)
   - **Required skills** (what the worker must be able to do)
   - **Required attitudes/behaviours** (how the worker should conduct themselves)
   - **Tools, equipment, and materials** needed
   - **Performance standards** (how well the task must be done)

**Example (Economics/Business context):**
- **Task:** Prepare a monthly budget for a business unit
  - Step 1: Gather previous period financial data
  - Step 2: Identify income sources and estimate revenues
  - Step 3: List all anticipated expenditures by category
  - Step 4: Calculate net surplus/deficit
  - Step 5: Present budget to management for approval
  - *Knowledge required:* Accounting principles, budget formats, financial analysis
  - *Skills required:* Spreadsheet use, financial calculation, report writing
  - *Attitudes:* Accuracy, attention to detail, confidentiality

---

### 17.2 DACUM (Developing A CurriculUM)

DACUM is a **systematic occupational analysis process** that captures the major duties and tasks that competent workers must perform, as defined by a focus group of high-performing, incumbent workers through a facilitated workshop (Norton, 1997; TVET CDACC, 2016).

**Origins:** First developed at Ohio State University in 1976; widely adopted in TVET systems globally, including Kenya's CDACC framework.

**DACUM Process:**
1. A **facilitator** convenes a panel of **5–8 expert workers** in the occupation
2. The panel identifies all major **duties** (general areas of competence)
3. For each duty, the panel identifies all related **tasks** (specific work activities)
4. The panel also identifies required **knowledge, skills, worker behaviours/attitudes, and tools/equipment**
5. Results are compiled into a **DACUM Chart (Occupational Analysis Chart)**

**Writing Duties and Tasks (TVET CDACC, 2016):**
- Formula 1: **Verb + Modifier + Noun** → e.g., *Manage Programme Budget*
- Formula 2: **Verb + Object + Condition** → e.g., *Identify tools for preparing a seedbed in a farm*

**DACUM Chart Structure:**

| DUTIES | TASKS |
|--------|-------|
| A. [Duty name] | A1, A2, A3, A4, A5 |
| B. [Duty name] | B1, B2, B3, B4, B5 |
| C. [Duty name] | C1, C2, C3, C4, C5 |

**Enablers section includes:**
- Required knowledge (e.g., principles of economics, business law)
- Required skills (e.g., financial analysis, communication)
- Worker behaviours/personal attributes (e.g., integrity, professionalism)
- Required resources — tools, equipment, materials

**DACUM in Economics/Business Training:**
- Duties might include: *Conduct market research*, *Manage business finances*, *Develop a business plan*, *Analyse economic data*
- Tasks under *Manage business finances* might include: *Prepare cash flow statements*, *Reconcile bank accounts*, *Process payroll*, *File tax returns*

**Relationship between DACUM and Curriculum:**

| DACUM/Occupational Standard | Curriculum |
|-----------------------------|------------|
| Duty | Unit of Competency |
| Task/Element | Learning Outcome |
| Performance Criteria | Assessment Criteria |
| Required Knowledge & Skills | Content/Learning Experiences |
