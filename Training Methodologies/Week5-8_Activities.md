## Week 5 — Constructive Alignment and Learning Outcomes (Session 1, ~90 min)

### Why This Matters
The most common failure in TVET training is not poor delivery — it is poor design. A trainer can be engaging and enthusiastic, but if the learning outcomes, activities, and assessments are not aligned, learners will not develop the competence the OS requires. Biggs & Tang (2022) call this *constructive alignment*, and it is the design principle that underpins all CBET planning.

### Background: Constructive Alignment (Biggs & Tang, 2022)

Biggs & Tang (2022) argue that the central problem in education is *misalignment* — when what teachers intend, what they teach, and what they assess are three different things. Their solution:

```
Intended Learning Outcomes (ILOs)
           ↕                    ↕
Teaching & Learning Activities ←→ Assessment Tasks
```

All three must point at the same Bloom's verb level. If your ILO says *design*, your activity must require learners to design something, and your assessment must judge the quality of their design — not just ask them to *describe* what design means.

**In CBET terms:** The OS performance criterion is your ILO. The curriculum learning activity is your TLA. The evidence requirement is your assessment task. Constructive alignment is built into CBET by design — but only if the trainer reads and uses the documents correctly.

> **Critical thinking:** Biggs & Tang (2022) warn that misalignment produces a "hidden curriculum" — learners learn to pass assessments rather than develop genuine competence. Can you identify a real example of this in TVET? What are the consequences for the graduate, the employer, and the institution?

### Task 2 — Writing Strong Learning Outcomes (45 min)

A CBET-compliant learning outcome uses **Mager's format**:
- **Performance** — observable verb (what the learner will *do*)
- **Condition** — the circumstances under which they will do it
- **Standard** — the level of quality required

**Weak:** *Understand the principles of business communication.*
- "Understand" is not observable. You cannot watch someone understand.

**Strong:** *Given a workplace scenario, the trainee will draft a formal business letter that meets all elements of the standard format as specified in the OS performance criteria for Apply Business Communication.*
- Observable (draft a letter), conditioned (given a scenario), standard (meets all OS elements).

**Rewrite these weak outcomes** using Mager's format. Use the Business Manager OS for the standard:

1. *Know the importance of work ethics in the workplace.*
2. *Understand how to apply management skills.*
3. *Be aware of cybersecurity threats.*
4. *Learn about supply chain activities.*

Then write **two original outcomes** for *Perform Sales Activities* (BUS/OS/BM/CR/01/5/MA):
- One at the **Apply** level of Bloom's taxonomy
- One at the **Evaluate** level

**Peer check:** Exchange outcomes with a partner. For each outcome, verify: Is the verb observable? Is the condition specific? Is the standard measurable? If any element is missing, return it with a written suggestion.

## Week 6 — The Scheme of Work (Session 2, ~90 min)

### Why This Matters
A scheme of work is your term-level contract with the OS. It is the document that proves — to TVETA, to your institution, and to yourself — that every performance criterion will be addressed, every domain covered, and every assessment point distributed appropriately. A scheme written after the fact is not a scheme — it is a record. A scheme written before teaching is a *plan*, and planning is a professional obligation.

### Background: What a CBET Scheme Must Contain

| Column | Content | Why it matters |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| Week/Session | Number and date | Accountability and pacing |
| OS Unit & Element | Unit code and element reference | Traceability to the OS |
| Learning Outcome | Specific, measurable (Mager format) | Drives activity and assessment design |
| Key Content | Topics and concepts | Ensures coverage |
| Teaching/Learning Activity | What trainer and learners will do | Ensures active learning |
| Resources | Materials, equipment, references | Preparation and equity |
| Assessment | How competence will be evidenced | Formative checkpoints |
| TVETA Evidence | Type of evidence to be collected | Compliance with TVETA (2020) |

> **Critical thinking:** OECD (2021) argues that teachers/trainers are not just deliverers of content — they are *designers of learning environments*. A scheme of work is a design document. ## Week 7 — Lesson Plans and Session Plans (Session 3, ~90 min)

### Why This Matters
A lesson plan is not a script — it is a design. The difference matters. A trainer who reads from a script cannot respond to what learners actually do. A trainer who has designed a lesson understands the *purpose* of each element and can adapt when the plan meets reality. TVETA requires approved lesson and session plans as portfolio evidence, but the real purpose is to develop your capacity to think like a designer, not a performer.

### Background: TVETA-Compliant Lesson Plan Format

| Section | Content | Design principle |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| **Header** | Institution, trainer, date, class, unit code, topic, duration | Traceability and accountability |
| **Learning outcomes** | 1–3 Mager-format outcomes | Constructive alignment starting point |
| **Resources** | Materials, equipment, references | Equity — every learner needs access |
| **Set induction** | Activates prior knowledge; states purpose (5–10 min) | Kolb's CE stage; Silberman's opening exercise |
| **Development** | Step-by-step activities with timing and trainer/learner roles | Active learning; Kolb's full cycle |
| **Conclusion** | Summary; formative check; link to next session | Kolb's AC stage; metacognition |
| **Assessment** | How you will know learners achieved the outcomes | Constructive alignment; TVETA evidence |
| **Post-lesson reflection** | Completed after delivery | Brookfield's four lenses; CPD |

**OECD (2021) — four design elements to check in every lesson plan:**
1. **Learner agency** — do learners make meaningful choices during the lesson?
2. **Collaboration** — is social learning built in, not added on?
3. **Real-world connection** — are tasks authentic, not artificial?
4. **Metacognition** — are learners prompted to reflect on their own learning process?

## Week 8 — JSS/SSS Planning and Micro-teaching Preparation (Session 4, ~90 min)

### Why This Matters
Many TVET trainers also work in or alongside JSS/SSS contexts under Kenya's CBC framework. Understanding both frameworks — and the differences between them — makes you a more versatile and effective trainer. It also deepens your understanding of CBET by forcing you to compare it with an alternative approach.

### Background: TVET vs JSS/SSS Planning

| Feature | TVET (CBET) | JSS/SSS (CBC/KICD) |
|---------|------------|-------------------|
| Organising framework | Units of Competence / OS | Strands and Sub-strands |
| Outcome language | Performance criteria | Specific Learning Outcomes |
| Assessment basis | Demonstrated competence | Competency-based rubrics |
| Core focus | Occupational skills | Core competencies + values |
| Evidence | Product, process, testimony | Portfolios, projects, observation |
| Reference document | OS + TVETA guidelines | KICD Curriculum Designs ([kicd.go.ke](https://www.kicd.go.ke)) |

**CBC Core Competencies (KICD):** Communication and Collaboration, Critical Thinking and Problem Solving, Creativity and Imagination, Citizenship, Digital Literacy, Learning to Learn, Self-Efficacy.

When developing JSS/SSS lesson plans, explicitly state which core competency is being developed alongside the subject-specific outcome.

### Task 6 — Comparative Planning (45 min)

Take the topic *Business Communication* and develop:
1. A TVET lesson plan extract (CBET/OS format) — header, one learning outcome, one activity, assessment method
2. A JSS lesson plan extract (CBC/KICD format) — header, one specific learning outcome, one activity, one core competency addressed

Then write a short paragraph (~150 words): *What does a trainer need to understand about both frameworks to work effectively in a blended TVET/CBC environment? What is the risk of applying CBET thinking to a CBC context, or vice versa?*
