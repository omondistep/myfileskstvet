## Week 1 — Interpreting Training Standards

### What You Need to Know

TVET in Kenya operates within a **Competency-Based Education and Training (CBET)** framework governed by TVETA. Before you can train anyone, you must be able to read and interpret the documents that define what competence looks like.

#### The Three Core Documents

**1. Occupational Standards (OS)**
- Define the *work* — what a competent worker does in a specific occupation
- Organised into **Units of Competence**, each with:
  - Performance criteria (how well the task must be done)
  - Range statements (the contexts in which it applies)
  - Evidence requirements (what proof of competence looks like)
- The OS in your folder is for *Business Manager, KNQF Level 6* — use it as a live example to practise reading OS documents

**2. Curriculum**
- Translates the OS into a *training programme*
- Organised into **Units of Learning** with learning outcomes, delivery methods, resources, and assessment methods
- The CBET curriculum allows multiple entry and exit points — learners can be assessed on individual units

**3. Assessment Criteria**
- Specify the standard of evidence required to confirm competence
- Linked directly to performance criteria in the OS
- Drive your lesson planning: if you cannot assess it, you have not taught it

#### The CBET Principle (TVETA, 2020)
> Training must be industry-led, outcome-focused, and evidence-based. Certification is granted on demonstrated competence, not seat time.

*Source: TVET Authority (TVETA). (2020). TVET curriculum development, assessment and certification guidelines. [tveta.go.ke](https://www.tveta.go.ke)*

#### UNESCO's Global Context (UNESCO, 2022)
TVET is positioned as a pathway for individuals to *learn, work, and live* — a catalyst for digital and green transformation. Kenya's CBET reforms align with UNESCO's SDG 4 agenda: inclusive, equitable, quality education and lifelong learning.

*Source: UNESCO. (2022). Transforming TVET for successful and just transitions. UNESCO Strategy 2022–2029.*

### CAT 1 Task — Document Analysis Report (10%)

You will analyse an extract of an Occupational Standard and its matching curriculum unit. Your report must:
- Identify the unit of competence and its performance criteria
- Map curriculum learning outcomes to OS performance criteria
- Identify the evidence required and suggest how a trainer would collect it
- Reference TVETA (2020) and UNESCO (2022)

**Evidence to submit:** Written analysis report (annotated OS and curriculum extracts)

## Weeks 2–4 — Facilitating Learning

### What You Need to Know

Facilitation is not the same as teaching by lecture. A facilitator creates conditions for learners to construct knowledge through activity, dialogue, and reflection.

### 2.1 Experiential Learning — Kolb's Cycle

Kolb (2021) argues that learning is a four-stage cycle driven by experience:

```
Concrete Experience (CE)
        ↓
Reflective Observation (RO)
        ↓
Abstract Conceptualisation (AC)
        ↓
Active Experimentation (AE)
        ↓ (back to CE)
```

| Stage | What the learner does | Trainer's role |
|-------|----------------------|----------------|
| CE | Has an experience (does, feels) | Provide the activity/scenario |
| RO | Reflects on what happened | Ask "what did you notice?" |
| AC | Forms a theory or principle | Ask "what does this tell us?" |
| AE | Tests the theory in a new situation | Set a new task or problem |

**Key insight:** Learners have preferred styles (Diverger, Assimilator, Converger, Accommodator). Good facilitation cycles through all four stages so every learner is engaged at some point.

*Source: Kolb, D. A. (2021). Experiential learning: Experience as the source of learning and development (2nd ed.). Pearson Education.*

### 2.2 Active Learning (Silberman & Biech, 2015)

Active training is participant-centred, brain-friendly, and experiential. The core principle:

> It is not what you tell the learner that counts — it is what they take away.

**Active learning techniques relevant to TVET:**
- **Role play and simulation** — learners practise real workplace tasks in a safe environment
- **Case studies** — analyse real or realistic workplace problems
- **Peer teaching** — learners explain concepts to each other (deepens understanding)
- **Problem-based learning (PBL)** — learners tackle an authentic problem before being taught the theory
- **Inquiry-based learning (IBL)** — learners generate questions and investigate answers
- **Project-based learning** — extended tasks that produce a real product or outcome

*Source: Silberman, M. L., & Biech, E. (2015). Active training. Wiley.*

### 2.3 JSS/SSS Learner-Centred Pedagogies

The KICD CBC framework (Junior Secondary School / Senior Secondary School) emphasises:
- **Inquiry-Based Learning (IBL):** Start with a question, not an answer
- **Project-Based Learning (PBL):** Sustained investigation producing a tangible outcome
- **Problem-Based Learning:** Present the problem first; content is learned in service of solving it

These are directly applicable in TVET: a welding trainee who is given a faulty joint to diagnose before being taught the theory is experiencing problem-based learning.

### 2.4 Bloom's Taxonomy & Domains of Learning

**Cognitive domain (Bloom's revised taxonomy — Anderson & Krathwohl):**

| Level | Verb examples | TVET example |
|-------|--------------|--------------|
| Remember | Define, list, recall | Name the stages of Kolb's cycle |
| Understand | Explain, summarise | Explain why active learning improves retention |
| Apply | Demonstrate, use | Deliver a 5-minute micro-lesson |
| Analyse | Compare, differentiate | Compare IBL and PBL approaches |
| Evaluate | Justify, critique | Evaluate a peer's lesson plan |
| Create | Design, develop | Develop a CBET-compliant scheme of work |

**Affective domain** — attitudes, values, motivation (Ajzen's Theory of Planned Behaviour is relevant here — see below)

**Psychomotor domain** — practical skills; critical in TVET (e.g., operating equipment, demonstrating a procedure)

### 2.5 Learner Motivation — Ajzen's Theory of Planned Behaviour

Ajzen (2020) explains that behaviour (including learning behaviour) is predicted by:
1. **Attitude** toward the behaviour — does the learner believe training is worthwhile?
2. **Subjective norms** — do peers and supervisors value the training?
3. **Perceived behavioural control** — does the learner believe they can succeed?

**Practical implication:** If learners are disengaged, diagnose which factor is low. A learner who says "I can't do this" has low perceived control — scaffold the task. A learner who says "this is irrelevant" has a negative attitude — connect content to their real work context.

*Source: Ajzen, I. (2020). The theory of planned behavior: Frequently asked questions. [umass.edu/aizen/tpb.html](https://people.umass.edu/aizen/tpb.html)*

### 2.6 Facilitation Techniques

| Technique | When to use | Watch out for |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| Open questioning | To stimulate thinking | Avoid yes/no questions |
| Think-pair-share | To activate prior knowledge | Give adequate think time |
| Buzz groups | Quick discussion of a point | Keep time-boxed (2–3 min) |
| Fishbowl | Modelling a discussion | Rotate participants in/out |
| Gallery walk | Reviewing group outputs | Provide structured prompts |
| Jigsaw | Teaching different sections | Ensure expert groups are solid first |

### CAT 2 Task — Facilitation Performance (10%)

Deliver a **role-play or simulation** demonstrating a learner-centred methodology. You will be assessed on an observation checklist. Video evidence is required.

**Checklist items typically include:**
- Learners are active, not passive
- Trainer uses open questions
- Activity links to a learning outcome
- Trainer manages time and transitions
- Inclusive practice is evident

**Evidence to submit:** Observation checklist + video recording

## Weeks 5–8 — Planning Training Sessions

### What You Need to Know

CBET planning is backwards by design: start with the competency (what the learner must demonstrate), then design assessment, then design learning activities. This is **constructive alignment** (Biggs & Tang, 2022).

### 3.1 Constructive Alignment (Biggs & Tang, 2022)

The three elements must be aligned:

```
Learning Outcomes  ←→  Teaching/Learning Activities  ←→  Assessment Tasks
```

If your outcome says "demonstrate safe use of equipment" but your activity is a lecture and your assessment is a written test — the system is misaligned.

*Source: Biggs, J., & Tang, C. (2022). Teaching for quality learning at university (5th ed.). Open University Press.*

### 3.2 Writing Learning Outcomes

A well-written learning outcome has three parts (Mager's format):
- **Performance** — what the learner will do (observable verb)
- **Condition** — under what circumstances
- **Standard** — to what level

> *Example:* "Given a set of occupational standards, the trainee will develop a CBET-compliant lesson plan that includes all required elements as per TVETA guidelines."

Use Bloom's verbs. Avoid vague verbs like "understand" or "know" — they are not observable.

### 3.3 The Scheme of Work

A scheme of work is a **term/semester plan** that maps:
- Week-by-week topics
- Learning outcomes per week
- Teaching methods
- Resources needed
- Assessment points

**CBET scheme of work must include:**
- Unit of competence reference (from OS)
- Performance criteria being addressed
- Evidence to be collected
- Assessment method

### 3.4 The Lesson Plan

A lesson plan is a **single session plan**. Standard TVETA-compliant format:

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| Header | Institution, trainer name, date, class, unit, topic, duration |
| Learning outcomes | 1–3 specific, measurable outcomes |
| Resources | Materials, equipment, references |
| Introduction (set induction) | How you will activate prior knowledge and state the purpose |
| Development | Step-by-step activities with timing and trainer/learner roles |
| Conclusion | Summary, check for understanding, link to next session |
| Assessment | How you will know learners have achieved the outcomes |
| Reflection | (Completed after the lesson) What worked, what to change |

### 3.5 Session Plan vs Lesson Plan

| | Lesson Plan | Session Plan |
|--|-------------|--------------|
| Scope | One lesson (40–80 min) | One practical session or workshop |
| Focus | Knowledge/skills delivery | Competency demonstration |
| Assessment | Formative checks | Observation against performance criteria |
| CBET use | Theory units | Practical/workshop units |

### 3.6 JSS/SSS Schemes and Lesson Plans

When planning for Junior or Senior Secondary contexts (CBC), refer to KICD curriculum designs at [kicd.go.ke](https://www.kicd.go.ke). Key differences from TVET:
- Outcomes are framed as **competencies** within strands and sub-strands
- Assessment is **competency-based** with rubrics
- Learner activities emphasise **values and core competencies** (communication, critical thinking, creativity, citizenship, digital literacy, learning to learn, self-efficacy)

### 3.7 OECD — Teachers as Designers (OECD, 2021)

Paniagua & Istance argue that teachers/trainers are not just deliverers of content — they are **designers of learning environments**. Innovative pedagogy requires:
- Designing for learner agency (learners make choices)
- Designing for collaboration (social learning)
- Designing for real-world connection (authentic tasks)
- Designing for reflection (metacognition)

*Source: OECD. (2021). Teachers as designers of learning environments. OECD Publishing.*

### CAT 3 Task — Training Documents Portfolio + Micro-teaching (10%)

**Part A — Documents portfolio:**
Develop a complete set of CBET-compliant training documents for a topic of your choice:
- Scheme of work (minimum 4 weeks)
- Lesson plan (one full lesson)
- Session plan (one practical session)
- At least one learning activity worksheet

**Part B — Micro-teaching:**
In groups of no more than 5, each trainee delivers a **5–10 minute micro-lesson** on video. The video must show:
- A clear learning outcome stated at the start
- An active learning strategy in use
- Evidence of learner engagement
- A closing check for understanding

**Evidence to submit:** Portfolio of documents + video recording

## Week 9 — Managing the Learning Environment

### What You Need to Know

An inclusive, safe learning environment is not just a legal requirement — it is a prerequisite for learning. Learners who feel unsafe, excluded, or disrespected cannot engage cognitively.

### 4.1 Classroom and Workshop Management

**Physical environment:**
- Seating arrangements signal pedagogy (rows = lecture; circles = discussion; clusters = group work)
- Workshop layout must comply with health and safety regulations
- Ensure accessibility for learners with disabilities

**Social environment:**
- Establish ground rules collaboratively in the first session
- Model respect and inclusivity in your language and behaviour
- Address disruptive behaviour promptly and privately where possible

**Time management:**
- Start and end on time
- Use a visible agenda or lesson outline
- Build in transition time between activities

### 4.2 Health & Safety in TVET

TVET workshops carry specific H&S obligations:
- Conduct a **risk assessment** before practical sessions
- Ensure PPE is available and used correctly
- Display emergency procedures visibly
- Maintain equipment in safe working order
- Record any incidents

**Safety compliance records** are a TVETA evidence requirement for this element.

### 4.3 Inclusive Practice

Inclusion means every learner can access and participate in training. Consider:

| Dimension | Practical action |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Language | Use plain language; avoid jargon without explanation |
| Disability | Provide materials in accessible formats; adjust seating |
| Gender | Use gender-neutral examples; ensure equal participation |
| Prior learning | Use RPL (Recognition of Prior Learning) to acknowledge existing competence |
| Digital access | Do not assume all learners have equal device/internet access |

**RPL (Recognition of Prior Learning):** CBET allows learners to be assessed against OS performance criteria based on prior experience, without repeating training they have already mastered.

### 4.4 Digital & Blended Learning Environments

**Salmon's 5-Stage E-Moderating Model (Salmon, 2020):**

| Stage | Learner focus | E-moderator role |
|-------|--------------|-----------------|
| 1. Access & motivation | Getting online, staying motivated | Welcome, technical support |
| 2. Online socialisation | Building online identity | Facilitate introductions, build community |
| 3. Information exchange | Sharing and finding information | Guide searches, summarise threads |
| 4. Knowledge construction | Collaborative discussion and debate | Weave contributions, challenge thinking |
| 5. Development | Self-direction, critical reflection | Step back, encourage autonomy |

**Key principle:** The e-moderator's role is to guide *meaning-making*, not transmit content. As learners progress through the stages, the moderator's control decreases while learner autonomy increases.

*Source: Salmon, G. (2020). E-moderating: The key to teaching and learning online (4th ed.). Routledge.*

**Anderson & Dron's Three Generations of Distance Pedagogy (2020):**

| Generation | Pedagogy | Strength | Limitation |
|------------|----------|----------|------------|
| 1st | Cognitive-behaviourist | Clear objectives, measurable outcomes | Passive learner |
| 2nd | Social constructivist | Collaborative, community-based | Requires interaction infrastructure |
| 3rd | Connectivist | Networked, self-directed | Requires high digital literacy |

**Practical implication:** Effective blended learning draws on all three. Use structured content (1st gen) for foundational knowledge, discussion forums (2nd gen) for application, and open networks (3rd gen) for professional development.

*Source: Anderson, T., & Dron, J. (2020). Three generations of distance education pedagogy. IRRODL, 21(2).*

**UNESCO-UNEVOC Digital Skills Framework (2021):**

Four stages of ICT adoption in TVET:
1. **Emerging** — basic awareness, limited use
2. **Applying** — using digital tools for existing tasks
3. **Infusing** — integrating digital tools into pedagogy
4. **Transforming** — redesigning learning around digital possibilities

Assess where you and your institution are on this scale. Plan professional development accordingly.

*Source: UNESCO-UNEVOC. (2021). Digital skills development in TVET teacher training.*

**Evidence to submit:** Approved lesson/session plans with safety compliance records

## Week 10 — Reflecting on and Improving Practice

### What You Need to Know

Reflective practice is the habit of systematically examining your own teaching to improve it. It is not optional in CBET — TVETA requires evidence of reflection as part of your portfolio.

### 5.1 What is Reflective Practice?

Brookfield (2020) describes four "lenses" through which trainers can examine their practice:

| Lens | What it involves |
|------|-----------------|
| Autobiographical | Your own experience as a learner — what shaped your assumptions? |
| Students' eyes | How do learners experience your training? |
| Colleagues' experience | What do peers observe that you cannot see yourself? |
| Theoretical literature | What do researchers say about what you are doing? |

Using all four lenses produces richer, more honest reflection than simply asking "did it go well?"

*Source: Brookfield, S. D. (2020). The skillful teacher (4th ed.). Jossey-Bass.*

### 5.2 Reflective Models

**Gibbs' Reflective Cycle (commonly used in TVET):**

```
Description → Feelings → Evaluation → Analysis → Conclusion → Action Plan
```

**Kolb's cycle as a reflective tool:**
After a lesson, move through: What happened (CE) → What did I notice (RO) → What principle does this suggest (AC) → What will I do differently (AE)?

### 5.3 Writing a Reflective Journal

A good reflective entry:
- Describes a specific incident (not a general summary)
- Explores your feelings and assumptions honestly
- Analyses what happened using theory (cite your readings)
- Identifies a concrete action for next time

**Avoid:** Descriptive-only entries ("I taught X, then Y happened"). Push to the *why* and *so what*.

### 5.4 Continuous Improvement in CBET

TVETA expects trainers to:
- Maintain a **professional development portfolio**
- Seek and act on **learner feedback**
- Participate in **peer observation**
- Update training documents based on reflection
- Engage with current research and policy (UNESCO, OECD, KICD updates)

### 5.5 Peer Feedback Protocol

When giving feedback on a peer's micro-teaching:
1. **Describe** what you observed (no judgement yet)
2. **Identify** what was effective and why
3. **Suggest** one specific improvement
4. **Connect** to a theoretical principle

**Evidence to submit:** Reflective journal entries + peer feedback records
