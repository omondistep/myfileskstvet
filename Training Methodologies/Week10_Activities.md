## Activity 1 — Brookfield's Four Lenses (30 min)

### Background

Brookfield (2020) argues that teaching is always conducted through assumptions — about what learning is, what learners need, and what good teaching looks like. These assumptions are often invisible to us. Critical reflection means making them visible and examining them honestly.

He proposes four lenses through which to view your own practice:

| Lens | What it involves | Question to ask |
|------|-----------------|----------------|
| **1. Autobiographical** | Your own experience as a learner — what shaped your assumptions about teaching? | *What experiences as a learner made me believe teaching should look the way I do it?* |
| **2. Learners' eyes** | How do your learners actually experience your training? | *What would my learners say about what it feels like to be in my sessions?* |
| **3. Colleagues' experience** | What do peers observe that you cannot see yourself? | *What would a trusted colleague notice about my facilitation that I am blind to?* |
| **4. Theoretical literature** | What do researchers say about what you are doing? | *What does the evidence base say about the effectiveness of my approach?* |

Using only one lens produces a partial picture. Using all four produces honest, actionable insight.

### Task 1a — Autobiographical Lens (10 min)
Write for 5 minutes without stopping in response to:
> *Think of the best teacher or trainer you ever had. What did they do that made them effective? Now think of the worst. What did they do? What assumptions about teaching have you carried from those experiences into your own practice?*

### Task 1b — Learners' Eyes (10 min)
Imagine you are one of your learners sitting in your micro-teaching session. Write a brief account from their perspective:
- What did they experience?
- What was clear? What was confusing?
- Did they feel included and respected?
- What would they wish you had done differently?

### Task 1c — Theoretical Lens (10 min)
Review your micro-teaching video or your memory of it. Identify one moment where your practice aligned with theory and one where it did not. Complete this table:

## Activity 2 — Reflective Writing Models (20 min)

### Gibbs' Reflective Cycle
The most widely used model in professional education:

```
1. Description  →  What happened? (facts only, no judgement)
2. Feelings     →  What were you thinking and feeling?
3. Evaluation   →  What was good and bad about the experience?
4. Analysis     →  Why did things happen the way they did? (use theory here)
5. Conclusion   →  What else could you have done?
6. Action Plan  →  What will you do differently next time?
```

### Kolb as a Reflective Tool
After any lesson, move through:
- **CE** — What happened in the session?
- **RO** — What did I notice about learner engagement, my own behaviour, the environment?
- **AC** — What principle does this suggest? What theory explains it?
- **AE** — What will I do differently in the next session?

### The Difference Between Description and Reflection

| Descriptive (not enough) | Reflective (what is required) |
|--------------------------|-------------------------------|
| "I taught the session on business communication. Most learners participated." | "During the business communication session, I noticed three learners at the back were disengaged. Applying Ajzen's (2020) TPB, I suspect their perceived behavioural control was low — they may have felt the task was beyond them. Next time I will scaffold the activity into smaller steps and check in with quieter learners earlier." |

**Rule:** Every reflective entry must contain at least one reference to theory and one concrete action for next time.

### Task 2 — Write a Reflective Journal Entry (20 min)
Using Gibbs' cycle, write a reflective entry (~300 words) on your micro-teaching experience. Requirements:
- Work through all six stages of Gibbs' cycle
- Reference at least two course readings in the Analysis stage
- End with a specific, actionable improvement plan (not vague — name what you will do, when, and how you will know it worked)

## Activity 3 — Peer Feedback on Micro-teaching (30 min)

### The Feedback Protocol
Effective feedback is specific, evidence-based, and forward-looking. Use this structure:

1. **Describe** — state what you observed (no judgement): *"In the first 3 minutes, you..."*
2. **Strength** — identify what was effective and *why* it was effective (link to theory): *"The role play worked well because it moved learners through Kolb's CE stage..."*
3. **Suggestion** — one specific improvement: *"To strengthen the RO stage, you could ask learners to write down one thing they noticed before the debrief discussion."*
4. **Connect** — link the suggestion to a reading: *"Silberman & Biech (2015) recommend structured reflection prompts immediately after an experiential activity."*

### Task 3 — Peer Feedback Exchange (30 min)
In your micro-teaching group, each member:
1. Shares their self-assessment (2 min) — what they felt went well and what they would change
2. Receives peer feedback from each group member using the protocol above (2 min each)
3. Records the feedback in writing

## Activity 4 — Continuous Improvement Plan (30 min)

### Background — CBET and CPD
TVETA expects trainers to maintain a **professional development portfolio** and engage in continuous improvement. This is not optional — it is a competence requirement. The OS for trainers includes elements on reflective practice and professional development.

Kolb (2021, Ch. 8) frames lifelong learning as *integrative development* — the ongoing process of becoming more flexible, adaptive, and integrated as a learner and professional. He argues that the most effective professionals are those who can move fluidly through all four stages of the learning cycle, not just their preferred style.

### Task 4 — Personal Continuous Improvement Plan

Complete this plan based on your reflections across the entire course:

**Section 1 — Strengths (what you do well)**
List two facilitation or planning strengths you have demonstrated during this course. For each, cite one piece of evidence (e.g., peer feedback, CAT result, self-observation).

**Section 2 — Development areas (what to improve)**
Identify two specific areas for development. For each:
- Describe the gap (what you currently do vs. what you should do)
- Reference the theory that explains why this matters (e.g., Kolb, Biggs & Tang, Brookfield)
- State one concrete action to address it

**Section 3 — Professional reading plan**
From the course reading list, identify two texts you will read more deeply after this course and explain why each is relevant to your development area.

**Section 4 — Accountability**
- Who will you ask to observe your teaching in the next term?
- How will you collect learner feedback systematically?
- When will you review this plan?
