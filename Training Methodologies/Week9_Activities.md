## Activity 1 — Physical and Social Environment Audit (30 min)

### Why This Matters
The learning environment is not neutral. Seating arrangements, lighting, noise, and social climate all affect whether learners can engage cognitively. A learner who feels unsafe, excluded, or disrespected cannot learn — not because they are unwilling, but because the brain's threat-response system actively suppresses the cognitive functions needed for learning. Creating an inclusive environment is not a courtesy — it is a prerequisite for learning.

TVETA requires trainers to maintain safety compliance records for workshop sessions. This is not bureaucracy — it is professional accountability.

### Task 1a — Environment Design (15 min)

You are setting up a training room for 20 TVET trainees for a session on *Apply Management Skills*. The session includes a 15-minute input, a group case study, and a role play.

Describe or sketch:
1. How you would arrange the seating and *why* — what does the arrangement signal about the type of learning expected?
2. What you would display on the walls or board *before* learners arrive
3. Three ground rules you would establish *collaboratively* with the group in the first 5 minutes — and why collaboratively, not unilaterally

**Reference:** Silberman & Biech (2015) recommend arranging the physical environment before participants arrive and using the space to signal the type of learning expected. A room set up in rows signals lecture; a room set up in clusters signals collaboration. The environment is itself a message.

> **Critical thinking:** OECD (2021) argues that the learning environment is a design element, not a backdrop. What does it mean to *design* an environment for learner agency? What physical constraints in typical Kenyan TVET institutions make this difficult — and what low-cost adaptations are possible?

## Activity 2 — Health & Safety in TVET Workshops (20 min)

### Why This Matters
TVET workshops carry specific H&S obligations that classroom trainers do not face. A trainer who ignores these obligations is not just non-compliant — they are placing learners at risk. TVETA requires safety compliance records as evidence for Element 4.

**Before any practical session a trainer must:**
1. Conduct a **risk assessment** — identify hazards, assess likelihood and severity, state control measures
2. Ensure **PPE** is available and correctly used
3. Display **emergency procedures** visibly
4. Maintain **equipment records** (last service date, condition)
5. Record any **incidents** in the institution's incident log

## Activity 3 — Salmon's 5-Stage E-Moderating Model (40 min)

### Why This Matters
Blended and online learning is now a reality in TVET — accelerated by COVID-19 and embedded in this course's own delivery mode. But moving a face-to-face course online without redesigning it produces poor outcomes. Salmon (2020) provides a research-based model for how online learning communities develop and what the e-moderator must do at each stage to support that development.

### Background: The 5-Stage Model (Salmon, 2020)

Salmon developed this model from research at the Open University UK, where over 100,000 students participated in online courses. The model describes how learners progress through five stages in a computer-mediated environment.

| Stage | Learner focus | E-moderator role | If neglected... |
|-------|--------------|-----------------|----------------|
| **1. Access & Motivation** | Getting online; staying motivated | Welcome message; technical support; clear instructions | Learners drop out before engaging |
| **2. Online Socialisation** | Building online identity; forming community | Facilitate introductions; ice-breaker activities | Learners remain isolated; no peer learning occurs |
| **3. Information Exchange** | Sharing resources; finding information | Guide searches; summarise threads; validate sources | Information overload; no synthesis |
| **4. Knowledge Construction** | Collaborative discussion; debating ideas | Weave contributions; pose challenging questions | Surface-level discussion; no depth |
| **5. Development** | Self-direction; critical reflection; autonomy | Step back; encourage learners to lead | Learner dependency on moderator persists |

**Key principle (Salmon, 2020):** The e-moderator's role is to guide *meaning-making*, not transmit content. As learners progress through the stages, moderator control decreases while learner autonomy increases. An e-moderator who remains dominant at Stage 5 has failed — they have created dependency, not development.

> **Critical thinking:** Salmon (2020) developed her model in a UK higher education context with reliable internet access. UNESCO-UNEVOC (2021) data shows that in many low-income countries, fewer than 30% of TVET learners have internet access at home. How does this constrain the application of Salmon's model in the Kenyan TVET context? What adaptations would you make?

### Task 3b — Anderson & Dron: Three Generations of Distance Pedagogy (15 min)

Anderson & Dron (2020) describe three generations of distance education pedagogy. Unlike earlier classifications based on technology, their analysis focuses on the *pedagogy* — the learning design.

| Generation | Pedagogy | Core characteristic | Salmon stage most relevant |
|------------|----------|--------------------|-----------------------------|
| **1st — Cognitive-behaviourist** | Structured content, clear objectives, individual mastery | Locus of control is the teacher/designer | Stages 1–2 |
| **2nd — Social constructivist** | Collaborative discussion, community of inquiry | Learning happens through social interaction | Stages 3–4 |
| **3rd — Connectivist** | Networked, self-directed, open | Learner creates their own learning path | Stage 5 |

Anderson & Dron (2020) argue that high-quality distance education exploits all three generations — not just the most recent. A course that is entirely connectivist may overwhelm learners who need structure. A course that is entirely cognitive-behaviourist produces passive, isolated learners.

**Discussion task:** Your institution uses an LMS with pre-recorded videos and quizzes (1st generation). A learner complains the course feels isolated and irrelevant. Using Anderson & Dron (2020) and Salmon (2020), recommend two specific changes to the course design. For each change, state: what you would add, which generation it represents, and which Salmon stage it supports.

## Activity 4 — Digital Skills: UNESCO-UNEVOC Framework (20 min)

### Background

UNESCO-UNEVOC (2021) identifies four stages of ICT adoption in TVET, based on a global trends mapping study:

| Stage | Description | Trainer behaviour at this stage |
|-------|-------------|--------------------------------|
| **1. Emerging** | Basic awareness; limited use | Uses digital tools occasionally, mostly for administration |
| **2. Applying** | Uses digital tools for existing tasks | Presents slides, shares documents digitally |
| **3. Infusing** | Integrates digital tools into pedagogy | Uses LMS, online discussions, digital assessments |
| **4. Transforming** | Redesigns learning around digital possibilities | Flipped classroom, collaborative online projects, digital portfolios |

UNESCO-UNEVOC (2021) found that the most significant barrier to progression is not access to technology but *confidence and propensity to apply digital skills* — trainers who have the tools but do not use them because they lack confidence or see no pedagogical reason to.

> **Critical thinking:** UNESCO-UNEVOC (2021) data shows that even in OECD countries, only a minority of TVET teachers integrate digital technologies into their teaching at the Infusing or Transforming level. What structural factors — beyond individual confidence — explain this? Consider: workload, institutional support, assessment systems, and the nature of vocational skills themselves.

### Task 4 — Self-Assessment and Planning

1. Honestly assess which stage you are currently at. Give one piece of concrete evidence for your rating.
2. Identify one specific action you will take in the next 4 weeks to move to the next stage.
3. Identify one barrier that might prevent this and how you will address it.
4. Identify one learner in your context who faces a digital access barrier. What would you do to ensure they are not excluded from blended learning activities?
