# Case Studies as an Active Learning Strategy
**Course:** Training Methodologies | **Framework:** CBET | **Subject:** Economics/Business/Marketing

---

## Learning Outcome

By the end of this session, learners will be able to **analyse a real-world business failure using a structured case analysis framework**, identifying root causes, stakeholder impacts, and strategic options — and connect findings to relevant economic and business theory.

---

## What is a Case Study (as an Active Learning Strategy)?

A case study is a real or realistic scenario that learners analyse to apply knowledge, develop critical thinking, and make decisions — rather than passively receiving information.

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Learner-centred | Students do the thinking, not the instructor |
| Context-rich | Learning happens through a realistic situation |
| Discussion-driven | Meaning is constructed through peer dialogue and debate |

> The instructor's role shifts from **telling** to **facilitating**.

---

## Where Case Studies Fit in a Session

| Placement | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Introduction** | Activate prior knowledge, create curiosity | "Here's a problem — let's figure it out as we learn" |
| **Body** | Apply a concept just taught, deepen understanding | "Use what we just covered to analyse this case" |
| **Session Review** | Consolidate and check understanding | "Apply everything from today to this scenario" |

The Toys R Us case is used in the **body/review** phase — learners bring prior knowledge and apply it to a structured analysis.

---

## The Learner Activity — The Collapse of Toys R Us

### How It Connects to the Learning Outcome

The learning outcome requires learners to **analyse, identify, evaluate, and connect** — all higher-order thinking skills. The case activity operationalises this through six structured steps:

| Framework Step | Thinking Skill | Connection to Learning Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Situation | Comprehension | Understand the context and conditions |
| 2. Problem | Analysis | Identify root cause vs. symptoms |
| 3. Stakeholders | Evaluation | Assess who is affected and how |
| 4. Options & Pros/Cons | Critical thinking | Weigh trade-offs between strategic choices |
| 5. Recommendation | Synthesis | Justify a position using evidence |
| 6. Theory Link | Application | Connect case evidence to economic/business theory |

---

## How the Activity Runs (Instructor Demonstration)

**Step 1 — Present the case**
Learners receive *The Collapse of Toys R Us* and read it independently. They highlight key facts, decisions, and turning points.

**Step 2 — Provide the analysis framework**
The six-step framework structures their thinking. It tells them *what to look for* and *how to break it down*.

**Step 3 — Group analysis (groups of 4)**
Learners work through the framework together, completing the Pros & Cons table (Part 3) and the Theory Link section (Part 4).

**Step 4 — Group presentations**
Each group presents their analysis (3–5 minutes). Peers complete the feedback table while listening.

**Step 5 — Individual reflection**
Learners consolidate learning individually (Part 6), answering questions including a South African business context application.

**Step 6 — Instructor debrief**
The instructor facilitates a whole-class discussion, probing responses, surfacing different perspectives, and connecting student answers back to the learning outcome.

> See the full debrief guide below.

## Step 6 — Instructor Debrief Guide

**Purpose:** Consolidate learning, surface gaps, resolve disagreements, and explicitly connect the case back to the learning outcome.

**Time:** 10–15 minutes | **Format:** Whole-class facilitated discussion

---

### Phase 1 — Open the Debrief (2 min)

Do not summarise the case yourself. Start by drawing out what learners found.

> *"Before I say anything — what was the one thing that surprised you most about this case?"*

Allow 2–3 responses. This reactivates engagement and signals that their thinking matters.

---

### Phase 2 — Probe the Problem (3 min)

Target the root cause vs. symptom distinction — the most common area of shallow thinking.

| Prompt | What to listen for |
|---|---|
| *"Several groups identified debt as the problem. Is debt the problem — or a symptom of something else?"* | Learners should surface the LBO decision (2005) as the root cause |
| *"What would have had to be true in 2005 for the debt to not be a problem?"* | Pushes toward strategic foresight — e-commerce growth was already visible |
| *"Group [X] said the Amazon partnership was a mistake. Group [Y] said it made sense at the time. Who is right?"* | Surfaces the difference between hindsight and decision-making under uncertainty |

If groups agree too quickly, introduce a challenge:
> *"I'm going to push back — Amazon was growing fast. Could any amount of debt restructuring have saved Toys R Us?"*

---

### Phase 3 — Stakeholder Perspectives (2 min)

Most groups focus on shareholders and employees. Broaden the lens.

> *"We've heard about employees and investors. What about suppliers — toy manufacturers who depended on Toys R Us shelf space? What happened to them?"*

> *"What about the communities where stores closed — 33,000 jobs gone. Is that a business failure or a social failure?"*

This connects to the stakeholder analysis step and deepens the evaluation skill in the learning outcome.

---

### Phase 4 — Evaluate the Options (3 min)

Groups will have split on Option A (Digital Transformation) vs. Option B (Cost-Cutting). Use the disagreement productively.

> *"Show of hands — who recommended Option A? Who chose Option B? Who said neither would have worked?"*

Then:
> *"To the Option A group — where does the money come from if you're already $5 billion in debt?"*

> *"To the Option B group — if you cut stores and costs, but Amazon still has lower prices and free delivery, what have you actually solved?"*

> *"Is there a third option none of the groups considered?"*

The goal is not to reach consensus — it is to show that **good analysis requires evidence-based justification**, not just a preference.

---

### Phase 5 — Connect to Theory (3 min)

This is where you explicitly close the loop to the learning outcome.

| Theory | Connecting question |
|---|---|
| **Porter's Competitive Forces** | *"Which of Porter's five forces was most responsible for Toys R Us's decline — rivalry, substitutes, or buyer power? Use the case to justify."* |
| **Creative Destruction (Schumpeter)** | *"Schumpeter said innovation destroys old industries to create new ones. Was Toys R Us destroyed by innovation — or by its own decisions?"* |
| **Supply & Demand / Market Forces** | *"Consumer demand shifted online. Did Toys R Us cause that shift, or just fail to respond to it? What's the difference — and why does it matter?"* |
| **Leveraged Buyouts / Financial Strategy** | *"What does this case tell us about the relationship between financial structure and strategic flexibility?"* |
| **Competitive Advantage** | *"Toys R Us had a clear competitive advantage before 2005. Name it. Then explain exactly when and why it disappeared."* |

Do not answer these yourself. Nominate groups, then build on their responses.

---

### Phase 6 — Close and Anchor (2 min)

End by connecting the activity back to the learning outcome explicitly.

> *"Look at what you just did. You read a case, identified a root cause, evaluated options with trade-offs, made a recommendation with evidence, and connected it to theory. That is exactly what the learning outcome asked you to do — and you did it without me lecturing."*

Then ask one final question to anchor the meta-learning:

> *"As a future trainer — when would you use a case study like this in your own session? Introduction, body, or review? Why?"*

This closes the loop: learners are not just economics students — they are trainers learning how to use this strategy themselves.

---

### Debrief Principles (Instructor Reminders)

- **Don't validate too quickly.** Saying "correct" shuts down further thinking. Say *"interesting — does anyone want to add to that?"* instead.
- **Use disagreement.** When groups differ, name it and use it: *"We have two different answers here. Let's work out which one holds up."*
- **Redirect, don't rescue.** If a group is wrong, ask a question that leads them to reconsider rather than correcting them directly.
- **Name the skill, not just the answer.** After a strong response, say: *"Notice what [learner] just did — they separated cause from effect. That's analysis."*

---

## Key Point for Learners

> Notice the instructor did not lecture first. The case pulled you into the content. Your analysis *is* the learning.
