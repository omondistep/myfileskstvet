# Domains of Learning
**Diploma in Technical Trainer Education | Unit Code: ED/B/7101**
**Topic Thread: National Income | Framework: Revised Bloom's Taxonomy (Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001)**

---

## Overview

Learning is not a single, uniform process. Educational psychologists have identified three distinct **domains of learning** — broad categories that describe the different types of outcomes a learner can achieve. Every well-designed lesson should intentionally target at least one domain, and effective CBET training often integrates all three.

The three domains are:

| Domain | Also Known As | Focus |
|--------|--------------|-------|
| **Cognitive** | "Head" / Thinking domain | Knowledge, understanding, and intellectual skills |
| **Affective** | "Heart" / Feeling domain | Attitudes, values, emotions, and dispositions |
| **Psychomotor** | "Hands" / Doing domain | Physical skills, coordination, and practical performance |

> **Key principle for TVET trainers:** A competent worker does not just *know* — they *value* and *do*. Designing learning outcomes across all three domains produces holistic, workplace-ready competence.

---

## Why Domains Matter in CBET

In a Competency-Based Education and Training (CBET) framework, competence is defined as the ability to perform a work role to the standard expected in employment. This means:

- **Cognitive outcomes** ensure the learner understands the *why* and *what*
- **Affective outcomes** ensure the learner *cares* about quality, ethics, and professionalism
- **Psychomotor outcomes** ensure the learner can *perform* the task to the required standard

Occupational Standards (OS) implicitly require all three. When writing learning outcomes, trainers must consciously address each domain to achieve constructive alignment (Biggs & Tang, 2022).

---

## The Revised Bloom's Taxonomy

The original taxonomy was developed by Benjamin Bloom in 1956. It was revised by Anderson & Krathwohl (2001) to:
- Use **action verbs** instead of nouns (e.g., *Remembering* instead of *Knowledge*)
- Swap the top two levels — *Synthesis* became *Creating* and moved to the apex
- Better reflect how learning actually progresses

The revised taxonomy applies primarily to the **cognitive domain** but the principle of hierarchical progression (lower-order to higher-order) has been adapted for the affective and psychomotor domains as well.

# DOMAIN 1: COGNITIVE DOMAIN

**Focus:** Intellectual skills — what the learner *knows*, *understands*, and can *think through*

The cognitive domain is organised into **six hierarchical levels**, moving from simple recall at the base to complex creation at the apex. Lower levels are prerequisites for higher levels.

```
        ┌─────────────┐
        │   CREATING   │  ← Highest Order
        ├─────────────┤
        │  EVALUATING  │
        ├─────────────┤
        │  ANALYSING   │
        ├─────────────┤
        │  APPLYING    │
        ├─────────────┤
        │ UNDERSTANDING│
        ├─────────────┤
        │ REMEMBERING  │  ← Lowest Order
        └─────────────┘
```

# DOMAIN 2: AFFECTIVE DOMAIN

**Focus:** Attitudes, values, emotions, and dispositions — what the learner *feels*, *values*, and *is committed to*

The affective domain was developed by Krathwohl, Bloom, and Masia (1964) and describes how learners internalise values and adjust their behaviour accordingly. It is organised into **five hierarchical levels**, moving from passive awareness to deep personal commitment.

```
        ┌──────────────────┐
        │  CHARACTERISING  │  ← Highest Order (value becomes part of identity)
        ├──────────────────┤
        │   ORGANISING     │
        ├──────────────────┤
        │    VALUING       │
        ├──────────────────┤
        │   RESPONDING     │
        ├──────────────────┤
        │   RECEIVING      │  ← Lowest Order
        └──────────────────┘
```

> **Note for TVET trainers:** Affective outcomes are often the hardest to assess but are critical for professional practice. A trainer who values accuracy, a technician who takes pride in quality work, or an economist who is committed to ethical data use — these are affective outcomes.

# DOMAIN 3: PSYCHOMOTOR DOMAIN

**Focus:** Physical and practical skills — what the learner can *do* with their hands, body, and tools

The psychomotor domain covers the development of motor skills, coordination, and the ability to perform physical tasks. Bloom's team never formally completed the psychomotor handbook; the taxonomy most closely aligned with the Bloom's framework — and most widely referenced alongside the revised cognitive and affective domains — is **Simpson's Taxonomy (1972)**, which organises psychomotor learning into **seven hierarchical levels**.

> **Relevance to Economics/National Income:** While the psychomotor domain is most prominent in technical and vocational subjects (e.g., welding, nursing), it applies in economics and business training through skills such as constructing graphs, operating statistical software, presenting data, and using spreadsheet tools — all of which require physical coordination and procedural skill.

```
        ┌──────────────────────────┐
        │       ORIGINATION        │  ← Highest Order (creative, expert performance)
        ├──────────────────────────┤
        │       ADAPTATION         │
        ├──────────────────────────┤
        │  COMPLEX OVERT RESPONSE  │
        ├──────────────────────────┤
        │       MECHANISM          │
        ├──────────────────────────┤
        │     GUIDED RESPONSE      │
        ├──────────────────────────┤
        │           SET            │
        ├──────────────────────────┤
        │       PERCEPTION         │  ← Lowest Order
        └──────────────────────────┘
```

---

By the end of the unit, the learner will **accurately and fluently construct** a fully labelled circular flow of income diagram — including all leakages (savings, taxes, imports) and injections (investment, government spending, exports) — **without reference to notes**, achieving a minimum score of 80% on a diagram accuracy checklist under timed conditions (15 minutes).

**LO 5.2**
By the end of the unit, the learner will **precisely and smoothly execute** the full process of calculating real GDP from nominal GDP using a price deflator — including formula setup, substitution, and interpretation — **producing** a correctly formatted solution with all workings shown, achieving 100% accuracy on a structured task completed independently within 20 minutes.

### SMART Learning Outcomes — Inflation

**LO 5.3**
By the end of the unit, the learner will **accurately and fluently construct** a fully labelled cost-push inflation diagram — showing a leftward shift of the AS curve with correctly labelled axes, original and new equilibrium points, and annotated price and output effects — **without reference to notes**, achieving a minimum score of 80% on a diagram accuracy checklist under timed conditions (15 minutes).

**LO 5.4**
By the end of the unit, the learner will **precisely and smoothly execute** the full process of calculating the real interest rate from nominal interest rate and inflation data using the Fisher equation (r ≈ i − π), **producing** a correctly formatted solution with all workings shown and achieving 100% accuracy on a structured task completed independently within 20 minutes.

**Definition:** The learner can modify and adjust their skills to meet the demands of new, non-standard, or unexpected situations. Performance is flexible and problem-solving oriented.

**Key Verbs:**
Adapt, Modify, Adjust, Revise, Customise, Reorganise, Alter, Rearrange, Respond to change, Solve (practical problem)

By the end of the unit, the learner will **adapt** a standard national income data presentation to suit two different audiences — a government policy brief and a community awareness poster — by **modifying** the format, language, scale, and visual representation accordingly, with both outputs assessed against audience-specific rubrics.

**LO 6.2**

# Comparative Summary Table

| Domain | Levels (Low → High) | Core Question | Assessment Methods |
|--------|---------------------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Cognitive** | Remembering → Understanding → Applying → Analysing → Evaluating → Creating | What does the learner *know and think*? | Tests, essays, calculations, case studies, projects |
| **Affective** | Receiving → Responding → Valuing → Organising → Characterising | What does the learner *feel and value*? | Observation, participation records, reflective journals, peer feedback |
| **Psychomotor** | Perception → Set → Guided Response → Mechanism → Complex Overt Response → Adaptation → Origination | What can the learner *do and perform*? | Practical tasks, demonstrations, timed exercises, portfolios |

# Writing SMART Learning Outcomes — A Checklist

A SMART learning outcome must be:

| Criterion | Question to ask | Example check |
|-----------|----------------|---------------|
| **S**pecific | Is the behaviour clearly described? | "calculate GDP using the expenditure approach" ✓ vs "understand GDP" ✗ |
| **M**easurable | Can achievement be observed or measured? | "achieving 70% on a calculation exercise" ✓ |
| **A**chievable | Is it realistic for the learner at this level? | Appropriate to the learner's current knowledge ✓ |
| **R**elevant | Does it relate to the unit/competency? | Linked to national income topic and OS ✓ |
| **T**ime-bound | Is there a timeframe? | "within 20 minutes" / "by the end of the lesson" ✓ |

**Mager's Format (recommended for CBET):**

> *[Condition] + [Performance verb] + [Standard]*

**Example:**
> *"Given a set of national accounts data (condition), the learner will calculate Kenya's GDP using the expenditure approach (performance), achieving a minimum score of 70% on the structured exercise (standard)."*

# Alignment of Domains with CBET Competency Framework

In CBET, a unit of competence requires evidence across all three domains:

| CBET Evidence Type | Domain | Example — National Income |
|-------------------|--------|--------------------------|
| Knowledge evidence | Cognitive | Written test on GDP measurement methods |
| Performance evidence | Psychomotor | Constructing a national income data chart |
| Attitude/values evidence | Affective | Reflective journal on ethical use of economic data |

> **Trainer tip:** When designing your scheme of work and lesson plans, map each learning outcome to a domain and a Bloom's level. This ensures you are not over-relying on lower-order cognitive outcomes (remembering and understanding) at the expense of higher-order thinking and practical skill development.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Why it's a problem | Correction |
|---------|-------------------|------------|
| Using "understand" or "know" as outcome verbs | Not observable or measurable | Replace with "explain", "describe", "calculate" |
| Writing outcomes only at the Remembering level | Produces surface learning, not competence | Include Apply, Analyse, and Create levels |
| Ignoring the affective domain | Produces technically skilled but unprofessional graduates | Include valuing and commitment outcomes |
| Ignoring the psychomotor domain in economics | Misses practical data skills | Include Excel, graphing, and presentation skills |
| Outcomes that cannot be assessed | Undermines constructive alignment | Every outcome must have a matching assessment method |
