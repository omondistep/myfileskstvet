## Week 2, Activity 1 — Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle (80 min)

### Why This Matters
Most trainee trainers were educated through lecture. Kolb (2021) provides the theoretical foundation for why that is insufficient and what to do instead. His Experiential Learning Theory (ELT) is not abstract — it is a practical framework for designing every session you will deliver. Understanding it deeply is the difference between a trainer who follows a plan mechanically and one who can read a room and adapt.

### Background: Three Foundations of ELT

Kolb (2021, Ch. 1) built his theory on three scholars:

**John Dewey** argued that genuine education requires *continuity of experience* — each experience builds on the last. Learning disconnected from experience is inert and cannot be applied. For TVET, training must connect to the learner's real work context.

**Kurt Lewin** contributed the action research cycle: plan → act → observe → reflect. He showed that learning is fundamentally social — it happens in interaction between people, not just inside individual minds. His insight that "there is nothing so practical as a good theory" is directly relevant to TVET: practice without theory is blind.

**Jean Piaget** showed that cognitive development is active construction, not passive reception. Learners build knowledge by acting on the world and adjusting their mental models when reality surprises them. A learner who has never handled a real sales interaction cannot learn sales skills from a textbook alone.

Kolb (2021) synthesised these into one definition: **learning is the process whereby knowledge is created through the transformation of experience.** The word *transformation* is critical — experience alone does not produce learning. It must be reflected upon, conceptualised, and tested.

### The Four-Stage Cycle

| Stage | What the learner does | Trainer's move | Key question |
|-------|-----------------------|----------------|-------------|
| **CE — Feeling** | Has a direct, concrete experience | Provide the activity or task | *(none — let them experience first)* |
| **RO — Watching** | Steps back; observes what happened | Ask open, non-leading questions | *What did you notice? What happened?* |
| **AC — Thinking** | Forms a principle or theory | Prompt generalisation; introduce theory | *What does this tell us? What rule can we draw?* |
| **AE — Doing** | Tests the new idea in a new situation | Set a follow-up task | *How would you apply this next time?* |

Kolb (2021) describes the cycle as a **spiral** — each pass deepens understanding. A single cycle in one lesson is not enough; units must be designed so learners revisit the cycle across multiple sessions.

> **Critical thinking:** If the cycle is a spiral, what does this mean for how you plan a 4-week unit? Is it enough to go through the cycle once in Week 1 and move on?

### Task 1 — Experience the Cycle: Tower Challenge (60 min)

This activity is designed so you *experience* Kolb's cycle before you analyse it. Pay attention to your own learning process as it happens.

**CE — The Tower Challenge (15 min)**

In groups of 4–5, using only 20 sheets of A4 paper, 50 cm of tape, and scissors, build the tallest freestanding tower possible in 10 minutes. Rules: the tower must stand unsupported for 10 seconds; no other materials; no technique instructions given.

*Do not explain the purpose of this activity beforehand. The experience must come first.*

**RO — Structured Debrief (15 min)**

Each group discusses — appoint a note-taker:
1. What happened in your group? Describe the sequence of events.
2. Who took on which roles? Did this happen by agreement or by default?
3. What frustrated you? What surprised you? What worked better than expected?
4. At what point did your approach change? What triggered the change?

**AC — Theory Building (15 min)**

Whole group: What principles about group learning, problem-solving, and facilitation can we draw from this experience? Facilitator records on the board.

Then introduce Kolb's cycle explicitly. Ask: *Where in the cycle were you during the tower challenge? Where were you during the debrief?*

Connect to Silberman & Biech (2015, p. 1): "It is not what you tell the learner that counts — it is what they take away." The tower challenge produced learning not because the facilitator explained anything, but because learners *did* something and then *reflected* on it.

**AE — Transfer (15 min)**

Individually write:
1. How would you apply one principle from this experience when facilitating TVET trainees in a workshop?
2. Design a 5-minute opening activity for *Apply Work Ethics and Practices* that uses Kolb's CE stage. What would learners do? What experience would you create?

Share with a partner. Give feedback: *What I like about your idea is... One thing that would strengthen it is...*

### Task 2 — Learning Styles: Implications for Facilitation (20 min)

Kolb (2021, Ch. 4) identifies four learning styles based on preferred entry points into the cycle. These are tendencies, not fixed types.

| Style | Preferred stages | Strength | TVET implication |
|-------|-----------------|----------|-----------------|
| **Diverger** | CE + RO | Imaginative; sees many perspectives | Thrives in brainstorming, role play, open discussion |
| **Assimilator** | RO + AC | Logical; builds theoretical models | Thrives in structured input, reading, analysis |
| **Converger** | AC + AE | Practical problem-solver | Thrives in technical tasks, simulations, experiments |
| **Accommodator** | AE + CE | Action-oriented; adapts quickly | Thrives in projects, fieldwork, hands-on practice |

> **Critical thinking:** In TVET, the psychomotor domain is central. Does this mean training should favour Converger and Accommodator styles? What would be lost if Diverger and Assimilator learners were not catered for? Reference Kolb (2021) in your answer.

**Discussion task:** In pairs — which style best describes you? Then answer: *If your training group contains all four styles, what must you include in a single 60-minute lesson to ensure every learner is engaged at some point?*

## Week 3, Activity 2 — Active Learning Techniques (90 min)

### Why This Matters
Knowing Kolb's theory is not enough — you need a practical repertoire of techniques to create the CE stage and facilitate RO and AC. Silberman & Biech (2015) provide this toolkit grounded in adult learning theory. The key principle: adult learners bring experience, have immediate needs, and learn best when they are active participants, not passive recipients.

When learners are passive, they process information at a surface level. When they must retrieve, apply, reorganise, or explain — they process more deeply and retain longer. In TVET, where the goal is *performance* not recall, this distinction is critical.

### Six Experiential Approaches (Silberman & Biech, 2015, Ch. 6)

| Technique | Description | TVET application | Kolb stage activated |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Role Play** | Learners act out realistic scenarios | Sales interaction, disciplinary conversation, customer complaint | CE → RO |
| **Simulation** | Structured imitation of a real process | Mock procurement process, business risk event | CE → AC |
| **Observation** | Learners watch and analyse a demonstration | Watching a skilled worker, identifying what made it effective | RO → AC |
| **Action Learning** | Groups tackle a real problem from their own context | Analysing a real workplace challenge from a trainee's employer | AE → CE |
| **Case Study** | Learners analyse a written scenario and make decisions | Reviewing a business communication failure, proposing solutions | AC → AE |
| **Writing Tasks** | Structured writing to consolidate learning | Reflective journals, one-minute papers, learning logs | RO → AC |

> **Critical thinking:** Silberman & Biech (2015) note that trainers often resist active learning because it feels "less controlled" than lecturing. What are the legitimate concerns behind this resistance? Is there a place for direct instruction in CBET training? When is a lecture the *right* choice?

### Task 3 — Technique Carousel (60 min)

Four stations. Groups of 4–5 rotate every 12 minutes. Leave written responses at each station for the next group to build on.

**Station A — Role Play: Facilitation Under Pressure**

*Scenario:* A trainer is delivering *Apply Work Ethics and Practices* (BUS/OS/BM/BC/03/5/MA). Learner Kamau arrives 20 minutes late for the third consecutive session. When addressed, he says: "I have a job. I come when I can. Just teach me what I need to pass."

*Tasks:*
1. Role-play the trainer's response (5 min). One person plays the trainer, one plays Kamau, others observe.
2. Debrief: What did the trainer do well? What would you change?
3. Apply Ajzen (2020): which TPB factor (attitude, subjective norms, perceived control) is most likely driving Kamau's behaviour? What facilitation response does that suggest?
4. Write your group's recommended approach on the station sheet.

**Station B — Case Study: When Assessment Fails**

*Scenario:*
> A TVET institution delivers *Perform Sales Activities* (BUS/OS/BM/CR/01/5/MA) entirely by PowerPoint lecture. Learners complete a 50-question multiple-choice test. 80% pass the written test. When assessed on the practical component — conducting a simulated sales interaction — 65% fail.

*Questions:*
1. What is the root cause? Use Kolb (2021) and Silberman & Biech (2015) to explain.
2. What does Biggs & Tang's (2022) constructive alignment tell us about what went wrong?
3. Redesign the assessment approach. What TVETA (2020) evidence types would you use?
4. What does this case tell us about the relationship between teaching method and assessment method?

---

**Station C — Simulation Design**

Design a 10-minute simulation for *Perform Sales Activities*. Specify:
1. The scenario (what situation are learners simulating?)
2. Learner roles
3. What the learner must *do* — link to at least two OS performance criteria
4. Debrief questions at each Kolb stage
5. What TVETA evidence this simulation generates

> *Challenge:* Silberman & Biech (2015) warn simulations can feel artificial if poorly designed. What would make this simulation feel authentic and relevant?

---

**Station D — Action Learning**

Action learning uses real problems from participants' own contexts as the learning material — powerful in TVET because learners are often working adults with genuine workplace challenges.

1. Each member briefly describes one real challenge from their training or workplace context (1 min each).
2. Group selects the most complex challenge.
3. Spend 8 minutes generating solutions. The person whose challenge it is *listens only* — no defending or explaining.
4. Record: the challenge, solutions proposed, one action each member commits to.

> *Reflection:* How does action learning connect to Kolb's (2021) argument that learning is a transaction between person and environment? What makes it different from a case study?

### Task 4 — Learner-Centred Pedagogies: IBL, PBL, and Problem-Based Learning (30 min)

### Why This Matters
The course outline requires trainers to apply three specific learner-centred pedagogies used in JSS/SSS CBC contexts and increasingly in TVET: Inquiry-Based Learning (IBL), Project-Based Learning (PBL), and Problem-Based Learning. These are not interchangeable labels for "active learning" — each has a distinct structure, a different entry point, and different implications for how you design a session. Confusing them produces poorly designed activities that look learner-centred but are not.

### The Three Pedagogies Compared

| Pedagogy | Starting point | Learner's role | Trainer's role | Typical duration | TVET example |
|----------|---------------|---------------|----------------|-----------------|-------------|
| **Inquiry-Based Learning (IBL)** | A question the learner generates or is given | Investigates, gathers evidence, draws conclusions | Facilitates the inquiry; does not give the answer | Single session to multi-week | *"Why do some businesses fail in their first year? Investigate three real cases and identify common factors."* |
| **Project-Based Learning (PBL)** | A real-world task or product to create | Plans, produces, and presents a tangible outcome | Coaches; provides checkpoints; assesses the product | Multi-week | *"Develop a complete marketing plan for a small business in your community."* |
| **Problem-Based Learning** | A messy, authentic problem presented before any instruction | Identifies what they need to know; researches; proposes a solution | Presents the problem; withholds the answer; guides the process | Single session to multi-week | *"A business manager has received three customer complaints about staff communication this week. What is causing this and what should she do?"* |

**Key distinction:** In IBL the learner asks the question. In Problem-Based Learning the problem is given but the solution is unknown. In PBL the outcome is a product. All three require the learner to *do* something before being told the answer — this is what makes them learner-centred.

**Connection to Kolb (2021):** All three pedagogies begin at the CE stage — the learner encounters a question, problem, or task before receiving theory. The theory (AC stage) arrives *after* the learner has struggled with the experience, which is why it is retained and applied more effectively than theory delivered first.

> **Critical thinking:** The KICD CBC framework mandates IBL and PBL in JSS/SSS. TVETA's CBET framework does not use these terms explicitly — but the OS evidence requirements (product, process, testimony) map naturally onto PBL and Problem-Based Learning outcomes. What does this tell us about the relationship between the two frameworks? Is a TVET trainer who understands IBL/PBL better equipped to design CBET training? Why or why not?

### Task 4a — Identify the Pedagogy (10 min)

For each scenario, identify which pedagogy is being used (IBL, PBL, or Problem-Based Learning) and explain your reasoning in one sentence.

1. Trainees are given a scenario: *"A new employee has been caught falsifying attendance records. As the manager, what do you do?"* They must propose a solution before any input on HR procedures is given.
2. Trainees spend three weeks developing a full business plan for a fictional SME, which they present to a panel at the end of the unit.
3. Trainees are asked: *"What makes a sales pitch effective?"* They watch three real sales videos, identify patterns, and construct their own answer before the trainer introduces any theory.

### Task 5 — Bloom's Taxonomy Across Three Domains (30 min)

A common mistake is planning only for the cognitive domain and neglecting affective and psychomotor — which are often the most important in vocational training.

**Cognitive domain** (Anderson & Krathwohl — revised Bloom's):

| Level | Verbs | Example: *Apply Management Skills* |
|-------|-------|-------------------------------------|
| Remember | Define, list, recall | List the four functions of management |
| Understand | Explain, summarise | Explain the difference between leadership and management |
| Apply | Demonstrate, use | Apply a SWOT analysis to a given business scenario |
| Analyse | Compare, differentiate | Compare two management styles and their effects on staff |
| Evaluate | Justify, critique | Evaluate the effectiveness of a management decision using evidence |
| Create | Design, develop | Develop a management plan for a new department |

**Affective domain** (Krathwohl et al. — attitudes and values):

| Level | Description | TVET example |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| Receiving | Aware of and willing to attend to the value | Listens to the importance of work ethics |
| Responding | Actively participates | Contributes to discussions on ethical dilemmas |
| Valuing | Shows consistent commitment | Arrives on time; meets deadlines without prompting |
| Organising | Integrates value into personal system | Prioritises ethical behaviour even under pressure |
| Characterising | Value is part of identity | Advocates for ethical practice with colleagues |

**Psychomotor domain** (Simpson — critical in TVET):

| Level | Description | TVET example |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| Imitation | Copies a demonstrated procedure | Follows a sales script under supervision |
| Manipulation | Performs from memory | Conducts a sales interaction without a script |
| Precision | Performs accurately and efficiently | Meets all OS performance criteria consistently |
| Articulation | Adapts skill to new situations | Adjusts approach for different customer types |
| Naturalisation | Performs fluently without conscious thought | Handles complex situations with confidence |

> **Critical thinking:** Most TVET assessments focus on cognitive (written tests) and lower psychomotor levels (imitation). What would it take to assess at Evaluate/Create cognitively and Articulation/Naturalisation psychomotorally? What are the resource implications for a typical TVET institution?

**Writing task:** For *Apply Digital Literacy* (BUS/OS/BM/BC/01/6/MA), write one learning outcome at each level below using Mager's format (Performance + Condition + Standard):
1. Cognitive — Apply level
2. Cognitive — Evaluate level
3. Affective — Valuing level
4. Psychomotor — Precision level

## Week 4, Activity 3 — Learner Motivation and Facilitation Practice (90 min)

### Why This Matters
Even the best-designed lesson fails if learners are not motivated to engage. Motivation is not a fixed trait — it is a dynamic state trainers can influence. Ajzen's (2020) Theory of Planned Behaviour gives you a diagnostic framework: when a learner is disengaged, you can identify *why* and respond specifically, rather than applying a generic "motivational" intervention that misses the actual problem.

### Background: Ajzen's Theory of Planned Behaviour (2020)

Ajzen (2020) proposes that the best predictor of behaviour is *intention*, shaped by three factors:

```
Attitude toward the behaviour ("Is this worthwhile?")
        +
Subjective norms ("Do people I respect value this?")    →  Intention  →  Behaviour
        +
Perceived behavioural control ("Can I actually do this?")
```

**Applied to TVET facilitation:**

| Factor | Low = learner shows... | Trainer's diagnostic question | Facilitation response |
|--------|----------------------|------------------------------|----------------------|
| **Attitude** | "This is irrelevant to my job" | Does the learner see the connection to their real work? | Use their workplace as the case study; show how the OS unit maps to their job role |
| **Subjective norms** | "My colleagues don't take this seriously" | Does the learner's social environment support engagement? | Build positive group culture; connect training to employer expectations |
| **Perceived control** | "I can't do this — it's too hard" | Does the learner believe they can succeed? | Scaffold into smaller steps; celebrate early wins; use peer learning |

> **Critical thinking:** Ajzen (2020) developed the TPB in health behaviour research (predicting exercise, smoking cessation). Is it valid to apply it to learning behaviour in TVET? What assumptions does the transfer require? What might the model miss about the TVET context — for example, the role of financial pressure, family obligations, or institutional power dynamics?

### Task 5 — Motivation Diagnosis (30 min)

For each profile: (a) identify which TPB factor is most likely low, (b) explain your reasoning with reference to Ajzen (2020), (c) describe one specific, concrete facilitation strategy.

**Profile 1 — Amina**
A 35-year-old experienced sales supervisor attending mandatory training on *Apply Business Communication*. She participates minimally, checks her phone frequently, and when asked to contribute gives brief dismissive answers. She says: "I've been doing this job for 10 years. I know how to communicate."

- Which TPB factor is low? What evidence supports your diagnosis?
- What facilitation strategy would you use? Be specific — not "make content relevant" but *how* you would do that in this session, with this learner.
- How does Kolb's (2021) concept of learning styles inform your approach to Amina?

**Profile 2 — Brian**
A 19-year-old recent school leaver in his first TVET programme. Eager and attentive during input but freezes when asked to speak in front of the group or attempt a practical task. He whispers: "I always get things wrong. Everyone will laugh at me."

- Which TPB factor is low?
- What does Kolb (2021) tell us about how Brian's learning style might be contributing to his anxiety?
- How do you build perceived control without lowering the standard? Reference Silberman & Biech (2015) on scaffolding experiential activities.

**Profile 3 — Cynthia**
Attends all sessions, polite and cooperative. Does not complete assignments. Her supervisor told her the training "doesn't count for anything at work" and she should focus on her actual job tasks during training hours.

- Which TPB factor is low?
- This is a systemic problem, not just individual. What does TVETA (2020) say about the role of employers in CBET? What should the institution do?
- What can *you* as the trainer do within your facilitation, even without employer cooperation?

**Group debrief (10 min):** Do the three TPB factors always operate independently, or do they interact? Describe a learner where all three factors are simultaneously low. What would that look like? What would you do first?
